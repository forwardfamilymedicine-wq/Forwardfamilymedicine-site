# How to Read Food Labels: A Practical Guide to Making Better Choices

**Quick answer:** The most important part of a food label is the ingredient list, not the nutrition facts panel. A short ingredient list of recognizable whole-food ingredients is the most reliable indicator of a nutritious product — regardless of front-of-package marketing claims.

The modern supermarket is an environment carefully engineered to influence purchasing decisions. Food labels are part of that environment. Some information on them is genuinely useful. Some is tightly regulated and meaningful. And some — particularly the front-of-package marketing — is designed to create an impression of healthfulness regardless of what's actually in the product.

Learning to navigate food labels is a skill. Once you know what to look for, and what to ignore, grocery shopping becomes considerably less confusing.

---

## Why Food Labeling Is Confusing

The United States Food and Drug Administration regulates what food companies can claim on labels, but the regulatory system has meaningful gaps that leave room for misleading presentation.

There are two main types of claims. **Health claims** — statements that link a food or ingredient to a reduced risk of a disease — require FDA review and approval based on scientific evidence. These are tightly regulated. **Structure/function claims** — statements about how a nutrient "supports" a bodily function, like "calcium supports bone health" — require only notification to the FDA and can be made without the same level of evidence.

The front of the package is where marketing has the most latitude. Terms like "natural," "made with real fruit," "artisan," "clean," and many others have no precise regulatory definition. A company can call its product "natural" while it contains dozens of chemical additives. As food journalist and researcher Marion Nestle has documented extensively, front-of-package claims are primarily marketing tools, not reliable nutritional guidance.

The antidote to this confusion is knowing which parts of the label to prioritize — and what each part actually tells you.

---

## Part 1: The Ingredient List — The Most Important Section

The ingredient list is regulated and honest in a way that front-of-package claims are not. It tells you exactly what is in the product, listed in order from highest to lowest weight. The heaviest ingredient comes first; the lightest comes last.

**What you want to see:** A short list of ingredients you recognize and could plausibly find in a kitchen. Whole grain oats, olive oil, almonds, dried cranberries, sea salt. Five ingredients or fewer is a useful rough threshold, though not an absolute rule.

**What to look for as a warning sign:**

*Added sugars under multiple names.* Food companies sometimes list sugar in several forms to push each one lower on the ingredient list, making the product appear less sweet than it is. Common aliases include high-fructose corn syrup, cane sugar, brown rice syrup, agave nectar, maltose, dextrose, fructose, and corn syrup solids. If you see several of these in the ingredient list, the total added sugar load is likely high.

*Ultra-processed indicators.* The NOVA classification system, developed by Monteiro and colleagues at the University of São Paulo, identifies ultra-processed foods partly by the presence of ingredients that you would not find in your kitchen and that exist solely to serve a technical or sensory function: emulsifiers like carrageenan, polysorbate 80, and lecithin; stabilizers; artificial flavors; artificial colors (Red 40, Yellow 5); hydrogenated or interesterified fats; and textured or modified proteins.

These ingredients are not inherently toxic at the levels present in individual servings, but they are markers of a product that has been extensively manufactured rather than minimally processed. Research by Monteiro's group and others links high ultra-processed food intake to worse health outcomes, likely through multiple mechanisms including gut microbiome disruption.

*Hydrogenated and interesterified fats.* Partially hydrogenated oils contain trans fats; interesterified fats are a newer substitute that may have similar metabolic effects. Both are red flags.

**The practical rule:** If the ingredient list has more than five to seven ingredients and contains multiple items you cannot picture as a real food, put it back.

## Part 2: The Nutrition Facts Panel

The Nutrition Facts panel was updated by the FDA in 2020 with several improvements. Here's how to read its most important elements:

**Serving size.** This is the most important number on the panel to check first, because everything else is expressed per serving. Serving sizes are set by the FDA to reflect amounts people actually eat — but a bag of chips might still list a serving as 1 ounce when most people eat considerably more in a sitting. A snack listed as 150 calories can easily become 450 calories if the bag contains three servings. Always check the servings per container.

**Calories.** Calorie counts are useful but not the most important number for food quality assessment. A 200-calorie serving of lentils and a 200-calorie serving of potato chips are profoundly different foods despite identical calorie counts. Use calories as context, not as the primary metric.

**Added sugars.** The 2020 label update required food companies to list added sugars separately from naturally occurring sugars — a significant improvement. Added sugars are sugars and syrups added during processing; naturally occurring sugars are those in fruit (fructose) and milk (lactose). The American Heart Association recommends a maximum of 36 grams (9 teaspoons) of added sugar per day for men and 25 grams (6 teaspoons) per day for women (Johnson et al., AHA Scientific Statement, Circulation 2009). A single sweetened yogurt or flavored oatmeal packet can contain 15–20 grams of added sugar — roughly half the daily limit for women. Checking this line is particularly worthwhile for breakfast foods, flavored dairy products, condiments, and sauces, where added sugar often hides.

**Sodium.** The recommended daily limit for most adults is 2,300 mg. Sodium is often assumed to live primarily in obviously salty foods, but the major dietary sources are bread, deli meats and cured meats, condiments, soups, and restaurant food — many of which do not taste particularly salty. A single serving of canned soup can contain 800–1,000 mg of sodium — nearly half the daily limit. Check the sodium content of packaged foods even when they do not taste salty.

**Dietary fiber.** For any product making a whole-grain claim, look for at least 3 grams of fiber per serving. Less than that suggests the whole grain contribution is minimal. Fiber content is one of the few nutritional numbers where more is reliably better within normal dietary ranges.

**Saturated fat.** The relationship between dietary saturated fat and health is more nuanced than it was presented 30 years ago — the food matrix and overall dietary pattern matter considerably. A single food's saturated fat content is less important than the total dietary pattern. That said, consistently checking this number can help you identify heavily processed products that use large amounts of palm oil or coconut oil.

## Part 3: Front-of-Package Claims — What They Mean (and Don't)

**"Natural."** This term has no regulatory definition for most foods in the United States. The FDA solicited public comment on a definition for "natural" in 2015 but ultimately declined to establish a formal regulatory definition. As of 2024, no standard exists and none is expected in the near term. "Natural" remains an unregulated marketing term on most food packaging — treat it accordingly. A product containing artificial colors, additives, and refined ingredients can legally be marketed as "natural."

**"Multigrain."** This means the product contains multiple types of grain. It says nothing about whether those grains are whole or refined. White bread can be multigrain. Always look for "whole grain" as the first ingredient, or check the fiber content.

**"Whole grain."** This is a more meaningful term, but it requires scrutiny. A product can technically be labeled "whole grain" if any portion of the grains used are whole, even if the majority are refined. The most reliable signal is the ingredient list: look for "whole wheat flour," "whole oats," or "whole grain [grain name]" as the first flour-type ingredient.

**"Low-fat" and "reduced-fat."** When fat is removed from a food, something must replace it to maintain palatability — and that replacement is often added sugar or refined starch. Low-fat flavored yogurt, low-fat salad dressings, and low-fat peanut butter frequently have significantly more added sugar than their full-fat counterparts. Check both the fat content and the added sugars before concluding "low-fat" means healthier.

**"Reduced sodium."** This means the product contains at least 25% less sodium than the original version. A product with 1,200 mg of sodium per serving reduced by 25% still contains 900 mg — a substantial amount. "Reduced" is relative, not absolute.

**FDA-regulated health claims.** Claims like "may reduce the risk of heart disease" on oat products or "may reduce the risk of certain cancers" on some plant-based foods are FDA-reviewed and require supporting evidence. These carry more weight than structure/function claims or unregulated marketing language.

## Practical Supermarket Strategy

A few habits that make label-reading less exhausting in practice:

**Shop the perimeter first.** The outer aisles of most grocery stores contain produce, meat, dairy, and fish — foods that generally require no label reading because they are whole ingredients without packaging. Building your cart primarily from the perimeter means most of what you buy needs minimal scrutiny.

**Apply more scrutiny in the center aisles.** Packaged and processed foods live in the center of the store. This is where label-reading matters most.

**Use a shopping list made in advance.** Pre-committing to a list based on recipes you've already planned reduces in-store decision fatigue and exposure to impulsive purchases of marketing-heavy products.

**Find your reliable staples.** Once you've read the labels on the canned beans, the olive oil, the yogurt, and the whole-grain bread you like, you don't need to re-read them every trip. The time investment is front-loaded.

## The Simplest Heuristic: Can You Recognize Every Ingredient?

If you want a single question to apply to any packaged food, this is it: Could you walk into a kitchen and find every ingredient on this label?

Whole wheat flour, oats, olive oil, almonds, eggs, honey, sea salt — yes. Disodium phosphate, polysorbate 60, maltodextrin, artificial flavor, carrageenan, modified corn starch — no.

This heuristic is not perfect. Some additives with unfamiliar names are benign (citric acid is just vitamin C's structural cousin; ascorbic acid is vitamin C). But it is a fast, practical filter that catches the vast majority of heavily processed products reliably.

## FAQ

**Is organic food healthier?**

"Organic" is a USDA-regulated term that refers to how food was produced — without synthetic pesticides, synthetic fertilizers, genetically modified organisms, and with specific standards for animal welfare. It does not directly measure nutritional quality. An organic cookie is still a cookie. The evidence that organic produce is significantly more nutritious than conventionally grown produce is limited. The more meaningful purchase decision is usually whole food vs. processed food, not organic vs. conventional. If pesticide exposure is your primary concern, the Environmental Working Group's "Dirty Dozen" and "Clean Fifteen" lists can help prioritize which items are worth buying organic.

**How do I identify ultra-processed food quickly?**

Look at the ingredient list. If it contains ingredients you wouldn't find in a home kitchen — emulsifiers, stabilizers, artificial flavors, artificial colors, modified starches, hydrogenated oils — it is likely ultra-processed in the NOVA sense. A useful shortcut: if you cannot picture the ingredient as a whole food or as something you'd use in cooking, it's an additive. A five-second scan of the ingredient list is usually sufficient to categorize a product.

**Are nutrition facts always accurate?**

FDA regulations allow a 20% margin of error in nutrition label values. In practice, calorie and macronutrient counts can be slightly off. This is rarely clinically meaningful for individual foods but is worth knowing. The ingredient list, which is not subject to the same margin of error (it is either in the product or it isn't), is the more reliable section.

**Does the order of ingredients really matter?**

Yes. Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. If sugar is the first or second ingredient, the product is primarily sugar by weight. If whole grain flour is listed fourth after several refined ingredients and additives, the whole grain content is minimal even if the front of the package highlights it. The position of an ingredient in the list is one of the most information-dense data points on any label.
