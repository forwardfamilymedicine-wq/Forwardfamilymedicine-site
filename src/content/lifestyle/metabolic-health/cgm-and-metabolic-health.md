# CGM and Metabolic Health: How Continuous Glucose Monitoring Helps

**Quick answer:** A CGM (continuous glucose monitor) is a small sensor worn on the arm or abdomen that tracks blood glucose every few minutes throughout the day and night. Originally developed for diabetes management, CGMs are increasingly used by people without diabetes to understand how their body responds to food, exercise, sleep, and stress — and to make more targeted lifestyle changes as a result.

---

## What Is a CGM?

A continuous glucose monitor is a wearable biosensor that measures glucose in the interstitial fluid just beneath the skin. It consists of a small filament inserted just under the skin surface — the insertion is quick and most people find it painless or nearly so — paired with a transmitter that sends readings wirelessly to a smartphone app or dedicated reader every one to five minutes.

Unlike a traditional fingerstick blood glucose test, which gives you a single snapshot in time, a CGM produces a continuous stream of data: hundreds of readings per day, across all meals, all activities, and all hours of the night.

Several CGM options are now available in the United States:

**Dexcom G7.** The most established brand, traditionally used by people with type 1 and type 2 diabetes. Requires a prescription for insurance coverage, though some practices prescribe it off-label for metabolic assessment.

**Abbott FreeStyle Libre 2 and 3.** Widely used globally, now available in multiple generations. The Libre 3 provides real-time readings every minute and has received FDA approval for a range of use cases.

**Stelo (Dexcom).** As of 2024, Dexcom launched the Stelo — the first CGM specifically designed and FDA-cleared for non-diabetic adults, available over-the-counter without a prescription. Abbott has also received OTC clearance for the FreeStyle Libre. This represents a meaningful shift: CGM is no longer exclusively a diabetes tool.

Most sensors are worn for 10–15 days before replacement and are water-resistant for showering and exercise.

## Why Blood Sugar Variability Matters

A traditional HbA1c tells you your average blood sugar over the past three months. It is a useful population-level screening tool. But it misses something important: variability.

Two people can have the same HbA1c — say, 5.5% — while having very different glucose patterns. One might have stable, flat glucose all day. The other might spike to 180 mg/dL after breakfast, crash to 70 mg/dL mid-morning, spike again after lunch, and have a normal reading at the time the fasting blood draw was taken. The average looks the same. The metabolic experience — and the physiological stress — is completely different.

Research published in *PLOS Biology* (Hall et al., 2018) identified what the authors called "glucotypes": patterns of glucose variability in individuals without diabetes that varied dramatically from person to person and were not captured by HbA1c. Even among apparently healthy individuals, some showed significant postprandial glucose dysregulation — spikes that exceeded levels typically associated with prediabetes — in response to certain foods.

Why does variability matter clinically? Large postprandial glucose spikes:

- Drive compensatory insulin surges that, over time, promote insulin resistance
- Are associated with increased oxidative stress and inflammation
- Correlate with increased cardiovascular risk independent of average glucose
- Produce the energy fluctuations — the post-meal energy crash, mid-morning hunger — that many people experience but don't connect to blood sugar

**Time in range** is the metric CGM makes possible that average glucose cannot provide. Defined as the percentage of time glucose stays within a target range (typically 70–140 mg/dL for non-diabetics), time in range gives a more complete picture of metabolic function than any single-point measurement.

## What CGM Reveals That A1c Doesn't

The most practically useful insight CGM provides is highly individual. Here is what most users find illuminating:

**Individual food responses.** Research has consistently shown that people's glucose responses to the same food can vary dramatically based on their gut microbiome, insulin sensitivity, physical activity level, and other factors. A landmark study from the Weizmann Institute by Zeevi and colleagues (Cell, 2015), which monitored continuous glucose responses in over 800 individuals, found that glycemic responses to identical foods varied dramatically between people — driven by individual differences in gut microbiome composition, insulin sensitivity, and other factors. Some participants spiked strongly in response to foods considered "healthy" (sushi rice, whole-grain bread) while tolerating foods considered less healthy with minimal glucose elevation — and vice versa. CGM makes this individual variation visible.

**Meal timing and composition effects.** The order in which you eat foods within a meal significantly affects your postprandial glucose response. Research by Shukla and colleagues (Diabetes Care, 2018) found that eating vegetables and protein before carbohydrates in a meal reduced postprandial glucose peaks by approximately 28–37% compared to eating carbohydrates first — a simple strategy that CGM makes visible in real time.

**Nocturnal glucose patterns.** Some people who appear metabolically normal during the day have elevated overnight glucose, elevated dawn phenomenon (early-morning glucose rise driven by cortisol and growth hormone), or glucose instability during sleep that is completely invisible on standard testing. CGM catches this.

**Exercise effects.** Aerobic exercise typically lowers glucose in real time; resistance training can cause a transient spike followed by a prolonged lowering effect. Seeing these patterns helps people optimize the timing of meals relative to exercise.

## What You Learn from CGM Data

Wearing a CGM for two to four weeks tends to produce a few common revelations:

**Which specific foods spike your glucose significantly.** For many people, it's not the foods they expected. White rice, fruit juice, and sports drinks are common surprises. So is the difference between a bowl of oatmeal eaten alone versus with nuts and eggs.

**How a post-meal walk changes everything.** Research by DiPietro and colleagues (Diabetes Care, 2013) found that short 15-minute walks after each meal were more effective at lowering 24-hour glucose levels in older adults at risk for diabetes than a single 45-minute morning walk — with postprandial glucose reductions of approximately 22% after evening meals. Many people see blunting of 30–50% on their own CGM traces, making this one of the most motivating lifestyle changes to observe in real time.

**How stress raises glucose.** Many CGM users are surprised to see their glucose rise during stressful meetings, difficult conversations, or anxious moments — without eating anything. The cortisol response to psychological stress mobilizes glucose from the liver, and CGM makes this visible in a way that makes the mechanism feel real and personal.

**How sleep affects morning glucose.** Poor sleep consistently produces elevated fasting glucose the following morning for many people. Seeing this connection on a CGM trace — particularly the relationship between sleep quality and next-day glucose patterns — is often a powerful motivator for sleep behavior change.

**Patterns across time.** Over two to four weeks, a picture of your metabolic baseline emerges: your typical fasting glucose, your time in range, your worst-spiking meals, and your best days.

## Is CGM Useful for Non-Diabetics?

This is an honest question worth answering honestly.

CGM provides genuine value for people with prediabetes, metabolic syndrome, or known insulin resistance — where glucose patterns are likely to be dysregulated and where visible data can motivate and guide meaningful behavior change. The feedback loop is tight: you eat, you see the result, you adjust.

CGM is also valuable for people who are actively working to improve metabolic health and want to understand which specific dietary or lifestyle changes are producing the most impact. Rather than waiting three months for an HbA1c, you can see effects within days.

For people who are metabolically healthy and simply curious, CGM can still be a useful educational experience — but the clinical utility is lower, and the expense may not be justified long-term.

Where the value is lower: as a permanent or ongoing monitoring tool for people who are metabolically well and eating a stable, already-optimized diet. CGM is most powerful as a diagnostic and behavior-change tool during a period of active metabolic assessment or intervention.

The honest bottom line: CGM for non-diabetics is a useful tool, not a necessary one. For motivated individuals working on metabolic health, a two-to-four-week CGM trial is likely to produce actionable insights. As a permanent lifestyle accessory, the cost-benefit is more individual.

## Limitations of CGM

CGM is a genuinely useful tool, but it's worth understanding what it doesn't do.

**Accuracy.** CGMs measure interstitial glucose, not blood glucose. There is typically a 5–15 minute lag between blood glucose changes and what the sensor detects. Readings can be affected by sensor placement, pressure on the sensor (sleeping on it), certain medications, and hydration status. Readings at the extreme low end are less accurate and should always be confirmed with a fingerstick if symptoms are present.

**Cost.** OTC CGMs currently cost roughly $50–$100 per sensor (10–15 days of use). This is not covered by most insurance for non-diabetics. Over weeks or months of use, cost becomes a real consideration.

**Interpretation.** The data is only as useful as your ability to interpret it. Raw glucose traces can be confusing, and without context — what you ate, when you exercised, how you slept — the numbers may raise anxiety more than generate insight. Ideally, CGM use is paired with a knowledgeable provider who can help contextualize the data.

**Psychological burden.** For some people, constant glucose monitoring creates anxiety about every meal and every spike. A glucose reading of 140 mg/dL after a meal is not a crisis for a non-diabetic — it is a normal postprandial response in many contexts. Over-interpreting normal variation can produce unnecessary dietary restriction and food anxiety. This is a real risk, especially for individuals with any history of disordered eating.

**It measures glucose, not insulin.** CGM tells you about glucose patterns but doesn't directly measure insulin. A person can have significant insulin resistance with apparently normal CGM patterns if their pancreas is compensating effectively. CGM is one window into metabolic health, not the whole picture.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need a prescription for a CGM?**

A: Not necessarily. As of 2024, both the Dexcom Stelo and Abbott FreeStyle Libre OTC have received FDA clearance for over-the-counter use in adults without diabetes. These are available directly from pharmacies and online retailers without a prescription. Prescription-grade CGMs (Dexcom G7, FreeStyle Libre 2 and 3 in their higher-capability configurations) still require a prescription and are covered by insurance primarily for people with diabetes.

**Q: What is a normal glucose range on a CGM?**

A: For non-diabetics, fasting glucose typically runs between 70–90 mg/dL, and postprandial (after-meal) glucose typically peaks within 30–60 minutes of eating and returns to near-fasting levels within two hours. Values up to 140 mg/dL after a meal are generally considered within the normal range for a healthy person. Values consistently above 140 mg/dL postprandially, or fasting values consistently above 100 mg/dL, warrant clinical attention. The most meaningful metric is time in range: ideally, most readings (roughly 90% or more) should fall between 70–140 mg/dL.

**Q: Can CGM help me lose weight?**

A: Indirectly, yes. CGM doesn't directly cause weight loss, but it provides feedback that can support dietary changes that lead to weight loss. Seeing which foods spike your glucose, understanding the meal composition and timing strategies that keep glucose stable, and building behavioral habits around glucose management can all contribute to improved diet quality — which typically supports weight loss for people who need it. The behavior change mechanism, not the measurement itself, is what drives results.

**Q: How is CGM different from checking blood sugar with a fingerstick?**

A: A fingerstick gives you a single data point — one glucose reading at one moment in time. It tells you where glucose is right now but nothing about the trend (is it rising, falling, or stable?), how it got there, or where it's going. A CGM provides continuous trending data — you can see that your glucose is currently 115 mg/dL and rising rapidly after a meal, versus stable at 95 mg/dL after the same meal on a different day following a different food choice. The pattern data is where CGM's value lies.
