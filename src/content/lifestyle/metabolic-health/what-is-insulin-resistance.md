# What Is Insulin Resistance? Causes, Signs, and What to Do

**Quick answer:** Insulin resistance is a condition in which your cells don't respond normally to the hormone insulin, causing your pancreas to produce more insulin to compensate — eventually leading to elevated blood sugar, prediabetes, and type 2 diabetes if left unaddressed. It typically develops silently over years and is present in tens of millions of Americans who have no idea.

---

## How Insulin Works

To understand insulin resistance, it helps to start with what insulin is supposed to do.

Insulin is a hormone produced by beta cells in the pancreas. Its primary job is to act as a key: when you eat, glucose from food enters your bloodstream, blood sugar rises, and the pancreas releases insulin. Insulin binds to receptors on cells — particularly in muscle, liver, and fat tissue — and signals them to open their doors and absorb that glucose.

Muscle cells use glucose for energy. Liver cells can store it as glycogen or convert it into fat. Fat cells store it for later. Once glucose is cleared from the bloodstream, blood sugar returns to a normal fasting level, and insulin falls.

This system, when it works well, is elegant: blood sugar rises modestly after meals and falls back within one to two hours. Insulin levels track closely with blood sugar. The pancreas does its job, cells respond efficiently, and the process repeats thousands of times over a lifetime without issue.

---

## What Happens When Cells Resist Insulin

Insulin resistance is what happens when the lock stops responding to the key.

When cells become resistant to insulin's signal, glucose has difficulty entering them. Blood sugar stays elevated longer than it should. The pancreas detects this and does the only thing it can: produce more insulin, trying to force the same result.

For a period — which can last years — this compensation works. Blood sugar stays in the normal range because the pancreas is working hard to keep it there. This is why fasting glucose is often the last marker to go abnormal: the body is compensating behind the scenes.

What's happening during this compensated phase is that fasting insulin levels are creeping upward — a sign of the pancreas working overtime. Triglycerides begin to rise. HDL cholesterol tends to fall. Waist circumference expands as the liver increasingly converts excess glucose into fat. Blood pressure may begin to edge upward.

Eventually, if nothing changes, the pancreas can no longer fully compensate. Fasting glucose begins to climb into the prediabetes range (100–125 mg/dL). HbA1c rises toward 5.7–6.4%. If still unaddressed, full type 2 diabetes follows.

The critical insight is that this progression is not inevitable. At every stage — including the early compensated phase — lifestyle interventions can reverse course.

## Causes and Risk Factors

Insulin resistance is not simply the result of eating sugar or being overweight, though those factors contribute. It develops through several overlapping biological mechanisms, most of which are tied to lifestyle:

**Visceral fat accumulation.** Fat stored around the abdominal organs — sometimes called belly fat or intra-abdominal fat — is metabolically active in ways that subcutaneous fat (under the skin) is not. Visceral fat releases inflammatory molecules called cytokines and free fatty acids that directly interfere with insulin signaling in muscle and liver cells. This is why waist circumference is a more reliable metabolic risk indicator than total body weight or BMI.

**Physical inactivity.** Skeletal muscle is the primary site of insulin-stimulated glucose disposal in the body. Active muscle is highly insulin-sensitive because it needs glucose for energy. Sedentary muscle doesn't need that glucose as urgently, so it becomes less responsive over time. Even short periods of immobility — a few days of bed rest in otherwise healthy people — produce measurable insulin resistance.

**Chronically poor diet.** Repeated rapid spikes in blood sugar from refined carbohydrates and ultra-processed foods keep insulin levels chronically elevated. Over time, cells exposed to persistently high insulin downregulate their insulin receptors as a protective response — a classic feedback loop that worsens the very problem it's compensating for.

**Sleep deprivation.** Consistently sleeping fewer than seven hours per night — the minimum recommended by the American Academy of Sleep Medicine for adult health — is an independent risk factor for insulin resistance. Sleep deprivation raises cortisol, reduces growth hormone (which supports insulin sensitivity), and impairs glucose metabolism through pathways that are still being fully characterized.

**Chronic psychological stress.** The stress hormone cortisol mobilizes glucose from the liver to fuel "fight or flight" responses. Chronically elevated cortisol keeps blood sugar and insulin activity dysregulated, and promotes visceral fat accumulation — feeding the cycle described above.

**Genetic predisposition.** Some individuals develop insulin resistance more readily at a given level of adiposity or activity than others. People of South Asian, East Asian, Hispanic, and some other ethnic backgrounds tend to develop insulin resistance at lower body weights than white populations. Family history of type 2 diabetes is a meaningful risk factor. Genetic predisposition does not make lifestyle intervention less effective — it makes it more important.

## Warning Signs of Insulin Resistance

Because insulin resistance is often metabolically compensated for years, it typically causes no dramatic symptoms. But there are signs worth knowing:

**Acanthosis nigricans.** Dark, velvety patches of skin that appear in skin folds — particularly the back of the neck, armpits, and groin — are a visible sign of elevated insulin levels and a well-recognized clinical marker of insulin resistance.

**Central weight gain.** Expanding waist circumference, even without dramatic changes in total body weight, often reflects visceral fat accumulation driven by insulin resistance.

**Fatigue after meals.** Feeling unusually tired or foggy after eating — especially after carbohydrate-heavy meals — can reflect impaired glucose metabolism.

**Persistent hunger despite eating.** Insulin resistance can impair the brain's satiety signaling, leading to hunger that doesn't resolve normally after meals.

**Frequent urination and increased thirst.** These symptoms are more associated with overt diabetes than early insulin resistance, but they can appear as blood sugar begins to rise.

**Elevated triglycerides and low HDL on a blood test.** A triglyceride level above 150 mg/dL paired with low HDL is one of the most reliable lab-based indicators of insulin resistance — often visible years before fasting glucose becomes abnormal.

**Skin tags.** Multiple skin tags, particularly around the neck and armpits, are associated with elevated insulin levels, though they are not a definitive diagnostic marker.

It's worth emphasizing: many people with significant insulin resistance have none of these symptoms and feel entirely well. The condition announces itself through lab work, not through obvious illness — which is why routine metabolic screening matters.

## How It's Diagnosed

Standard lab work doesn't always capture insulin resistance early. Here's what the most useful tests actually measure:

**Fasting glucose.** A fasting blood glucose of 100 mg/dL or above suggests impaired fasting glucose (prediabetes range) and indirectly indicates insulin resistance. Normal fasting glucose does not rule out insulin resistance.

**HbA1c.** Reflects average blood sugar over the past three months. Values of 5.7–6.4% indicate prediabetes. Normal HbA1c can coexist with early insulin resistance.

**Fasting insulin.** This is the most direct and sensitive early test. An optimal fasting insulin is generally considered to be below 7 uIU/mL; values of 7–10 uIU/mL are borderline; values above 15–20 uIU/mL strongly suggest insulin resistance even with normal fasting glucose. Reference ranges vary slightly between labs, so interpret results in the context of your full metabolic picture. Fasting insulin is not routinely ordered by most providers — you may need to ask specifically.

**HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance).** Calculated from fasting glucose and fasting insulin values. A HOMA-IR above 2.5–3.0 is generally considered consistent with insulin resistance. Many lab ordering platforms allow this to be requested directly.

**Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio.** A simple ratio calculated from a standard lipid panel. A ratio above 3.0 (using mg/dL) correlates well with insulin resistance in large population studies and can serve as an indirect indicator when fasting insulin isn't available.

If you're concerned about your metabolic health, bringing these specific questions to your next primary care appointment is a reasonable starting point. At Forward Family Medicine, we routinely include fasting insulin and HOMA-IR in our metabolic assessments.

## The Progression: Insulin Resistance → Prediabetes → Type 2 Diabetes

It helps to understand this as a spectrum rather than a series of distinct conditions.

Insulin resistance is the earliest phase — present for years, often while all standard labs appear normal. The compensated pancreas keeps blood sugar normal at the cost of chronically elevated insulin.

Prediabetes is the phase when compensation begins to fail. Fasting glucose and HbA1c are elevated above normal but below the diabetic threshold. This is still a highly reversible stage.

Type 2 diabetes is diagnosed when fasting glucose reaches 126 mg/dL or above on two separate tests, HbA1c reaches 6.5% or above, or a two-hour oral glucose tolerance test result of 200 mg/dL or higher. Even at this stage, meaningful reversal is possible with intensive intervention — but it requires more effort and, in many cases, medication support alongside lifestyle change.

The key implication: earlier intervention produces better and easier results. Addressing insulin resistance before it produces prediabetes is far simpler than reversing established type 2 diabetes.

## How to Reverse Insulin Resistance

Insulin resistance is not a fixed biological state. The same lifestyle factors that cause it can be reversed to fix it. The four most evidence-supported interventions are:

**Diet.** Reducing refined carbohydrates and ultra-processed foods decreases the frequency and magnitude of blood sugar spikes, allowing insulin levels to normalize. Increasing fiber and choosing whole foods over processed ones are the most consistently effective dietary changes. For detailed strategies, see [Diet Strategies for Blood Sugar Control](/lifestyle/metabolic-health/diet-strategies-blood-sugar).

**Physical activity.** Both aerobic exercise and resistance training improve insulin sensitivity through different mechanisms. Aerobic exercise enables insulin-independent glucose uptake into muscle during activity; resistance training builds muscle mass, expanding the body's total glucose disposal capacity. Consistent physical activity is one of the most potent insulin sensitizers available. See our guide at [Physical Activity](/lifestyle/physical-activity).

**Weight loss.** Even modest weight loss — 5–7% of body weight — produces disproportionately large improvements in insulin sensitivity. This appears to be driven primarily by reduction in visceral fat, which means that changes in waist circumference may be a more meaningful progress marker than changes on the scale.

**Sleep and stress management.** Normalizing sleep to 7–9 hours per night and reducing chronic psychological stress lower cortisol burden, which directly improves insulin sensitivity. These are underappreciated interventions that many people overlook in favor of dietary changes alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can you have insulin resistance without being overweight?**

A: Yes. While excess weight — particularly visceral fat — is a common driver of insulin resistance, it is not the only one. Sedentary behavior, chronic sleep deprivation, stress, and genetic predisposition can all produce insulin resistance in people who are at a normal or near-normal body weight. This is sometimes called TOFI (thin outside, fat inside), referring to individuals who carry excess visceral fat despite a normal BMI. Fasting insulin and waist circumference are more reliable metabolic risk indicators than BMI alone.

**Q: What does insulin resistance feel like?**

A: For most people in the early stages, it doesn't feel like anything distinct. This is part of what makes it clinically challenging. Some people notice fatigue after meals, persistent hunger, or gradual weight gain around the abdomen. Others have no symptoms at all and are surprised to learn from lab work that their metabolic health is compromised. The absence of symptoms is not reassurance.

**Q: How long does it take to reverse insulin resistance?**

A: Meaningful improvements in insulin sensitivity can begin within days to weeks of consistent dietary change and physical activity. Research using glucose tolerance tests has demonstrated measurable improvements in insulin sensitivity within two to four weeks of consistent lifestyle intervention. Full normalization of fasting insulin and related markers typically takes three to six months of sustained change, depending on the degree of resistance present at the start.

**Q: Does insulin resistance mean I will definitely get diabetes?**

A: No. Insulin resistance increases the risk of progression to prediabetes and type 2 diabetes, but it is not deterministic. The Diabetes Prevention Program showed that people with prediabetes — a more advanced state than early insulin resistance — could reduce their risk of progressing to diabetes by 58% with modest lifestyle changes. Earlier intervention, at the insulin resistance stage, likely produces even better outcomes. The trajectory is very much within your influence.
