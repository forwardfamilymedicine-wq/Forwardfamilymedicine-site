## Quick Answer
Concierge and Direct Primary Care practices are built around the needs busy professionals actually have: same-day appointments, direct physician access, telehealth that works, and a doctor who knows your history — without the scheduling friction of a conventional practice.

## Explanation

Be honest: when did you last have a primary care appointment that didn't require rearranging your calendar, sitting in a waiting room for 40 minutes past your scheduled time, and then getting 12 minutes with a physician who seemed to be reading your chart for the first time?

For most busy professionals, the answer is "not recently" — and often the follow-up is "so I just don't go."

That's a problem. Skipping primary care doesn't make health issues disappear; it makes them more expensive, more complex, and harder to treat when they finally demand attention. The patients who end up in the emergency room at 11 PM are often the ones who kept putting off a 20-minute conversation with their doctor that would have caught something six months earlier.

The traditional primary care model simply doesn't work well for high-functioning people with demanding schedules. It was designed for a world where a 9 AM appointment meant you'd be seen at 9 AM, where the physician had time to think about your case, and where there was an actual ongoing relationship between patient and doctor. That world largely no longer exists in conventional practice.

**What Busy Professionals Actually Need from Primary Care**

Talk to any executive, entrepreneur, or working professional who values their health, and the wish list tends to be consistent:

*Predictable scheduling.* Not three weeks out, not two-hour blocks blocked off for a 20-minute visit, and not a 9 AM appointment that doesn't start until 9:48 AM. Same-day or next-day access when something comes up, and the ability to book quickly when you actually have a window.

*Telehealth that isn't a workaround.* Standalone telehealth services — the kind offered through insurance apps or retail health platforms — involve seeing a different provider every time, no continuity of record, and limited ability to manage anything beyond the most straightforward issues. What professionals actually need is telehealth with their own physician: someone who knows them, has their history, and can have a real conversation.

*Direct messaging.* Being able to send your doctor a message about a lab result or a medication question and receive a thoughtful answer the same day — not a phone tree, not a nurse relay system, not a callback in three business days — is genuinely valuable. It turns minor questions into minor questions instead of unresolved anxieties.

*A physician who knows them.* When your doctor knows that you travel internationally for work, that you've had elevated blood pressure under stress, that you have a family history of early cardiac disease, and that you're the type of person who will ignore symptoms until forced to address them — that context informs every interaction. It's the difference between generic advice and care that actually fits your life.

*Longer visits when needed.* Annual physicals for high-performing adults should include time for a real conversation about risk factors, preventive strategies, lifestyle considerations, and health goals. A 20-minute appointment can't do that. A 1-to-2-hour appointment with a physician who's been thinking about your care can.

**Preventive and Executive Health**

One of the most significant benefits of concierge-style primary care for professionals is access to proactive, comprehensive preventive care. A physician who has time and who knows your history can work through advanced lipid panels, inflammatory markers, metabolic screening, blood pressure optimization, and lifestyle-based risk reduction in a way that a rushed conventional visit simply cannot accommodate.

For many professionals, the most valuable health investment is catching problems early — when they're manageable, reversible, or preventable — rather than treating them when they've compounded into something serious. That kind of proactive medicine requires a relationship, not a transaction.

At Forward Family Medicine, this proactive approach is deepened by integrated board-eligible Lifestyle Medicine with Dr. Lieberman — evidence-based guidance on nutrition, movement, sleep, and stress built directly into your care, not referred out. For professionals managing cardiovascular risk, metabolic health, or the physiological effects of chronic work stress, this is a meaningful clinical differentiator.

**Time Is the Real Luxury**

People talk about concierge medicine as a luxury, and in one sense that's accurate — it's better than the standard experience. But framed differently, what you're really buying is time: your physician's time (to think carefully about your care), and your own time (not spent waiting, rescheduling, navigating phone trees, or deferring care because the system makes it too difficult to access).

For professionals whose time genuinely has economic value, the calculus is often obvious once they do it.

## Practical Tips

- Block your annual physical on your calendar at the start of each year, the same way you'd schedule a business commitment — preventive care deferred indefinitely is care not received.
- Ask whether your prospective DPC practice offers telehealth as a standard part of the membership, not as an add-on, and clarify what types of visits can be handled virtually versus in person.
- If you travel frequently, ask about the practice's approach to caring for patients who are out of town — a good DPC physician can often manage a significant amount remotely.
- Direct messaging your physician (a feature of most DPC memberships) is valuable for minor questions that don't warrant an appointment — use it rather than letting things accumulate.
- Pair your DPC membership with a comprehensive annual physical that includes the labs you actually want: lipid panels, inflammatory markers, metabolic panels, and any screenings relevant to your family history.
- If your employer or business offers health reimbursement arrangement (HRA) funds, ask whether a DPC membership or associated costs can be covered.
- Give your physician full context about your work demands, stress levels, travel schedule, and lifestyle — this information directly affects the quality of care you receive.

## FAQ

**Q: What can I actually handle via telehealth or direct message versus in person?**
A: Telehealth and messaging work well for medication questions, minor illness triage, lab result review, follow-up on known conditions, mental health check-ins, and many prescription refills. In-person visits are better for new or complex symptoms, any physical examination finding, procedures, and anything requiring direct assessment. Your DPC physician will help you decide which is appropriate for a given situation.

**Q: I travel a lot — can a DPC practice actually serve me?**
A: Better than most conventional practices, in fact. Telehealth and direct messaging allow your physician to manage many issues regardless of where you are. For acute care needs while traveling, your DPC physician can often help triage whether something warrants an urgent care visit locally or can be managed remotely — and they'll have context that a stranger at a walk-in clinic won't.

**Q: How do I fit in an annual physical if my schedule is unpredictable?**
A: DPC practices generally offer more flexible scheduling than conventional practices. Because the physician isn't packing a waiting room full of patients, finding a time that works for you — including early morning or other accommodated times — is often possible. Ask during your initial conversation about scheduling flexibility.

**Q: What advanced preventive screenings should I be asking about?**
A: This depends on your age, sex, family history, and lifestyle factors. A comprehensive annual physical in a DPC setting typically includes a review of cardiovascular risk (including advanced lipid panels if indicated), metabolic markers, blood pressure assessment, relevant cancer screenings, and a conversation about lifestyle factors including sleep, stress, and exercise. Your physician can walk you through what's appropriate for your specific profile.
