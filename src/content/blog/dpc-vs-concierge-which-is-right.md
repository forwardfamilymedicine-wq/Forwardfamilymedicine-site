## Quick Answer
Both DPC and concierge medicine offer smaller patient panels, same-day access, and longer visits — but DPC is typically more affordable and cleaner in structure, while concierge medicine may offer additional executive health services and still bills insurance alongside its retainer fee.

## Explanation

If you've started exploring alternatives to the standard insurance-based primary care experience, you've likely encountered two terms: Direct Primary Care (DPC) and concierge medicine. Both represent meaningful improvements over traditional primary care. But they're not the same, and the difference matters when you're deciding what's right for your situation.

**Direct Primary Care: The Basics**

In a Direct Primary Care practice, patients pay a flat monthly membership fee directly to the physician's practice. The practice does not bill insurance for primary care services. That's the defining structural feature. No claims, no coding, no prior authorizations, no insurance overhead. The membership fee covers a defined scope of primary care services: unlimited visits, preventive care, chronic disease management, urgent care issues, and direct access to the physician by phone, text, or secure message.

DPC physicians maintain smaller patient panels — typically 300–600 patients rather than the 2,000+ common in insurance-based practices. Forward Family Medicine caps at approximately 250 patients per physician, which is below the DPC average and is the structural reason same-day access is consistently reliable. Smaller panel means more time per patient, same-day or next-day appointments, and a physician who actually knows you.

DPC practices do not cover specialist care, hospitalizations, surgery, advanced imaging, or emergency care. Patients still need health insurance for those services — typically a high-deductible plan paired with the DPC membership.

**Concierge Medicine: The Basics**

Concierge medicine also operates on a retainer or membership model, and also offers smaller panels, better access, and longer appointments. The key differences:

- **Insurance billing continues**: Many concierge practices still bill your insurance for services rendered, in addition to the membership fee. The retainer covers "enhanced access" — the direct line to the physician, longer visits, same-day scheduling. Insurance pays for the clinical services themselves.
- **Higher price point**: Because the infrastructure of insurance billing remains, concierge practices often have higher operational costs, which are reflected in higher retainer fees — commonly in the range of $150 to $500 or more per month.
- **Broader service scope**: Some concierge practices offer executive health add-ons: comprehensive annual health assessments, cardiovascular imaging, advanced blood panels, and wellness consultations that go beyond standard primary care. This is where the premium price is often justified.

Some concierge practices operate on a "pure" model without insurance billing — essentially DPC at a higher price point with more comprehensive services. Terminology varies, and it's worth asking any prospective practice precisely how they operate.

**The Practical Comparison**

| Feature | DPC | Concierge |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly cost | Lower | Higher |
| Insurance billing | No | Often yes |
| Panel size | typically 300–600; Forward Family Medicine caps at ~250 | 300–800 |
| Same-day access | Yes | Yes |
| Direct physician access | Yes | Yes |
| Executive health add-ons | Varies | Often included |
| Requires health insurance? | Yes (for non-primary care) | Usually yes |

**When DPC Is the Better Choice**

DPC is the cleaner, simpler, more affordable option for most patients. If you primarily want:
- Affordable access to excellent primary care
- A physician who has time for you
- Direct communication without phone trees or portal delays
- Freedom from surprise bills for primary care services

...DPC delivers all of this at a lower cost and with less complexity. The absence of insurance billing is a feature, not a limitation — it's what makes the model financially sustainable for the physician and affordable for the patient.

**When Concierge Medicine Might Make Sense**

Concierge medicine may be worth the higher price if you:
- Want comprehensive executive health assessments and advanced diagnostics as part of your annual care
- Have complex insurance relationships that benefit from continued insurance billing (particularly if you have a rich employer-sponsored plan covering a large share of your costs)
- Value specific service add-ons like travel medicine comprehensive programs, executive physicals, or coordinated specialist networks

The decision comes down to what you actually value and what you'll use. Paying for concierge services you don't need is less sensible than a DPC membership that covers what you actually use with room to spare.

## Practical Tips

- Ask any prospective DPC or concierge practice directly: "Do you bill my insurance for primary care services?" The answer tells you immediately which model you're looking at
- Compare total annual cost: DPC membership + HDHP premium vs. concierge retainer + richer plan premium — the DPC combination is often meaningfully less expensive overall
- Ask about panel size: a "concierge" practice with 1,500 patients may not deliver the access it promises
- Understand what's in scope: list the primary care services you actually use and confirm they're included in the membership
- Think about your relationship preferences — both models offer better access, but the physician-patient relationship depends more on the individual physician than the model
- If you're healthy and primarily want preventive care and same-day access for acute issues, DPC likely covers everything you need
- If you have complex medical needs requiring frequent specialist coordination, ask how each practice handles referral management

## FAQ

**Q: Can I use my HSA to pay for DPC or concierge membership fees?**
A: The Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026) clarified that DPC membership fees are HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying high-deductible health plan. If you are enrolled in an HDHP, you can now use HSA funds for your DPC membership. Confirm with your plan administrator for your specific account.

**Q: Do I still need health insurance if I join a DPC practice?**
A: Yes. DPC covers primary care services only. Health insurance remains necessary for hospitalizations, specialist care, surgeries, emergency services, advanced imaging, and other non-primary-care services. Most DPC patients pair their membership with a high-deductible health plan.

**Q: Is concierge medicine only for wealthy patients?**
A: Higher-end concierge practices have historically served a higher-income demographic, which is where the perception comes from. DPC emerged partly as a more accessible alternative to expensive concierge care. DPC practices serve a wide range of patients, including families and individuals for whom the membership fee is a meaningful but manageable expense.

**Q: What happens to my specialist care and insurance billing in a DPC practice?**
A: Specialist referrals, lab work done at outside facilities, imaging, and hospitalizations all go through your health insurance as they would in any practice. Your DPC physician manages the referral process, communicates with specialists, and follows up on results — but the specialist's services are billed through insurance, not through the DPC membership.
