## Quick Answer
Telehealth is genuinely useful for medication management, follow-up care, minor illness triage, and lab result review — but requires in-person evaluation for new complex symptoms, physical exam findings, and procedures. The key variable is whether you're seeing your own physician who knows your history, or a stranger.

## Explanation

The pandemic accelerated telehealth adoption in a matter of weeks, pushing virtual visits from a marginal feature to a mainstream expectation. And something genuinely good came out of that acceleration: patients discovered that many primary care needs can be addressed efficiently without driving to an office, sitting in a waiting room, and arranging half a day around a 15-minute appointment.

But telehealth also acquired a reputation problem. Standalone telehealth services — the kind offered through insurance apps, retail health platforms, and direct-to-consumer telemedicine companies — introduced millions of patients to a different kind of transactional care: seeing a physician they've never met, who has access to a fragmentary medical record at best, for a single isolated encounter. The experience was often fine for simple issues. For anything more complex, it highlighted exactly what gets lost when there's no relationship.

Understanding when telehealth serves you well — and when it doesn't — is a genuinely useful piece of health literacy.

**Where Telehealth Works Well**

*Medication management and refills.* If you've been taking a medication for a known condition and need a refill or a dosage discussion, a virtual visit is often ideal. Your physician can review your recent labs, assess your symptoms, and update your prescription without requiring you to take time off work for an in-person visit.

*Follow-up care for known conditions.* A three-month hypertension check-in, a post-hospitalization follow-up, a review of recent blood work, or a check-in after starting a new medication are all well-suited to telehealth. The physician knows your history, you have results to review together, and no physical examination is necessary.

*Minor illness triage.* Upper respiratory infections, sinus symptoms, urinary tract infections, minor skin issues that can be photographed, and similar complaints can often be appropriately assessed and treated via video. Your physician can evaluate whether symptoms warrant in-person assessment or can be managed remotely.

*Mental health check-ins.* Follow-up for anxiety, depression, stress management, and similar concerns are often equally effective or better virtually — patients frequently feel more at ease discussing sensitive topics from their own home environment.

*Pre-visit and post-visit communication.* Questions before a scheduled appointment, clarification after a visit, or updates on how a treatment is working are efficiently handled via messaging or brief telehealth.

**Where In-Person Care Is Necessary**

*New or unexplained complex symptoms.* Chest pain, unexplained weight loss, neurological symptoms, new lumps or masses, significant changes in function — these warrant a physical examination. No physician can responsibly evaluate new complex symptoms through a screen alone.

*Physical examination findings.* Heart sounds, lung findings, lymph node assessment, abdominal tenderness, skin findings that require direct inspection — these require an actual physical exam. Video can give a physician some information; it cannot replace hands-on assessment.

*Procedures.* Anything that involves the physician's hands — wound care, injections, biopsies, IUD placement, laceration repair — requires an in-person visit. This is obvious but worth stating.

*Annual physical examinations.* A complete preventive exam should include a full physical assessment. Telehealth can support annual care but shouldn't replace the periodic in-person physical.

**The Critical Difference: Your Doctor vs. A Stranger**

This is where the conversation about telehealth becomes most important. Standalone telehealth — seeing a random provider through an insurance app or retail platform — is a fundamentally different experience from telehealth with your own primary care physician. The difference isn't technology; it's relationship and context.

A physician who knows you can assess "this is different from your baseline" versus "this is within normal for you" in a way that a stranger cannot. They can evaluate a symptom in the context of your full history, your current medications, your recent labs, and the pattern they've observed over years of caring for you. That context enables better clinical judgment and prevents both under-reaction and over-reaction.

Telehealth with a physician who doesn't know you produces a narrow, incomplete picture. Telehealth with your own physician, who has full context and ongoing accountability for your health, is a genuine clinical tool.

**The DPC Model Makes Telehealth Actually Work**

In a Direct Primary Care practice, telehealth isn't a workaround or a separate service — it's an integrated part of how your physician cares for you. Because DPC physicians maintain small panels and have direct access to their patients, a telehealth visit is a continuation of an ongoing relationship, not a standalone transaction.

Direct messaging — another feature standard in most DPC practices — extends this further. Many concerns that would otherwise require an appointment can be handled through an asynchronous message exchange: a quick question about a medication interaction, an update on a symptom, a request to review a recent specialist note. This kind of communication is only useful when the person on the other end knows who you are.

## Practical Tips

- When in doubt about whether a concern warrants in-person care, contact your physician and ask — most DPC physicians would rather you message them with the question than either ignore a real problem or make an unnecessary trip in.
- Take advantage of direct messaging for minor questions and updates; not every question needs an appointment, and the friction of scheduling shouldn't stand between you and useful medical advice.
- For telehealth video visits, set yourself up in a well-lit space where you can position the camera clearly if needed — your physician may ask to see a skin finding, throat, or other visible area.
- Keep a list of current medications, doses, and any supplements updated so you can reference it quickly during a telehealth visit.
- Don't use standalone insurance telehealth apps as your primary care relationship — they're useful for isolated acute concerns when your own physician is unavailable, but they shouldn't replace continuity.
- If you're uncertain whether a symptom is urgent, a brief telehealth check-in with your physician is often the most efficient triage tool available.
- After an urgent care or ER visit, follow up with your primary care physician — telehealth can work well for these post-acute check-ins, and it closes the loop on your ongoing care record.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I get a prescription through telehealth?**
A: In most cases, yes — your physician can prescribe medications for appropriate conditions via telehealth. Controlled substances have specific regulatory requirements around telehealth prescribing that have evolved since the pandemic; your physician can advise on what's possible for your situation.

**Q: What if I need a physical exam but can't get to the office?**
A: Contact your physician and explain your circumstances. In some cases, a telehealth visit can assess severity and determine urgency. In others, your physician may be able to arrange a home visit, or identify an appropriate alternative for interim care. The goal is to get you the right level of care for your specific situation.

**Q: Is telehealth covered by my insurance if I have a DPC practice?**
A: In a DPC practice, telehealth visits with your DPC physician are covered as part of your membership — there's no additional insurance billing. If you use insurance-covered standalone telehealth services separately, those would be processed through your insurance according to your plan's telehealth benefits.

**Q: How does direct messaging work — is it secure?**
A: DPC practices use HIPAA-compliant secure messaging platforms for patient communication, not regular email or text. Your physician can access your messages during the day and respond directly. Most DPC physicians are clear about their response time expectations for different types of messages — urgent concerns may warrant a phone call or same-day telehealth; non-urgent questions typically receive a response within one business day.
