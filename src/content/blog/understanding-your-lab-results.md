## Quick Answer
Most routine blood tests fall into a handful of panels — CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, lipid panel, HbA1c, and TSH. Understanding what each component measures helps you have a more informed conversation with your physician, and helps you understand why a single flagged value does not always mean something is wrong.

## Explanation

When lab results land in your patient portal, it can feel like you are reading a foreign language — rows of abbreviations, numbers, and H or L flags that seem alarming without context. This guide explains what the most common labs actually measure, why the "normal range" is not the whole story, and how to approach your results with useful questions rather than unnecessary anxiety.

**Complete Blood Count (CBC):**

The CBC measures the cells in your blood. It is one of the most commonly ordered tests in medicine.

- **Red blood cells (RBC) and hemoglobin/hematocrit**: These measure the oxygen-carrying capacity of your blood. Low values indicate anemia; high values (polycythemia) can suggest dehydration or certain medical conditions. Hemoglobin is the protein in RBCs that actually carries oxygen; hematocrit is the percentage of your blood volume made up of RBCs.
- **Mean corpuscular volume (MCV)**: The average size of your red blood cells. Small red cells (low MCV) often indicate iron deficiency anemia; large red cells (high MCV) can indicate B12 or folate deficiency, thyroid disease, or certain medications (including methotrexate and alcohol use).
- **White blood cells (WBC)**: These are your immune cells. Elevated WBC can indicate infection, inflammation, or, rarely, a blood cell disorder. A low WBC can reflect viral infections, certain medications, or bone marrow suppression.
- **Platelets**: These are the cells involved in blood clotting. Very low platelets can increase bleeding risk; very high platelets can indicate inflammation, iron deficiency, or, rarely, a clotting disorder.

**Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP):**

The CMP is a 14-test panel covering kidney function, liver function, electrolytes, and blood sugar.

- **Glucose**: Blood sugar. Elevated fasting glucose (100 mg/dL or above) suggests prediabetes; 126 mg/dL or above on two separate occasions indicates diabetes.
- **BUN (blood urea nitrogen) and creatinine**: These measure kidney function. BUN is a waste product from protein metabolism; creatinine is a muscle waste product, both filtered by the kidneys. Elevated values can indicate dehydration, kidney disease, or high protein intake. Creatinine is used to calculate your estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), the primary measure of kidney function.
- **Sodium, potassium, bicarbonate, chloride**: These electrolytes regulate fluid balance, nerve function, and pH. Abnormalities can indicate dehydration, kidney disease, certain medications, adrenal conditions, and more.
- **Calcium**: Regulated by the parathyroid glands. Mildly elevated calcium (hypercalcemia) is often found incidentally and most commonly caused by primary hyperparathyroidism.
- **Liver enzymes (AST, ALT, alkaline phosphatase, total bilirubin, albumin)**: AST and ALT are enzymes that leak into the blood when liver cells are damaged — elevated levels can indicate fatty liver disease, alcohol use, medication effects, viral hepatitis, or other liver conditions. Albumin reflects the liver's synthetic function; low albumin can indicate malnutrition or chronic liver disease.

**Lipid Panel:**

Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides — see our dedicated cholesterol article for a full breakdown of what each component means and when to start screening.

**HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin):**

HbA1c reflects your average blood sugar over the past 2–3 months, specifically by measuring what percentage of hemoglobin has sugar attached to it. It is the primary tool for diagnosing and monitoring diabetes and prediabetes:
- Below 5.7%: normal
- 5.7–6.4%: prediabetes
- 6.5% or above: diabetes (on two separate occasions, or once with symptoms)

For patients with established diabetes, an HbA1c below 7% is the general treatment target, though individualized goals are appropriate based on patient age, risk of hypoglycemia, and other factors.

**TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone):**

TSH is the primary screening test for thyroid function. It is produced by the pituitary gland and drives thyroid hormone production. A high TSH indicates the pituitary is working harder to stimulate an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism); a low TSH indicates the thyroid may be overactive (hyperthyroidism) or that external thyroid hormone is suppressing the pituitary. TSH is highly sensitive and is usually the first thyroid test ordered.

**Understanding "flagged" results in context:**

Lab reference ranges are typically built from the middle 95% of a healthy population — meaning about 5% of healthy people will fall outside the reference range on any given test, purely by statistical chance. When you run 20 tests on a healthy person, on average one will come back flagged even if nothing is wrong. A single mildly abnormal value with no symptoms or clinical context is rarely diagnostic by itself.

What matters is the pattern, the magnitude of the abnormality, the trend over time, and the clinical picture. A creatinine of 1.2 mg/dL in a 25-year-old muscular man is almost certainly normal. The same value in a 75-year-old woman may reflect significant kidney dysfunction. Your physician's job is to interpret results in the context of who you are — not just whether a number is above or below a line on a page.

## Practical Tips

- Do not assume a flagged result is a crisis — ask your physician to explain the clinical significance before drawing conclusions
- Bring previous lab results to appointments so trends can be assessed, not just single data points
- Ask specifically what you can do to improve any abnormal values — often there are concrete lifestyle changes that move numbers meaningfully
- Fasting labs (blood draw after 8–12 hours without food) give more consistent glucose and triglyceride readings; confirm with your physician whether you should fast before your draw
- If you receive results through a portal without explanation, reach out to your physician's office directly — important results should not be interpreted in isolation
- Keep a personal copy of your key lab values over time; tracking HbA1c, cholesterol, and kidney function year over year is genuinely useful
- Ask about units and context: cholesterol in mg/dL vs. mmol/L, creatinine in a large vs. small person — numbers mean different things in different contexts

## FAQ

**Q: My ALT was slightly elevated. Does that mean I have liver disease?**
A: Not necessarily. Mildly elevated ALT (alanine aminotransferase) is common and can be caused by fatty liver disease, strenuous exercise in the 48 hours before the test, alcohol, certain medications (including ibuprofen, acetaminophen, and statins), and many other factors. A single mild elevation typically warrants repeat testing and a review of potential causes rather than alarm. Significantly elevated ALT or persistent elevation over time requires further workup.

**Q: My WBC is slightly low. Should I be concerned?**
A: A mildly low white blood cell count (leukopenia) is often a normal variant, particularly in people of African or Middle Eastern descent (benign ethnic neutropenia). Viral infections commonly suppress WBC transiently. If you are otherwise healthy and the value is only mildly reduced, your physician will likely recheck it in a few weeks rather than initiating an extensive workup. Consistently low WBC or very low WBC does warrant further evaluation.

**Q: What does it mean if my vitamin D level is low?**
A: Vitamin D deficiency is extremely common, particularly in people who have limited sun exposure or live in northern latitudes. Low vitamin D (typically defined as below 20 ng/mL) is associated with bone health issues, and there is suggestive evidence linking it to immune function and other health outcomes (though the evidence for supplementing to prevent non-bone conditions is less definitive). Most people respond well to oral vitamin D3 supplementation; your physician can recommend an appropriate dose based on your level.

**Q: My eGFR was 62. Is my kidney function bad?**
A: An eGFR of 62 mL/min/1.73m² represents Stage 2 chronic kidney disease by definition, but context is critical. eGFR normally declines slowly with age — an eGFR in the 60s is not unusual in an older adult, and it may not require any active treatment beyond monitoring and avoiding nephrotoxic medications. What matters is whether it is stable, slowly declining, or dropping rapidly. A single value needs to be compared with prior values; a kidney function that has been stable at 62 for years is very different from one that has dropped from 90 to 62 in 12 months.
