## Quick Answer
An Explanation of Benefits is a statement from your insurance company — not a bill — that shows how a medical claim was processed. Understanding each section helps you verify your charges, catch billing errors, and understand what you actually owe.

## Explanation

After any medical visit, procedure, or prescription claim processed through insurance, your insurer sends you an Explanation of Benefits, or EOB. Many patients either ignore it or panic that it's a bill they need to pay. It's neither: it's a detailed accounting of how your claim was processed and a tool for catching errors and understanding your cost responsibility.

Billing errors in healthcare are surprisingly common. Studies have estimated that up to 80 percent of medical bills contain errors — though the rates of consequential errors are lower. Regardless, reviewing your EOB is a meaningful form of financial self-protection.

**Key columns in an EOB**

While layouts vary by insurer, every EOB contains the same essential information:

**Billed Amount (or Charged Amount)**
This is the amount the provider billed to the insurance company — sometimes called the "sticker price." It often looks alarming. This number is typically not what anyone actually pays. Providers and insurers have negotiated rates that are significantly lower than the billed amount.

**Allowed Amount (or Negotiated Rate)**
This is the amount your insurer has agreed to accept for the service based on their contract with the provider. If your provider is in-network, they're contractually obligated to accept this lower amount. The difference between the billed amount and the allowed amount is simply written off — neither you nor the insurance company pays the difference.

**What Insurance Paid**
This is what your insurance company paid directly to the provider. If you haven't met your deductible yet, this may be zero, even if the service is covered. After meeting your deductible, insurance pays its share of the allowed amount (minus any coinsurance or copay).

**What You Owe (Patient Responsibility)**
This is the only number that matters for payment purposes. It reflects your deductible contributions, copays, and coinsurance applied to the allowed amount. This is what you should eventually see on a bill from the provider — and if the bill doesn't match the "patient responsibility" on your EOB, that's a discrepancy worth investigating.

**EOB vs. bill: what's the difference?**

An EOB comes from your insurance company. A bill comes from the provider. You should receive an EOB before or around the same time as the provider's bill. The patient responsibility amount on your EOB should match what the provider bills you. If they don't match, contact the provider's billing department first — and your insurer if needed.

**Balance billing: what it is and when it's illegal**

Balance billing occurs when an out-of-network provider bills you for the difference between their charge and what your insurance paid — not just your cost-sharing portion. This can result in unexpectedly large bills, particularly after emergency care or after receiving care at an in-network facility from an out-of-network provider (a common situation in hospital-based care).

The No Surprises Act, which went into effect in 2022, prohibits balance billing in many emergency and some non-emergency situations, particularly when patients didn't have a meaningful choice of provider. If you receive a balance bill you believe violates this law, you can dispute it through your insurer or the federal dispute resolution process.

**How to spot errors on your EOB**

- Check that the service date and provider name are correct — wrong-provider billing is more common than you'd expect.
- Verify the procedure codes (listed on the EOB) match what actually happened. A code for a complex office visit when you had a simple one can significantly change costs.
- Look for duplicate charges — the same service billed twice.
- Check that your insurance information was applied correctly — wrong member ID or policy numbers cause claim processing errors.
- If you were billed for services you didn't receive, that's a red flag for billing fraud, which should be reported.

**How DPC eliminates the EOB for primary care**

In a direct primary care practice, there is no insurance claim for primary care services. Your physician is paid through your membership fee, not through insurance. No claim is submitted, no EOB is generated, and there's no billing department to call about a discrepancy. The financial relationship is transparent and direct.

## Practical Tips

- Save your EOBs until you've received and paid the corresponding bill from the provider. They're your record for resolving discrepancies.
- Never pay a bill from a medical provider before checking that it matches your EOB. Overpayment is difficult to reclaim.
- If you're confused by a claim decision, call the member services number on your insurance card — they're required to explain any decision.
- Ask your provider's billing department for an itemized bill. This gives you the specific codes billed so you can verify them against your EOB.
- EOBs are available digitally through your insurer's member portal — often faster than waiting for the paper version.
- If you receive a bill significantly larger than your EOB's patient responsibility amount, suspect an error before assuming it's correct.
- Know the No Surprises Act exists and covers you in most balance billing situations from out-of-network providers in emergency settings.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need to do anything when I receive an EOB?**
A: Not always — but you should read it. Compare the patient responsibility amount to any bill you receive from the provider. If they match and the amounts seem correct, you're in good shape. If there's a discrepancy, investigate before paying.

**Q: What if my insurance denied a claim on the EOB?**
A: Denials appear on EOBs with a reason code. Common reasons include services not covered by your plan, missing prior authorization, or out-of-network providers. You have the right to appeal any denial. The EOB will include information about the appeals process, and your provider's office can often help initiate an appeal.

**Q: Can I request an itemized bill to check against my EOB?**
A: Yes, and you should for any significant medical bill. An itemized bill lists every service, supply, and procedure with the specific codes billed. Comparing it to your EOB helps you spot inaccuracies. Providers are required to provide itemized bills upon request.

**Q: My EOB shows the billed amount is $2,000 but I only owe $150. Is that normal?**
A: Yes, this is normal and reflects how in-network billing works. The billed amount is the provider's charge. The allowed amount is the negotiated rate your insurer and provider agreed to. Insurance pays part of the allowed amount; you pay the rest (deductible, copay, coinsurance). The difference between billed and allowed is simply written off by the in-network provider.
