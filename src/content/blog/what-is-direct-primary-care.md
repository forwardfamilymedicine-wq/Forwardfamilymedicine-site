## Quick Answer
Direct primary care (DPC) is a healthcare model where you pay your doctor a flat monthly membership fee — typically $100–$200/month for adults — in exchange for unlimited primary care visits, same-day access, and a direct relationship with your physician. There is no insurance billing, no copays, and no surprise fees.

## Explanation

If you've ever felt rushed during a doctor's appointment, struggled to get a same-day slot when you were sick, or received a bill weeks later that made no sense, you've experienced the frustrations built into traditional primary care. Direct primary care was designed to fix exactly those problems.

### The Simple Version

DPC works like a gym membership — except instead of access to treadmills, you get access to your doctor. You pay a predictable monthly fee, and in return, you can see your physician as often as you need. Appointments aren't billed individually. There's no co-pay at the door. Your doctor doesn't have to code your visit for insurance reimbursement. You just show up (or send a message), and your doctor takes care of you.

### What's Included in a DPC Membership

While every DPC practice is a little different, most memberships include:

- **Unlimited office visits** — sick visits, follow-ups, physicals, and preventive care
- **Same-day or next-day appointments** — because your doctor has a smaller patient panel
- **Longer appointment times** — 1 to 2 hours at Forward Family Medicine, compared to the 7–10 minutes typical in traditional care
- **Direct communication** — text, phone, or email your doctor directly
- **After-hours access** — reach a real physician when you need one, not a nurse line
- **Telehealth visits** — included in your membership, no separate fee

Many DPC practices also offer deeply discounted labs and medications at wholesale prices, passing those savings directly to patients.

### What DPC Is NOT

This is where a lot of confusion happens, so let's be direct:

**DPC is not health insurance.** It does not cover hospitalization, emergency care, specialist visits, imaging, or surgery. If you're enrolled in a DPC practice, you still need health insurance — or some coverage — for anything beyond primary care.

**DPC is not concierge medicine.** Concierge medicine charges a retainer fee on top of billing your insurance. DPC eliminates insurance billing entirely and is typically far more affordable than concierge practices, which can cost $300–$500/month or more.

**DPC is not a restricted network.** You're not locked out of using your insurance elsewhere. Your insurance handles hospitals, specialists, and emergencies. Your DPC practice handles everything primary care.

### How DPC Differs from Traditional Primary Care

In a traditional primary care practice, your doctor may be responsible for 2,000 to 3,000 patients. To keep the lights on, they need to see 20–30 patients every single day. That math leaves very little time per patient — which is why appointments feel rushed, why it takes weeks to get a routine visit, and why your doctor sometimes seems like a stranger.

DPC practices cap their panels at 300–600 patients per physician generally; Forward Family Medicine caps at approximately 250 patients per physician. Because the practice is funded by membership fees rather than insurance reimbursement, the doctor has no incentive to overbook. The result: longer visits, faster access, and a physician who actually knows your health history.

### Who Is DPC For?

DPC works beautifully for people who:

- Want a real relationship with a physician who knows them
- Are self-employed or don't have employer-sponsored insurance
- Have a high-deductible health plan and pay out of pocket for most primary care anyway
- Manage one or more chronic conditions (diabetes, hypertension, anxiety, etc.) and need frequent check-ins
- Are tired of waiting weeks for a routine appointment
- Have a family and want one trusted physician for everyone

DPC is not the right fit for someone whose employer provides a traditional PPO with low copays and they never actually use primary care. But for the vast majority of Americans who feel underserved by the current system, DPC offers something genuinely better.

## Practical Tips

- **Pair DPC with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP)** — lower premiums on your insurance plus flat-fee primary care is often less expensive than a traditional PPO.
- **Use your DPC membership for what it's designed for** — don't avoid calling your doctor. The monthly fee is already paid; use it.
- **Ask about labs and medications** — many DPC practices offer wholesale lab pricing and in-office dispensing that can save hundreds per year.
- **Schedule a same-day visit when you're sick** — don't default to urgent care. Your DPC doctor can often handle the same conditions for no extra charge.
- **Communicate directly** — if your practice allows texting, use it for follow-up questions. No appointment needed for a quick check-in.
- **Bring your family** — per-member pricing often means real savings when you enroll multiple people.
- **Ask about specialist coordination** — a good DPC physician will help you navigate referrals and communicate with specialists on your behalf.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I still need health insurance if I join a DPC practice?**
A: Yes. DPC covers primary care only. You still need insurance for hospitalizations, emergency care, specialist visits, imaging, and surgery. Most DPC members pair their membership with a high-deductible health plan or health-sharing arrangement to keep overall costs manageable.

**Q: How much does direct primary care cost?**
A: DPC membership fees vary by practice and age but typically range from $100 to $200 per month for adults. Children are often less. At Forward Family Medicine, visit our [membership page](/membership) for current pricing. There are no per-visit fees on top of your monthly membership.

**Q: Is direct primary care the same as concierge medicine?**
A: No. Concierge medicine charges a retainer fee and still bills your insurance for visits. DPC does not bill insurance at all, which eliminates administrative overhead and keeps costs significantly lower. DPC is accessible to a much broader range of patients than traditional concierge care.

**Q: Can I use my HSA to pay for DPC membership fees?**
A: Following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP. Confirm with your plan administrator. HSA funds can also be used for other services your DPC practice provides, such as labs and medications.
