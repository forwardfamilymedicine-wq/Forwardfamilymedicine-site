## Quick Answer
Direct primary care can be an excellent fit for families — per-member pricing, no per-visit fees, and one physician who knows your whole family means fewer surprises and better care for kids and adults alike. For families who use primary care regularly, the math often favors DPC over traditional copays and deductibles.

## Explanation

When you have kids, primary care isn't something you use occasionally — it's something you use constantly. Ear infections, sports physicals, back-to-school checkups, mystery rashes at 7 p.m. on a Friday, follow-ups after strep. Add adults managing their own health needs and the cumulative cost of traditional primary care in a family of four can be staggering before you've even thought about a specialist.

Direct primary care offers a different model, and for families, the fit can be especially good.

### How DPC Pricing Works for Families

Most DPC practices charge on a per-member basis, with pricing that typically varies by age:

- **Children** are usually the least expensive tier — often significantly less than adult pricing, reflecting that their care needs, while frequent, tend to be shorter and more straightforward.
- **Adults** pay a standard monthly rate, often with slight increases at older age brackets.
- **Families** pay the sum of their individual member rates, with no per-visit fees on top.

This structure means a family of four pays a predictable monthly amount and can see the doctor as many times as needed throughout the month with no additional cost. Contrast that with a family carrying a traditional PPO — $30–$50 in copays per visit adds up fast when you have two kids who need to be seen four to six times per year each, plus adult visits on top.

### The Value of One Physician Who Knows Everyone

One of the most underappreciated benefits of family DPC isn't the cost — it's the continuity. When one physician follows your whole family over time, they accumulate something genuinely valuable: context.

Your doctor knows that your youngest tends to get ear infections that resolve on their own. They know your teenager has anxiety that surfaces as stomachaches during the school year. They know your spouse has a family history of early heart disease and takes that into account when reviewing labs. They know you've been struggling to sleep.

In a traditional practice, that continuity is frequently broken. You might see a different provider each time because availability is limited, or your children's pediatrician is across town from your own internist, and no one is looking at the full picture. DPC restores that coherence.

### Is DPC Appropriate for Children?

Absolutely. Many DPC physicians practice family medicine, meaning they're trained to care for patients from infancy through adulthood. Well-child visits, immunizations, developmental screenings, sports physicals, and acute sick care are all well within the scope of DPC.

A few things worth knowing:

- **Immunizations** at DPC practices may be handled differently than at a traditional pediatric office. Some DPC practices stock vaccines as part of membership; others have patients obtain vaccines through their pharmacy or county health department. Ask your DPC practice how they handle this before enrolling.
- **Newborns and infants** can typically be enrolled in DPC from birth.
- **Adolescents** often benefit enormously from having a trusted physician relationship — particularly for mental health conversations, which tend to go better when there's an established relationship rather than a rushed visit with a stranger.

### The Real Cost Comparison

Let's sketch out a rough comparison for a family of four — two adults, two kids — currently on a PPO:

**Traditional PPO:**
- Monthly premium: $800–$1,200/month (employer may subsidize, but many families contribute $400–$600/month)
- Primary care copays: $30–$50 each, 8–12 visits/year = $240–$600/year
- Specialist copays if needed: additional cost
- Deductible exposure: $1,500–$3,000 per person if anyone gets sick

**DPC + HDHP:**
- DPC membership for family of four: varies by practice (visit [membership page](/membership))
- HDHP premium: often $300–$500/month less than comparable PPO
- Primary care visits: no additional cost (included in membership)
- HSA contributions possible to cover deductible exposure — and following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP — an additional financial benefit worth factoring into your total cost comparison

For families who regularly use primary care, the DPC + HDHP combination frequently delivers equivalent or better care at a meaningfully lower total annual spend.

## Practical Tips

- **Enroll the whole family at once** — the continuity benefit compounds when one physician knows everyone.
- **Use sick visits liberally** — you're already paying the monthly fee; don't default to urgent care when your child has an ear infection. Call your DPC practice first.
- **Ask about vaccine logistics upfront** — before enrolling, confirm how your DPC practice handles childhood immunizations to avoid surprises.
- **Use telehealth for quick questions** — rashes, pink eye, post-strep follow-ups, and minor concerns can often be handled by video or photo, saving everyone a trip to the office.
- **Schedule well-child visits early in the year** — DPC practices can usually accommodate these quickly, but getting ahead of the back-to-school rush makes sense.
- **Have a conversation with your doctor about the family's health patterns** — a good DPC physician will want to know about family history, recurring issues, and any concerns you have about each family member.
- **Compare your actual annual spend** — add up last year's premiums, copays, deductibles, and urgent care visits to get a real number before dismissing DPC as "too expensive."

## FAQ

**Q: Can a family doctor in a DPC practice see my newborn?**
A: Most DPC family medicine physicians can care for newborns and infants. You'll want to confirm before enrolling, as some practices have age minimums. Newborn care in a DPC setting can be wonderful — your physician already knows your family and can provide consistent, personalized attention from the very start.

**Q: What if my child needs a specialist?**
A: DPC covers primary care. If your child needs a specialist, that referral goes through your health insurance as it would with any primary care physician. A good DPC physician will help coordinate that referral, communicate with the specialist, and follow up afterward — which is often better coordination than families receive in traditional care.

**Q: Are sports physicals and school forms included in the DPC membership?**
A: In most DPC practices, yes — sports physicals, school physical forms, and camp forms are included in membership and are not billed separately. This is a meaningful benefit for families with school-age children who need these documents annually.

**Q: Is DPC worth it if my family is generally healthy and rarely needs the doctor?**
A: It depends on how you look at it. If your family truly uses primary care once every couple of years and has good insurance with low copays, DPC may not deliver obvious cost savings. But many families find that when they finally have easy access to a doctor they trust — no waiting, no hassle — they actually engage with preventive care more, catch issues earlier, and feel less anxious about their health. The value isn't always purely financial.
