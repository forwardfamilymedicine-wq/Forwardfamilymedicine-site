## Quick Answer
Both models offer smaller patient panels and better access to your physician, but traditional concierge medicine typically charges a higher retainer and still bills insurance, while Direct Primary Care charges a flat monthly fee with no insurance billing — delivering a similar experience at a significantly lower cost.

## Explanation

The terms "concierge medicine" and "direct primary care" get used interchangeably in conversation, and the confusion is understandable. Both involve paying directly for primary care. Both promise a closer relationship with your physician. Both offer same-day appointments and direct access. But the underlying structures are quite different, and those differences have real consequences for what you pay and how the practice operates.

**Traditional Concierge Medicine**

Traditional concierge medicine emerged in the late 1990s, pioneered by practices catering to high-net-worth patients who wanted more than standard insurance-based care could provide. The model works like this: patients pay an annual or monthly retainer fee — typically ranging from $150 to $500 per month, or $1,500 to $6,000 or more per year — in exchange for enhanced services and access. That retainer buys the VIP experience: same-day appointments, the physician's personal cell number, unhurried visits, house calls in some cases, and a doctor who genuinely knows you.

The key detail: most traditional concierge practices still accept and bill insurance. The retainer covers the "access and amenity" layer; insurance is still billed for the actual clinical services rendered at each visit. This means the practice maintains all the billing infrastructure, coding staff, and administrative burden of a conventional practice — it's just layered on top of the retainer. For the physician, this is financially stable but operationally complex. For patients, it means the retainer is genuinely an additional cost on top of existing insurance premiums and copays.

**Direct Primary Care**

Direct Primary Care (DPC) took shape in the mid-2000s and represents a more radical departure from the insurance-based system. In a DPC practice, patients pay a flat monthly membership fee — typically $75 to $200 per month for adults depending on the practice, location, and what is included. At Forward Family Medicine, individual membership is $200/month — which includes integrated board-certified Lifestyle Medicine that most DPC practices do not offer — and the practice does not bill insurance for any primary care services. The monthly fee covers office visits, most in-office procedures, care coordination, direct physician access, and often basic lab work. Period.

Because DPC practices opt out of insurance billing entirely, they eliminate the administrative overhead that consumes 25 to 30 percent of revenue in conventional practices. No billing staff. No coding audits. No pre-authorization calls. No claims denials to fight. That overhead reduction is what makes the DPC membership price so much lower than the traditional concierge retainer — not a lesser level of care, but a leaner back office.

**The experience for patients is remarkably similar.** In both models, you get:

- A physician with a small patient panel (typically 300–600 patients in DPC generally, though at Forward Family Medicine we cap our panel at 250 patients per physician — smaller than the DPC average, which is what makes same-day access consistently reliable — versus 1,500–2,500 in conventional practices)
- Same-day or next-day appointment availability
- Extended visits with time to actually talk
- Direct physician access by phone, text, or email
- A care relationship built on continuity

**Where they diverge:**

The biggest practical difference is cost and complexity. Traditional concierge can run $3,000 to $6,000 per year just for the retainer, before insurance premiums, copays, and deductibles. DPC typically costs $900 to $1,800 per year in membership fees, and because primary care visits are covered, patients can often pair a DPC membership with a high-deductible health plan — keeping total healthcare spending lower than a traditional PPO arrangement.

There's also a philosophical difference. DPC practices are explicitly outside the insurance system for primary care; the physician's financial incentive is your continued satisfaction and health, not maximizing billable encounters. Traditional concierge practices, because they still bill insurance, retain some of the fee-for-service incentive structure.

**Which is better for you?**

At Forward Family Medicine, we don't think of DPC and concierge as a tradeoff. Every member receives what the industry would call a concierge experience — a panel capped at 250 patients, a doctor who picks up the phone, 1–2 hour visits, and direct access by phone, text, and email. We've simply structured it without the insurance billing overhead that inflates the traditional concierge retainer. The result is a concierge-quality experience at a DPC price point.

## Practical Tips

- When evaluating any concierge or DPC practice, ask explicitly: "Do you still bill insurance for visits, or is everything covered by the membership?" — the answer tells you which model you're looking at.
- Calculate your true annual cost: traditional concierge retainer plus insurance premiums plus expected copays/deductibles, versus DPC membership plus an HDHP premium.
- For patients with one or more chronic conditions requiring frequent primary care visits, DPC often delivers the greatest financial benefit — all those visits are covered in the flat fee.
- DPC pairs well with a health-sharing ministry membership or a low-premium catastrophic plan for patients who are otherwise healthy.
- Ask about panel size at any practice you're considering — this is the single most important determinant of whether you'll actually get same-day access.
- Some employers are beginning to offer DPC memberships as a benefit; ask your HR department if this is an option.
- Don't overlook direct messaging and telehealth as part of the value equation — for many patients, asynchronous access to their physician is worth as much as in-person visits.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I use insurance at a DPC practice for anything?**
A: DPC practices don't bill insurance for primary care services, but patients still use their insurance for outside services: specialist visits, hospitalizations, imaging, procedures, prescriptions, and emergency care. Most DPC patients maintain health insurance for those needs.

**Q: Is DPC only for healthy people who rarely need care?**
A: Actually the opposite is often true. Patients with chronic conditions like diabetes, hypertension, or thyroid disorders frequently benefit the most from DPC because they need regular primary care visits — which are all included in the flat fee. The continuity and access also supports better chronic disease management.

**Q: Why do some DPC practices call themselves "concierge" and vice versa?**
A: The terminology has never been precisely regulated, and marketing language varies by practice. The most reliable way to understand any practice is to ask directly: what does the membership fee include, do you bill insurance, and what is the current panel size?

**Q: If I join a DPC practice, do I still need to pay for lab work and imaging?**
A: It depends on the practice. Many DPC practices include basic labs in the membership or negotiate deeply discounted rates for members. Imaging and specialist visits are generally separate and would be billed through your insurance or paid at negotiated cash-pay rates. Ask any prospective DPC practice to walk you through their specific inclusions and the costs for common services outside the membership.
