## Quick Answer
Health insurance is full of terms most people never learned to define — and that confusion costs real money. Understanding the key concepts helps you choose the right plan, use it correctly, and avoid unexpected bills.

## Explanation

Health insurance is one of the most significant financial products most Americans interact with — and one of the least understood. The terminology is dense, the tradeoffs are real, and the stakes are high. This article walks through the terms you actually need to understand and explains how they interact.

**Premium**

Your premium is what you pay each month to have health insurance, regardless of whether you use it. Think of it as the cost of having the coverage. A lower premium sounds appealing, but it almost always comes with higher costs when you actually use your insurance — higher deductibles, copays, and coinsurance.

**Deductible**

The deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before your insurance starts covering most services. If your deductible is $3,000, you pay the first $3,000 of covered medical costs yourself each year. After meeting your deductible, your insurance begins sharing costs. Preventive services (annual physicals, certain screenings) are typically covered before the deductible under the ACA.

**Copay**

A copay is a fixed dollar amount you pay for a specific service — for example, $30 for a primary care visit or $50 for a specialist. Copays may apply before or after the deductible depending on your plan. Some plans have copays that count toward the deductible; others have copays that are separate from it.

**Coinsurance**

After you meet your deductible, you often still pay a percentage of costs rather than a fixed copay. This is coinsurance. If your plan has 20% coinsurance, your insurance pays 80% and you pay 20% of covered costs until you reach your out-of-pocket maximum.

**Out-of-Pocket Maximum**

The out-of-pocket maximum is the most you'll pay in a given year for covered services. Once you hit this number, your insurance covers 100% of covered costs for the rest of the year. This is your financial protection against catastrophic medical events.

**In-Network vs. Out-of-Network**

Insurance plans negotiate rates with specific providers and facilities — those providers are "in-network." Seeing an out-of-network provider usually means significantly higher costs, and some plans don't cover out-of-network care at all except in emergencies. Always verify a provider is in-network before your appointment.

**Prior Authorization**

Many insurance plans require prior authorization — advance approval — before covering certain medications, procedures, or specialist referrals. If your doctor orders something that requires prior auth, the practice submits a request to your insurer, which may approve, deny, or request more information. This process adds time and administrative burden and can delay care.

**Explanation of Benefits (EOB)**

After an insurance claim is processed, you receive an EOB — a statement showing what was billed, what the insurance allowed, what they paid, and what you owe. This is not a bill. It's a statement of how a claim was processed. (We have a full article on how to read an EOB.)

**HDHP vs. PPO**

A High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) has a lower monthly premium but a higher deductible before insurance kicks in. A PPO (Preferred Provider Organization) typically has higher premiums but lower deductibles and more flexibility in choosing providers. HDHPs qualify for use with Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) — tax-advantaged accounts you can use to pay for medical expenses.

**Calculating your actual annual cost**

The cheapest plan isn't the one with the lowest premium — it's the one with the lowest total cost given how you actually use healthcare. A rough calculation:

Annual cost = (monthly premium × 12) + expected out-of-pocket expenses

If you're generally healthy and rarely use care, a lower-premium, higher-deductible plan may save money. If you have ongoing conditions or anticipated needs, a higher-premium plan with lower cost-sharing may cost less overall.

**The DPC + HDHP combination**

Many DPC members pair their membership with a high-deductible health insurance plan. The logic: primary care — the majority of your medical needs — is covered through your DPC membership at a predictable monthly cost. The HDHP provides catastrophic coverage for hospitalizations, specialist care, surgeries, and other major events that primary care can't handle. This combination often lowers total annual healthcare spending significantly while improving access to primary care.

## Practical Tips

- Don't choose a health insurance plan based on the premium alone. Calculate your expected total annual cost using your actual utilization history.
- If you have an HDHP, open and fund an HSA. Contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free. It's one of the best tax-advantaged accounts available.
- Verify that your providers are in-network before every appointment — networks change annually.
- Prior authorization denials can be appealed. If your insurer denies coverage for something your doctor has ordered, ask your provider's office to help you appeal.
- Read your plan's Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) document — it's standardized and summarizes costs clearly in plain language. Every plan is required to provide one.
- Your out-of-pocket maximum is your ceiling. Knowing this number helps you plan for worst-case scenarios and avoid financial panic during serious illness.
- If you're healthy and primarily need preventive and routine care, DPC membership may cover the vast majority of what you actually use — making a lower-premium, higher-deductible plan a reasonable option.

## FAQ

**Q: What's the difference between a deductible and an out-of-pocket maximum?**
A: Your deductible is the amount you pay before insurance begins cost-sharing. Your out-of-pocket maximum is the most you'll pay in a year — the ceiling. After meeting your deductible, you typically pay coinsurance until you hit the out-of-pocket max, at which point insurance covers 100% of covered costs.

**Q: Does a DPC membership count toward my deductible?**
A: Generally no. DPC membership fees are paid directly to the practice and don't go through insurance, so they don't count toward your deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. Note for DPC members: The Primary Care Enhancement Act, effective January 1, 2026, clarified that DPC membership fees are qualified expenses for HSA reimbursement when paired with a qualifying HDHP. This resolves prior uncertainty and makes the DPC + HDHP + HSA combination even more financially compelling. Confirm with your HSA administrator.

**Q: What happens if I need surgery or hospitalization as a DPC member?**
A: DPC covers primary care. For hospitalizations, surgeries, specialist care, and emergency services, you'll use your health insurance. This is why DPC members should carry health insurance alongside their membership, particularly for catastrophic coverage.

**Q: Is a PPO always better than an HDHP?**
A: Not necessarily. A PPO costs more in premiums and may make sense if you have high healthcare utilization or ongoing specialist needs. An HDHP costs less in premiums, requires more out-of-pocket spending before coverage kicks in, and qualifies you for an HSA. If most of your care is primary care (covered by DPC), an HDHP is often the more economical choice.
