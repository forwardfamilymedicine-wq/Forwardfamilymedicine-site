## Quick Answer
Taking five or more medications simultaneously — a condition called polypharmacy — significantly increases the risk of drug interactions and side effects. Maintaining an accurate, complete medication list and reviewing it regularly with your primary care physician is one of the most important things you can do for your safety.

## Explanation

Most people are aware that medications can interact with each other. Fewer people realize how common this problem is, or how often it goes unrecognized. When a patient is seeing multiple specialists — a cardiologist, an endocrinologist, an orthopedist, a psychiatrist — each provider may be prescribing medications with limited visibility into what the others have added. The primary care physician, ideally, is the person who sees the whole picture.

Polypharmacy technically refers to taking five or more medications concurrently, though the meaningful risks begin to escalate with each additional drug. More than 40 percent of adults over 65 take five or more prescription medications. Adverse drug reactions are among the leading causes of hospitalization in older adults — and many of these events are preventable.

**Why the medication list problem is harder than it seems**

Several factors make medication management genuinely difficult:

Patients often don't think of vitamins, supplements, and over-the-counter medications as "medications." They're not prescription, so they seem irrelevant to report. In fact, many supplements have significant drug interactions. Fish oil affects clotting. St. John's Wort interacts with dozens of medications. Grapefruit juice — not even a supplement — affects the metabolism of numerous drugs.

Medications get added over time but rarely removed. A drug started for a temporary problem may continue for years. A medication appropriate at one life stage may be harmful at another. The concept of deprescribing — the intentional, supervised reduction or discontinuation of medications that are no longer necessary or appropriate — is gaining traction in medicine precisely because this accumulation problem is so common.

People fill prescriptions at multiple pharmacies. This matters because pharmacies run drug interaction checks at the point of dispensing — but only for medications in their own system. If your cardiologist's prescription is filled at pharmacy A and your psychiatrist's at pharmacy B, neither system sees the full picture.

**Building and maintaining your medication list**

A complete medication list includes:
- Drug name (generic and brand if known)
- Dose and strength
- How often you take it
- Why you take it (the indication)
- Prescribing physician

Update this list every time something changes. Keep it somewhere accessible — a notes app on your phone works well, because it's always with you. Share it at every medical appointment, not just primary care.

**The brown bag review**

One practical tool that physicians use is the "brown bag review" — literally bringing all of your medication bottles to an appointment so your physician can review the actual containers rather than a list. This catches discrepancies between what's prescribed and what's being taken, expired medications, duplicates, and medications from multiple prescribers that no one has reconciled.

This kind of review is especially valuable at annual physicals, after any hospitalization, and after any new specialist visit.

**Questions to ask about every medication**

- Do I still need this? Has the original condition resolved?
- Is the dose still appropriate?
- Are there any interactions with my other medications?
- Are there lifestyle changes that might reduce my need for this medication?
- What are the long-term effects of taking this medication?

## Practical Tips

- Keep one up-to-date medication list in a notes app on your phone. Share it at every medical appointment, every urgent care visit, and every ER visit — regardless of whether you think it's relevant.
- Include supplements, vitamins, and over-the-counter medications on your list. They're medications.
- Fill prescriptions at the same pharmacy whenever possible. It enables the pharmacy's interaction-checking system to see your complete picture.
- After any hospitalization or specialist visit, review your medication list with your primary care physician. Hospitalizations often add, change, or discontinue medications in ways that need reconciliation after discharge.
- Ask your doctor once a year: "Are there any medications on my list that we should reconsider?" This opens the deprescribing conversation.
- Never stop a prescription medication without discussing it with your physician first — even if you feel the medication isn't helping. Some drugs have serious withdrawal effects.
- If you're caring for an aging parent, help them maintain their medication list. Polypharmacy is most dangerous in older adults, and a caregiver's involvement in medication management is often protective.

## FAQ

**Q: What's the most common dangerous drug interaction in primary care?**
A: Several interactions are especially important to know about. Blood thinners like warfarin interact with dozens of medications and supplements. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) can worsen kidney function and interact with blood pressure medications. Combining multiple medications that affect serotonin can cause serotonin syndrome. Ask your physician which interactions are most relevant to your specific combination of medications.

**Q: Can a pharmacist help review my medication list for interactions?**
A: Yes. Pharmacists are highly trained in drug interactions and are an underused resource. Many pharmacies offer medication therapy management (MTM) consultations, sometimes at no cost for qualifying patients. This is complementary to — not a replacement for — your physician's clinical judgment.

**Q: What is deprescribing and how do I ask for it?**
A: Deprescribing is the supervised reduction or discontinuation of medications that are no longer necessary or that may be causing more harm than benefit. You can simply ask your physician: "Are there any medications I'm taking that might be worth reducing or stopping?" This is a completely appropriate clinical question and a good doctor will welcome it.

**Q: If I'm seeing multiple specialists, who is responsible for making sure my medications are safe together?**
A: Ideally, your primary care physician. Specialists may not have full visibility into your complete medication list, especially medications prescribed by other specialists. Make sure your primary care physician receives notes from every specialist visit and has your complete, current medication list. This coordination role is central to what primary care is designed to do.
