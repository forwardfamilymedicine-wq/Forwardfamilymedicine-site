## Explanation

Cost is usually the first question patients have about concierge medicine — and it's a fair one. Healthcare spending is already a significant household expense, and paying an additional membership fee requires a real value calculation. The honest answer is that the cost varies widely depending on which type of concierge model you're looking at. Understanding the difference can save you thousands of dollars per year.

**Traditional Concierge Medicine: What You're Paying**

Traditional concierge practices — the kind that originated in the late 1990s as a premium offering for affluent patients — typically charge a retainer fee ranging from $1,500 to $6,000 or more per year. In practice, that works out to roughly $150 to $500 per month. High-end practices in major metropolitan areas can charge considerably more.

Critically, most traditional concierge practices still accept health insurance. The retainer buys you the enhanced access layer: the physician's personal number, same-day appointments, longer visits, and the relationship. But when you actually come in for care, the practice bills your insurance for the visit just like any conventional office would. That means you're paying the retainer *and* your insurance premiums *and* your copays and deductibles.

For some patients, this arrangement makes sense — particularly if they have comprehensive employer-sponsored insurance and are primarily buying access and time with the physician, not trying to reduce total healthcare spending.

**Direct Primary Care: The Affordable Concierge Alternative**

Direct Primary Care (DPC) practices typically charge $75 to $200 per month for adult patients, with lower rates for children and sometimes tiered pricing by age bracket. Importantly, DPC practices do not bill insurance for primary care services — the monthly fee is all-inclusive for the services covered by the practice.

What that fee typically covers:

- All office visits (as many as you need)
- Most in-office procedures (wound care, skin biopsies, EKGs, spirometry, etc.)
- Telehealth and phone/text/message access to your physician
- Preventive care visits and annual physicals
- Chronic disease management
- Basic labs in many practices, or deeply discounted lab pricing through wholesale arrangements
- Same-day and next-day scheduling

What the DPC fee typically does not cover:

- Specialist visits
- Hospitalizations
- Emergency room care
- Imaging (X-ray, MRI, CT)
- Prescription medications (though some practices offer wholesale pricing)
- Any service requiring referral outside the practice

**A Real-World Cost Comparison**

Consider a typical patient — a 45-year-old with mild hypertension who sees their primary care physician four to six times per year, gets annual labs, and occasionally needs urgent care for minor illness.

In a traditional insurance-based practice with a PPO plan:
- Annual premium contribution (employer plan, employee share): $2,400–$4,800
- Copays for six visits at $30–$40 each: $180–$240
- Lab work applied to deductible: $150–$300
- **Total: roughly $2,700–$5,300 per year**

In a DPC practice paired with a high-deductible health plan:
- HDHP premium (often $1,200–$2,400 lower than PPO): $1,200–$2,400
- DPC membership at $200/month (Forward Family Medicine individual rate)
- Labs included or deeply discounted within the DPC membership
- **Total: often comparable or lower, with significantly better access**

The math varies based on your specific situation, but for patients who use primary care regularly — especially those managing chronic conditions — DPC paired with an HDHP frequently beats a traditional PPO in both cost and service quality.

## Practical Tips

- Before comparing costs, clarify whether the practice you're evaluating still bills insurance — many "concierge" practices do, which means the retainer is a true add-on to existing insurance spending.
- Run your actual numbers: what do you pay annually in premiums, copays, and deductibles today? How often do you use primary care? A DPC membership may cost the same or less once you account for all-inclusive visits.
- Following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP — confirm current eligibility with your plan administrator.
- Ask prospective DPC practices whether labs are included in the membership or discounted, and at what price — this is often where significant savings exist.
- Consider the full value equation: not just dollars, but the time you spend navigating phone trees, waiting for appointments, and sitting in waiting rooms. DPC pricing often looks even better when that time has value.
- For families, ask about household or family membership rates — many DPC practices offer better pricing for multiple family members.
- If your employer offers an HSA-compatible plan, pairing it with a DPC membership is worth exploring as a total benefits optimization strategy.

## FAQ

**Q: Is there a one-time enrollment fee on top of the monthly membership?**
A: Some DPC practices charge a one-time enrollment fee; others don't. It's worth asking upfront. At Forward Family Medicine, visit [/membership](/membership) for current fee structure details.

**Q: Does the concierge or DPC fee cover prescriptions?**
A: Most DPC practices do not include prescriptions in the membership fee, but many have access to wholesale medication pricing through dispensaries or programs that can dramatically reduce what patients pay — sometimes to just a few dollars per medication. Ask about this when evaluating any DPC practice.

**Q: What if I need to see a specialist? Does the DPC membership help with that?**
A: The membership fee itself doesn't cover specialist visits, but your DPC physician can help you navigate the specialist landscape more efficiently. Because they know your history and have time to coordinate care, a DPC physician can often help you get to the right specialist faster, with better preparation, and avoid unnecessary referrals.

**Q: Is concierge medicine ever covered by insurance?**
A: In most cases, the membership or retainer fee is not covered by insurance. Some flexible spending accounts and health reimbursement arrangements may allow reimbursement depending on plan structure, but this varies. The underlying clinical services at a traditional concierge practice (which still bills insurance) would be processed through your plan normally. At a DPC practice, services are covered by the membership and not submitted to insurance at all.
