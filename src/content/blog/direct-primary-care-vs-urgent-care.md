## Quick Answer
Most conditions people bring to urgent care — ear infections, sinus infections, UTIs, minor injuries, sick kids — can be handled same-day by a DPC physician, at no additional cost beyond your monthly membership. Urgent care still makes sense for true walk-in needs when your DPC doctor isn't available, but DPC members use it far less often.

## Explanation

Urgent care centers have filled a real gap in American healthcare. When you can't get a timely appointment with your primary care doctor and your condition can't wait, urgent care is there. Walk in, get seen, get a prescription, pay $150–$300 (plus whatever your insurance covers or doesn't, depending on your deductible).

But here's the thing: most of what urgent care does, your primary care doctor could do better — if they were actually available.

DPC changes that. When you have a primary care physician who can see you same-day, the case for urgent care gets much narrower.

### What Urgent Care Typically Handles

Urgent care centers are designed for conditions that need timely attention but aren't emergencies:

- Upper respiratory infections, sinus infections, and the flu
- Ear infections and sore throats
- Urinary tract infections
- Minor cuts and lacerations (some UC centers do suturing)
- Sprains and strains
- Minor burns
- Pink eye
- Rashes
- X-rays for suspected minor fractures

These are competent services. Urgent care fills an access gap. The problem is cost and context.

An urgent care visit with a $40 copay sounds reasonable until you remember that visit is on top of the premiums you already pay. If you're on a high-deductible plan, that visit might cost $150–$250 out of pocket before you've met your deductible. Urgent care visits for a family of four — two kids with ear infections, a UTI, a sprained ankle — add up to hundreds or thousands of dollars per year.

And urgent care providers don't know your history. The person you see has your chart for exactly this visit and nothing else. There's no continuity, no context, no follow-up unless you schedule one.

### What DPC Handles Instead

A DPC physician can handle nearly everything on the urgent care list above — including same-day sick visits, telehealth triage, prescription calls, and follow-up — all included in your membership with no per-visit fee.

Common conditions DPC physicians handle same-day or via telehealth that routinely drive urgent care visits:

- Strep throat (rapid test, prescription if positive)
- Ear infections
- UTIs (prescription called in same day)
- Sinus infections and respiratory illnesses
- Rashes and skin conditions (photo-based telehealth works well here)
- Medication refills and prescription issues
- Follow-up after an illness
- Minor injury assessment
- Sick children

The most important difference between DPC and urgent care isn't speed — it's context. An urgent care provider sees you as a stranger. Your DPC physician already knows your medication list, your allergies, your past diagnoses, and your family history. That context changes clinical decisions in ways that matter: the antibiotic that's safe for you given your medication list, the blood pressure reading that's alarming compared to your baseline, the symptom that's new versus part of a chronic pattern. Urgent care cannot provide this regardless of how convenient it is.

### What the Research Shows

Studies of DPC practices consistently show significant reductions in urgent care utilization among members. In multiple published analyses, DPC members used urgent care 50–65% less frequently than comparable traditionally insured patients. The math makes sense: when your primary care physician is genuinely available, you don't need urgent care as a workaround.

Those avoided urgent care visits represent real money. If a family of four was previously visiting urgent care five or six times per year at $150–$250 per visit, that's $750–$1,500/year in avoided out-of-pocket costs — a meaningful offset against a DPC membership fee.

### When Urgent Care Still Makes Sense

DPC doesn't eliminate every use case for urgent care. There are scenarios where urgent care is still the right call:

- **After-hours or weekend needs when your DPC physician isn't reachable** — most DPC practices offer after-hours contact, but if you truly cannot reach your doctor and your condition needs timely attention, urgent care is appropriate.
- **True walk-in needs while traveling** — if you're away from home, urgent care may be your most practical option for acute illness.
- **Procedures your DPC practice doesn't offer** — some urgent care centers have imaging, IV fluids, or other capabilities that a smaller DPC practice may not.

### When to Go to the ER Instead

Urgent care and DPC are not appropriate substitutes for emergency care. Go to the emergency room for:

- Chest pain, difficulty breathing, signs of stroke
- Severe abdominal pain
- High fevers in infants under 3 months
- Significant head injuries
- Severe allergic reactions
- Any condition where you genuinely feel it is life-threatening

When in doubt, call your DPC physician first — they can often quickly triage whether you need the ER or whether they can manage your situation.

## Practical Tips

- **Contact your DPC physician first, even if you think you need urgent care** — a quick text or call may resolve the question without a trip anywhere.
- **Know what telehealth handles well** — rashes, pink eye, UTI symptoms, mild respiratory illness, and follow-up questions are often handleable remotely.
- **Keep your DPC physician's contact information easily accessible** — saved in your phone so you don't waste time searching when you need it fast.
- **Don't underestimate the value of continuity** — an urgent care provider who doesn't know your history will make more conservative (often more expensive) decisions than your DPC physician who knows you.
- **Track what you would have spent on urgent care** before enrolling in DPC, then compare at the end of the year. Many families find the avoided urgent care costs alone pay for the membership.
- **Know your ER indicators** — make sure your whole family knows the conditions that require an ER versus a call to your DPC physician.
- **Ask your DPC practice about after-hours protocols** — knowing exactly how to reach your physician after hours prevents unnecessary urgent care visits from uncertainty alone.

## FAQ

**Q: What does a typical urgent care visit cost?**
A: Urgent care costs vary, but cash-pay visits typically run $150–$300. If you're insured, you may pay a $40–$75 copay, but if you haven't met your deductible, you may owe the full visit cost. For a family with multiple urgent care visits per year, the annual total is often surprisingly high.

**Q: Can my DPC physician prescribe medications for conditions they diagnose via telehealth?**
A: Yes, in most cases. Telehealth prescribing for common acute conditions — antibiotics for UTIs or ear infections, antivirals, common acute medications — is well within the scope of what DPC physicians can do remotely. Controlled substances have more restrictions. Your DPC physician will let you know when an in-person visit is needed.

**Q: Do DPC practices have the same diagnostic equipment as urgent care centers?**
A: Many DPC practices have in-office rapid testing (strep, flu, COVID, urinalysis) and perform minor procedures. They typically don't have on-site imaging. If an X-ray or more advanced testing is needed, your DPC physician can order it and send you to the appropriate facility — often with their guidance on how to use your insurance coverage most effectively.

**Q: What if I need urgent care on a weekend when my DPC doctor isn't available?**
A: Most DPC practices offer some level of after-hours access — whether by direct text, a phone message line that reaches the physician, or shared coverage with a partner. The quality of after-hours access varies by practice, so it's worth asking before you enroll. When after-hours contact isn't available and your condition can't wait, urgent care is the appropriate next step.
