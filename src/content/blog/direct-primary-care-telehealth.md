## Quick Answer
Telehealth in DPC is fundamentally different from generic telehealth platforms — it's your own physician, who already knows your history, available to you by text, phone, or video as part of your membership with no per-visit fee. It's the convenience of virtual care with the quality of a real relationship.

## Explanation

Telehealth exploded during the pandemic, and many patients discovered that virtual visits could be genuinely useful. But most commercial telehealth platforms have a significant limitation: you're connecting with a random physician who has never met you, doesn't know your history, and will likely never see you again.

That physician follows a protocol. They're cautious because they don't know you. They prescribe conservatively, recommend you follow up with your "regular doctor," and the interaction ends when the video call does.

Telehealth in a DPC practice is something different.

### What Makes DPC Telehealth Different

When you have a video call, phone call, or text exchange with your DPC physician, the conversation begins with a foundation that commercial telehealth can never replicate: your doctor already knows you.

They know your medical history, your medication list, your allergies, your anxiety about certain symptoms, your family history, the chronic condition you've been managing, and the pattern of how you typically present when you're sick. That context changes everything.

A physician who knows you can:

- **Make faster, more confident decisions** — they're not hedging because they lack context
- **Give you a real assessment instead of a defensive one** — they know whether your symptoms fit a concerning pattern or a familiar benign one
- **Follow up** — a DPC telehealth visit doesn't end the relationship. Your physician will check in, review test results, and continue the conversation
- **Integrate the telehealth visit into your ongoing care** — what happened during your telehealth visit is part of your record and informs future care

### How DPC Telehealth Access Works

Different DPC practices structure telehealth access differently, but most offer some combination of:

**Text messaging:** For quick questions, photo review of a rash or wound, medication clarifications, or results questions, texting your physician directly is often the fastest path. This isn't about firing off texts at midnight for non-urgent questions — it's about having a professional communication channel that actually works.

**Phone calls:** For more nuanced conversations, a direct call with your physician is faster and more informative than the traditional "request a callback" approach. In most DPC practices, you're reaching your physician directly, not a nurse or answering service.

**Video visits:** Full video appointments are available for clinical assessments that benefit from visual examination — skin conditions, eye issues, observing how a patient looks and moves, conversations requiring nuance. Video works especially well for mental health check-ins, medication management, chronic disease follow-up, and second opinions on whether an in-person visit is needed.

### What Telehealth Handles Well in DPC

Many conditions and situations that generate in-person visits can be handled efficiently via telehealth:

- Upper respiratory infections and flu assessment
- UTI symptoms with prescription (where clinically appropriate)
- Rashes and skin conditions (photo-based assessment)
- Pink eye
- Medication questions, side effects, and refills
- Lab result review and interpretation
- Mental health check-ins and medication management for anxiety and depression
- Post-procedure or post-illness follow-up
- Blood pressure or blood sugar review after home monitoring
- Travel medicine consultations
- Pre-visit preparation for upcoming specialist appointments

### When In-Person Is Still Needed

Telehealth has real limits. Your DPC physician will ask you to come in when:

- A physical examination is clinically necessary (abdominal pain, chest symptoms, joint injuries, anything requiring hands-on assessment)
- Diagnostic testing needs to happen in the office (rapid strep or flu, urinalysis, EKG)
- A concern warrants more careful observation than a video call allows
- Immunizations, wound care, or minor procedures are needed

A good DPC physician will triage this honestly. They'll tell you when they can handle something remotely and when they need to see you. Their goal is efficient, high-quality care — not driving unnecessary in-person visits.

### The Value for Busy Main Line Patients

For patients in Wayne, PA and the surrounding Main Line area, telehealth access through a DPC practice offers something particularly practical: primary care that fits into a busy life. A quick video call during your lunch break for a medication question. A text to your physician about your child's fever on a school morning. A follow-up call after your latest lab results without taking time off work for an office visit.

DPC telehealth doesn't replace in-person care. But it dramatically reduces the friction involved in staying connected with your physician — which leads to better care, more consistent management of chronic conditions, and a healthier relationship with your own healthcare.

## Practical Tips

- **Save your physician's contact information immediately** after enrolling — you don't want to be searching for it when you're sick.
- **Use text for non-urgent questions** — medication questions, photo of a rash, quick follow-up are all well-suited to messaging rather than scheduling a visit.
- **Take photos of skin conditions** in good lighting before your telehealth call — this dramatically improves the quality of a visual assessment.
- **Have your medication list ready** for telehealth calls, including any over-the-counter medications or supplements.
- **Use telehealth proactively for chronic condition management** — blood pressure trending up at home? Don't wait for an appointment. Reach out.
- **Ask your DPC practice about after-hours telehealth availability** so you know what to expect before you need it.
- **Trust your physician's judgment on in-person triage** — if they say come in, come in. If they say this can be handled remotely, trust that too.

## FAQ

**Q: Is DPC telehealth covered by insurance?**
A: In most DPC practices, telehealth visits are included in your membership — there's no separate charge, and nothing is billed to insurance. This is one of the concrete financial advantages of DPC: you don't have to think about cost before reaching out to your doctor.

**Q: Can my DPC physician prescribe medications via telehealth?**
A: Yes, in most cases. Your DPC physician can prescribe medications based on a telehealth assessment for many common conditions. Controlled substances (opioids, benzodiazepines, stimulants) have stricter telehealth prescribing rules under federal law and typically require an in-person evaluation at least initially. Your physician will advise you when an in-person visit is required for prescribing.

**Q: What if I need a telehealth visit but my DPC physician is unavailable?**
A: DPC practices handle coverage differently. Some have partners who cover for vacations and after-hours. Others use an answering service for true emergencies. Ask your specific practice about their coverage model before enrolling — this is an important question. For truly urgent situations when no coverage is available, urgent care or the ER remains appropriate.

**Q: Is DPC telehealth HIPAA compliant?**
A: Yes. DPC practices are required to use HIPAA-compliant communication platforms for telehealth visits. Text messaging may use secure messaging platforms rather than standard SMS. Ask your DPC practice about their specific communication tools — they should be able to explain what's secure and how patient privacy is protected.
