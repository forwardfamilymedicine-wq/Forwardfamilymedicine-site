## Quick Answer
As of January 1, 2026, the Primary Care Enhancement Act clarified that HSA funds may be used for DPC membership fees, removing the previous uncertainty under IRS Notice 2004-23. If you are pairing a DPC membership with a qualifying high-deductible health plan (HDHP), you can now use your HSA to cover your DPC membership fees. Confirm specifics with your plan administrator.

## Explanation

Health savings accounts are one of the best financial tools available for managing healthcare costs. Triple tax benefit: contributions go in pre-tax, money grows tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. If you're pairing a DPC membership with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) — the smart strategy for many people — you're likely eligible to contribute to an HSA.

But the DPC-HSA relationship has a catch that's worth understanding clearly.

### The HSA Eligibility Question for DPC Membership Fees

The IRS determines what counts as a "qualified medical expense" eligible for HSA reimbursement. In general, insurance premiums are not eligible (with some exceptions), while direct medical expenses are. DPC membership fees fall into a gray zone — they're not insurance premiums, but they're also not a fee-for-service medical expense.

The IRS issued guidance (Notice 2004-23) stating that amounts paid to a DPC-type provider that covers multiple services are generally not HSA-qualified expenses. The reasoning: because DPC covers a broad range of primary care services for a flat fee, it functions more like a prepaid health plan than a direct medical expense — and prepaid health plan fees are not HSA-eligible.

**The practical takeaway:** Do not assume your DPC membership fee can be paid with HSA funds without consulting a tax advisor. The mainstream interpretation is that it cannot, and using HSA funds for ineligible expenses creates a tax liability plus penalty.

**Update as of January 1, 2026:** The Primary Care Enhancement Act, which took effect January 1, 2026, explicitly addressed this gray zone by clarifying that DPC membership fees are qualified medical expenses eligible for HSA reimbursement. This is a significant change that resolves the prior ambiguity. If your HDHP and HSA were established before this date, confirm with your plan administrator how this applies to your specific account. Going forward, DPC members on qualifying HDHPs can use HSA funds for their monthly membership fees.

### What IS HSA-Eligible at a DPC Practice

While the membership fee itself is the sticking point, many things you access through a DPC practice are clearly HSA-eligible as direct medical expenses:

- **Lab fees** — if your DPC practice charges separately for labs (rather than including them in membership), those costs are qualified medical expenses
- **Prescription medications** — drugs dispensed by your DPC practice or obtained with a prescription are HSA-eligible
- **Procedures** — any procedure billed separately (wound care, injections, minor surgery) is typically HSA-eligible
- **Imaging** — X-rays or other imaging ordered through your DPC practice and billed separately are eligible
- **Specialist visits** — any specialist visits covered by your health insurance remain HSA-eligible as standard cost-sharing

This is meaningful. If your DPC practice offers wholesale lab pricing and in-office medication dispensing — as many do — you can pay for those services with tax-advantaged HSA dollars, capturing the wholesale pricing savings plus the tax benefit.

### The DPC + HDHP + HSA Strategy

Despite the membership fee limitation, the combined DPC + HDHP + HSA strategy remains excellent for most patients:

**Step 1: Enroll in DPC** for unlimited primary care at a predictable monthly cost — paid from your regular (post-tax) income.

**Step 2: Carry a qualifying HDHP** for catastrophic coverage. HDHPs are the only insurance plans that allow HSA contributions under current law. They also carry the lowest monthly premiums, freeing up cash flow.

**Step 3: Maximize your HSA contribution** each year. In 2026, the contribution limits are $4,300 for individuals and $8,550 for families. This money can cover your deductible if a major health event occurs, pay for HSA-eligible services at your DPC practice, cover dental, vision, mental health, and other qualified expenses, and grow tax-free as an investment account if you choose not to spend it immediately.

**Step 4: Use your DPC membership for primary care** — avoiding the smaller primary care claims that would drain your HSA and your deductible unnecessarily.

The result: your primary care is excellent and accessible (DPC), your catastrophic exposure is covered (HDHP), and you're building a growing tax-advantaged reserve for medical expenses (HSA). It's genuinely a well-designed healthcare financial strategy.

### What Would Change: The AHLCA Act

The American Health Care Affordability Act (and similar legislation) has been introduced in Congress multiple times to explicitly make DPC membership fees HSA-eligible. The legislation has bipartisan support in concept — the argument is straightforward: DPC patients are taking significant primary care costs off the insurance system, and allowing HSA contributions to cover DPC fees would encourage this behavior and expand access to DPC.

Update: The Primary Care Enhancement Act became effective January 1, 2026, resolving the HSA eligibility question for most DPC members. The AHLCA and similar earlier proposals are now moot — the key legislation passed. If you enrolled in DPC before 2026 and want to know whether you can retroactively reimburse 2025 membership fees, consult a tax advisor — the Act is prospective.

### Practical Guidance

- **Do not use HSA funds for your monthly DPC membership fee** without explicit written guidance from a qualified tax advisor confirming eligibility in your specific situation.
- **Do use HSA funds for labs, medications, and procedures** obtained through your DPC practice — these are generally qualified expenses.
- **Keep receipts and documentation** for any DPC-adjacent expenses you pay with HSA funds, in case of audit.
- **Contribute the IRS maximum to your HSA** each year if you're on an HDHP — the triple tax benefit compounds over time and creates meaningful financial protection.
- **Consult a CPA or tax advisor** who understands healthcare expenses before making HSA decisions — the rules are nuanced and the penalties for errors are real.

## Practical Tips

- **Open an HSA the same year you enroll in a qualifying HDHP** — you cannot contribute retroactively, so don't wait.
- **Invest your HSA rather than spending it down** if you can afford to pay current medical expenses out of pocket — the investment growth compounds tax-free indefinitely.
- **Save HSA receipts permanently** — there's no time limit on reimbursing yourself for eligible expenses, meaning you can accumulate receipts and take reimbursements tax-free years later.
- **Don't conflate FSA rules with HSA rules** — flexible spending accounts have different rules and a use-it-or-lose-it feature. HSAs carry over indefinitely and are more flexible.
- **Ask your DPC practice for itemized receipts** for any services that may be HSA-eligible — labs, medications, procedures — so you have proper documentation.
- **Track HSA contributions against the annual limit** — excess contributions are subject to tax and penalty.
- **Stay informed on legislative changes** — the DPC-HSA legislation landscape is evolving, and what's not eligible today may become eligible in the future.

## FAQ

**Q: Why can't DPC membership fees be paid with an HSA?**
A: Under IRS Notice 2004-23 and related guidance, DPC membership fees are generally not considered "qualified medical expenses" because they function more like a prepaid health plan than a direct fee for a specific medical service. The IRS draws a distinction between paying for a specific medical service and paying for the right to access future services — and DPC memberships are classified as the latter.

**Q: Can I use a Flexible Spending Account (FSA) for DPC membership fees?**
A: The same basic analysis applies to FSAs as to HSAs — DPC membership fees are generally not considered qualified expenses under IRS rules. However, individual FSA plans may have different rules depending on how the plan administrator interprets the guidelines. Check with your FSA plan administrator directly.

**Q: Does having a DPC membership affect my ability to contribute to an HSA?**
A: Having a DPC membership itself does not disqualify you from contributing to an HSA. The key requirement is that you're enrolled in a qualifying high-deductible health plan (HDHP). If you have an HDHP, you can contribute to an HSA regardless of whether you also have a DPC membership. (There was some early debate about whether DPC membership might disqualify HSA eligibility, but regulatory guidance has clarified that DPC membership alone does not affect HSA eligibility.)

**Q: If Congress passes DPC-HSA legislation, will I be able to retroactively reimburse myself for past DPC membership fees?**
A: Probably not — HSA reimbursements are generally limited to expenses incurred after the account was opened and during the year for which the funds were contributed. If legislation changes the eligibility rules, it would likely apply prospectively (going forward), not retroactively. Consult a tax advisor if and when such legislation passes.
