## Quick Answer
The ACC/AHA recommend a fasting lipid panel starting at age 20 — or earlier if you have a family history of heart disease or a genetic cholesterol disorder. How often you retest depends on your results and risk factors, but every 4–6 years is a reasonable baseline for low-risk adults.

## Explanation

Cholesterol is one of the most commonly tested and most commonly misunderstood numbers in medicine. Many patients assume they do not need to worry about it until their 50s or 60s. But cardiovascular disease — the leading cause of death in the United States — builds silently over decades. The cholesterol that matters at 55 started accumulating at 25. Getting a baseline early is not alarmism; it is smart preventive medicine.

**When to start:**

The American College of Cardiology and American Heart Association recommend a fasting lipid panel beginning at age 20, and repeated at least every 4–6 years in low-risk adults. If you have a family history of premature cardiovascular disease (heart attack in a male first-degree relative before age 55, or female before age 65), or if you have been diagnosed with familial hypercholesterolemia, screening should start in childhood — sometimes as early as age 2.

For men over 35 and women over 45, the USPSTF recommends screening for lipid disorders as part of cardiovascular risk assessment, particularly in those with increased risk.

**What a standard lipid panel measures:**

- **Total cholesterol** — a combined measure that includes all lipoprotein fractions. Useful as a broad indicator but not the most clinically precise number.
- **LDL cholesterol** (low-density lipoprotein) — often called "bad" cholesterol. LDL particles deposit in arterial walls and drive atherosclerotic plaque formation. Lowering LDL is the primary target of most statin therapy.
- **HDL cholesterol** (high-density lipoprotein) — often called "good" cholesterol. HDL helps transport cholesterol away from arterial walls. Low HDL (under 40 mg/dL in men, under 50 mg/dL in women) is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease.
- **Triglycerides** — a form of fat in the blood that rises with high sugar and refined carbohydrate intake, alcohol, and certain metabolic conditions. Elevated triglycerides (above 150 mg/dL) are associated with increased cardiovascular and metabolic risk.

**Beyond the standard panel — advanced markers:**

The standard lipid panel captures a lot, but two additional markers add meaningful clinical value for some patients:

- **ApoB (apolipoprotein B)** — each LDL particle carries exactly one ApoB molecule, making ApoB a direct count of atherogenic lipoprotein particles. Some patients have "normal" LDL but elevated ApoB (meaning more particles, each carrying less cholesterol per particle) — and those patients carry higher risk than their LDL alone suggests. Growing evidence supports ApoB as a superior risk marker compared to LDL-C alone.
- **Lp(a) (lipoprotein(a))** — a genetically determined lipoprotein that is an independent risk factor for heart attack, stroke, and aortic stenosis. About 20% of the population has elevated Lp(a). Unlike LDL, lifestyle changes do not meaningfully lower Lp(a). Knowing your Lp(a) level — ideally measured once in adulthood — helps stratify your lifetime cardiovascular risk accurately. Specific Lp(a)-lowering therapies are in late-stage clinical trials.

**How often to retest:**

For low-risk adults with normal initial results: every 4–6 years.

More frequent testing (every 1–2 years, or more often) is appropriate if:
- Your baseline numbers are borderline elevated
- You have other cardiovascular risk factors (diabetes, hypertension, smoking, obesity)
- You have started or changed a statin or other lipid-lowering medication
- You have a strong family history of early cardiovascular disease
- You have undergone significant lifestyle changes (dietary overhaul, major weight loss or gain)

**When medication is indicated:**

Not everyone with elevated LDL needs a statin. The decision involves calculating your 10-year ASCVD (atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease) risk using your full risk profile: age, sex, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking status, and diabetes. A 10-year ASCVD risk above 7.5% generally supports a discussion about statin therapy. In certain high-risk patients — those with established cardiovascular disease, familial hypercholesterolemia, or very high LDL (above 190 mg/dL) — medication is typically appropriate regardless of calculated risk.

Lifestyle modifications — dietary changes (reduced saturated fat, increased fiber), regular aerobic exercise, weight management, limiting alcohol — remain the foundation of treatment at every level of risk.

## Practical Tips

- Get your first lipid panel at age 20 — do not wait until your 40s to establish a baseline
- Fast for 9–12 hours before a lipid panel for the most accurate triglyceride measurement (though non-fasting panels are acceptable for total cholesterol and HDL)
- Ask your physician about ApoB if your LDL appears normal but you have metabolic risk factors like insulin resistance, obesity, or a family history of early heart disease
- Get Lp(a) measured at least once in your adult life — it is a fixed genetic risk factor and knowing it changes how aggressively you manage other risks
- Do not ignore high triglycerides — they often reflect excess sugar and refined carbohydrate intake, and cutting back on those has rapid effects
- If you are prescribed a statin, take it consistently; the benefit compounds over years, not weeks
- Know your 10-year ASCVD risk score — ask your physician to calculate it so you understand the actual context of your cholesterol numbers

## FAQ

**Q: My total cholesterol is 210. Should I be worried?**
A: Not necessarily. Total cholesterol alone is a crude risk indicator. What matters more is the breakdown: your LDL, HDL, and triglycerides, along with your overall cardiovascular risk profile. A total cholesterol of 210 with high HDL and no other risk factors carries very different implications than 210 with low HDL, elevated LDL, and diabetes. Context is everything.

**Q: Can I lower my cholesterol through diet alone?**
A: For many patients, dietary changes can meaningfully reduce LDL — typically by 10–20%. A diet lower in saturated fat, higher in soluble fiber (oats, beans, fruits, vegetables), and incorporating plant sterols can all help. However, if your LDL is very high (particularly if you have familial hypercholesterolemia), lifestyle changes alone are rarely sufficient and medication is appropriate. Your physician can help you understand what is realistic for your situation.

**Q: Are statins safe for long-term use?**
A: Yes, for the vast majority of patients. Statins are among the most studied medications in history, with decades of large randomized trial data. The most common side effect is muscle aching, which occurs in a minority of patients and usually resolves with dose adjustment or switching to a different statin. The serious side effects (severe muscle breakdown, significant liver injury) are rare. For patients at elevated cardiovascular risk, the benefit of statin therapy substantially outweighs the risks.

**Q: My parents both had high cholesterol. Does that mean I will too?**
A: Genetics play a meaningful role in cholesterol levels — familial hypercholesterolemia is a specific inherited condition that causes very high LDL from birth and significantly elevates heart disease risk if untreated. But even without a formal diagnosis, a family history of high cholesterol or early heart disease is a strong reason to start screening early and monitor more frequently. It is not destiny, but it is information worth acting on.
