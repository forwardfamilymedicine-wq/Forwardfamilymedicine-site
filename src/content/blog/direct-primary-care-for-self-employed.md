## Quick Answer
For freelancers, 1099 workers, and small business owners, DPC paired with a high-deductible health plan delivers excellent primary care at a predictable monthly cost — often cheaper than ACA premiums alone, without the gaps in access that come with high-deductible coverage.

## Explanation

If you're self-employed, healthcare is probably one of the most frustrating parts of your financial life. You're paying full ACA marketplace premiums out of pocket. Your deductible may be $3,000, $5,000, or more. You're essentially self-insuring for most routine care — and when you actually need to see a doctor, the process is slow, inconvenient, and expensive.

This is the healthcare paradox of the self-employed: you pay a lot for coverage that doesn't serve you well for everyday needs.

Direct primary care doesn't solve everything, but it solves the part of the healthcare system that you interact with most: your relationship with a primary care doctor.

### The Problem with ACA Plans for 1099 Workers

The Affordable Care Act marketplace was a meaningful improvement for people who had no coverage at all. But for self-employed individuals earning a decent income, ACA plans often mean paying substantial premiums for plans with high deductibles — meaning you pay out of pocket for most primary care anyway.

The math goes something like this: you pay $400–$800/month in premiums (depending on your age, location, and income). You have a $4,000 deductible. Your primary care visit costs $150–$200 out of pocket until you meet that deductible — which may never happen in a healthy year. You're paying a lot for catastrophic protection and getting very little in return for routine care.

### How the DPC + HDHP Strategy Changes the Math

The smarter approach for many self-employed individuals:

1. **Enroll in DPC** for primary care. Pay a flat monthly membership fee — visit our [membership page](/membership) for current pricing at Forward Family Medicine. Get unlimited primary care, same-day access, telehealth, and often wholesale labs and medications.

2. **Pair with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP)** for catastrophic coverage. HDHPs carry the lowest monthly premiums of any ACA-compliant plan. You're protected against major events — hospitalization, surgery, specialist care — without paying for benefits you're not using at the primary care level.

3. **Open a health savings account (HSA)** to shelter pre-tax dollars for medical expenses. Following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP — meaning your monthly membership fee can be paid with pre-tax HSA dollars. Labs, medications, and procedures continue to be HSA-eligible as before.

For many self-employed individuals, the total annual spend — DPC membership + HDHP premium — is less than what they were paying for a mid-tier ACA plan while also receiving dramatically better primary care.

### The Hidden Cost of Not Having Good Primary Care Access

There's another dimension to this that goes beyond premiums: what does poor healthcare access actually cost you?

When you're self-employed, your time is money. Spending two hours at urgent care for a sinus infection because you can't get a same-day appointment with your doctor costs you in lost productivity, not just a $200 urgent care bill. Letting a manageable chronic condition drift because follow-up appointments are too hard to get means bigger, more expensive problems down the road.

DPC membership gives you something genuinely different: a doctor you can text. One who knows your health history and will respond without requiring you to schedule an appointment three weeks out. One who can handle a quick question in five minutes instead of requiring a full visit.

For a self-employed person whose time is genuinely valuable, that access is worth real money.

### What to Do About Coverage Gaps

One concern self-employed people sometimes raise: what if something major happens while I'm on an HDHP? The deductible feels scary.

The answer is your HSA. If you're contributing the IRS maximum to your HSA each year ($4,300 for individuals / $8,550 for families in 2026), you're building a tax-advantaged reserve that can cover your deductible if needed. Over a few years, a well-funded HSA gives you meaningful protection against deductible exposure while also growing tax-free.

Combined with DPC handling your routine care, the result is a healthcare strategy that's both financially smart and genuinely good for your health.

## Practical Tips

- **Run the numbers** — add up what you paid last year in premiums, copays, deductibles, and urgent care before assuming DPC is more expensive.
- **Open an HSA if you're on an HDHP** — the triple tax benefit (contributions pre-tax, growth tax-free, withdrawals tax-free for medical) is the best healthcare tax strategy available to self-employed individuals. (Note: Under the Primary Care Enhancement Act effective January 1, 2026, DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible — confirm with your plan administrator.)
- **Maximize your primary care usage** — if you're paying a monthly fee, use it. Annual physical, chronic condition follow-ups, mental health check-ins, telehealth questions — all included.
- **Factor in time** — urgent care waits, scheduling delays, and missed productivity all have real dollar values when you're self-employed.
- **Consider group DPC arrangements** — if you own a small business with employees, some DPC practices offer group pricing that can be a cost-effective benefit.
- **Don't wait until you're sick** — the real value of DPC is building a relationship with a physician who knows you. Enroll before you need it.
- **Check if your DPC practice offers medication dispensing** — wholesale drug pricing at a DPC practice can be a significant savings for common chronic condition medications.

## FAQ

**Q: Is DPC membership tax-deductible if I'm self-employed?**
A: This is a nuanced question. Self-employed individuals can generally deduct health insurance premiums, but the IRS has not clearly ruled that DPC membership fees qualify as a deductible healthcare expense in the same category. Some tax advisors treat DPC as a deductible medical expense; others are more conservative. Consult a CPA or tax advisor familiar with healthcare for the most current guidance.

**Q: Can I enroll in DPC without any health insurance at all?**
A: Technically yes — there is no legal requirement to carry health insurance alongside a DPC membership. However, it is strongly discouraged. DPC covers primary care only. Without insurance, a single hospitalization, emergency, or serious diagnosis could generate catastrophic debt. DPC is a complement to insurance, not a replacement for it.

**Q: What if I have a slow month and can't afford both DPC and my HDHP premium?**
A: This is a real concern for variable-income earners. Most DPC practices allow month-to-month membership with no long-term contract, though some offer modest discounts for annual payment. Your HDHP is the non-negotiable piece — keep that coverage. If you need to pause DPC temporarily during a lean stretch, most practices will allow you to re-enroll without penalty.

**Q: Does DPC help with specialist referrals and navigating the healthcare system?**
A: Absolutely — and this is one of the less-discussed benefits. A good DPC physician acts as a healthcare navigator for you. When you need a specialist, they help identify the right one, send a proper referral with your history, and follow up on the results. For self-employed individuals without HR departments to call for guidance, having a knowledgeable physician in your corner navigating the system is genuinely valuable.
