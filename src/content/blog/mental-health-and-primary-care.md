## Quick Answer
Most mental health care in the United States is delivered by primary care physicians, not psychiatrists. A strong, trusting relationship with your primary care doctor can be one of the most important assets in managing your mental health — but only if your doctor has the time to do it well.

## Explanation

There is a widespread assumption that mental health care belongs in a psychiatrist's office or a therapist's practice, and that primary care is for the physical body. The reality is more integrated and more complicated. Primary care physicians diagnose and treat the large majority of depression and anxiety in this country, initiate most psychiatric medications, and provide the ongoing monitoring that long-term mental health management requires.

This isn't a workaround or a stopgap. For many patients with mild to moderate mental health conditions, primary care is the right first stop — and for some, the right ongoing home for their mental health care. Understanding what primary care can appropriately offer, and where its limits are, helps you use the healthcare system more effectively.

**What primary care can appropriately manage**

Primary care physicians are trained to diagnose and treat:

- Mild to moderate depression
- Generalized anxiety disorder
- Panic disorder
- Adjustment disorders (stress reactions to life events)
- Mild to moderate PTSD symptoms
- Attention-deficit disorder (ADHD) in adults and children, depending on the practice
- Sleep disorders related to anxiety or mood

Treatment typically includes therapy referrals, medication initiation and monitoring, lifestyle medicine approaches (exercise, sleep hygiene, nutritional support), and ongoing follow-up. Validated screening tools — the PHQ-9 for depression, the GAD-7 for anxiety — are used in primary care settings to assess severity and track progress over time.

**The problem with traditional primary care for mental health**

Here's where the system falls short: mental health conditions require time, trust, and follow-up. A productive conversation about anxiety or depression can't happen in seven minutes. The nuances of how symptoms are affecting your relationships, your work, your sleep, your sense of self — these take longer to explore. And the follow-up matters enormously: medication management for depression typically requires closer monitoring in the first weeks, with adjustments based on response and side effects.

In a traditional fee-for-service primary care setting, physicians are under significant time pressure. Mental health concerns are frequently acknowledged but undertreated — not because the doctor doesn't care, but because the structural conditions don't allow for adequate depth. Patients often feel dismissed or rushed, which compounds the problem.

**The role of the therapeutic relationship**

One of the most consistent findings in mental health research is that the quality of the therapeutic relationship — the sense of being known, heard, and trusted by your provider — significantly predicts treatment outcomes. This is true for therapy, and it's also true in primary care mental health management.

A physician who knows you well, who understands your history, your stressors, your family context, and who you've built a relationship with over time, is better positioned to support your mental health than a provider you've met once. Continuity is therapeutic.

**When specialist referral is appropriate**

Primary care has limits that are important to know. Referral to a psychiatrist or specialist is appropriate for:

- Bipolar disorder (mood stabilizers and complex medication management require specialist expertise)
- Schizophrenia and psychotic disorders
- Severe, treatment-resistant depression
- Active suicidal ideation or self-harm requiring intensive support
- Eating disorders
- Complex PTSD
- When medication trials in primary care haven't achieved adequate response

Referral to a therapist or psychologist is appropriate for almost all mental health conditions alongside, or instead of, medication. Therapy — particularly cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — has strong evidence for depression and anxiety and is often more effective than medication alone for mild to moderate presentations.

## Practical Tips

- Tell your primary care doctor about mental health symptoms. Many patients feel uncomfortable raising mental health concerns with their "physical" doctor. Your physician is trained to address these and genuinely needs to know.
- If you've been prescribed an antidepressant or anxiolytic, follow up within two to four weeks of starting — not three months. Early follow-up allows for timely adjustments.
- Therapy and medication work better together than either does alone for most presentations of depression and anxiety. Ask for a therapy referral alongside medication.
- Regular aerobic exercise has the strongest evidence base of any lifestyle intervention for mild to moderate depression. This isn't a platitude — it's clinical data.
- Sleep quality and mental health are deeply intertwined. Poor sleep worsens anxiety and depression; anxiety and depression worsen sleep. Addressing sleep is often a meaningful part of mental health treatment.
- If you're not improving on a medication after six to eight weeks at an adequate dose, that's important information. Tell your doctor — don't just continue indefinitely.
- Crisis resources are always available: 988 is the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24/7 by call or text.

## FAQ

**Q: Can my primary care doctor prescribe antidepressants?**
A: Yes. Primary care physicians prescribe the large majority of antidepressants and many anti-anxiety medications in the United States. They are trained to initiate, monitor, and adjust these medications. More complex cases — severe presentations, unusual medication responses, bipolar disorder — typically benefit from psychiatric involvement.

**Q: Do I need a referral to see a therapist?**
A: Usually not. Therapists, psychologists, and licensed clinical social workers generally don't require a physician referral. However, your insurance may require a referral for coverage, and your primary care physician can help connect you with appropriate resources and make a formal referral if needed.

**Q: I'm worried my doctor will just prescribe medication without listening to me. What should I say?**
A: Be direct: "I want to talk through what's going on before we decide on treatment." A good primary care physician will welcome this. If medication is ultimately considered, ask what the alternatives are, what to expect, and what the plan is for follow-up and re-evaluation.

**Q: What if I've tried several medications and nothing has worked?**
A: This is called treatment-resistant depression and warrants psychiatric referral. Options including different medication classes, combination strategies, and interventional treatments like TMS (transcranial magnetic stimulation), esketamine (Spravato, FDA-approved for treatment-resistant depression), and IV ketamine (used off-label in specialist settings) are better managed by a psychiatrist with subspecialty expertise in treatment-resistant cases.
