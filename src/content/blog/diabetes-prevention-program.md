## Quick Answer
The National Diabetes Prevention Program (NDPP) is a CDC-recognized, evidence-based lifestyle change program proven to reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes by 58% in people with prediabetes — and by 71% in adults over 60. If you have prediabetes or are at elevated risk, this program is one of the most powerful interventions available.

## Explanation

Type 2 diabetes is not inevitable. For the roughly 98 million American adults with prediabetes — about 1 in 3 — the trajectory toward diabetes is not fixed. It can be interrupted. The National Diabetes Prevention Program (NDPP) is the most rigorously studied lifestyle intervention we have for doing exactly that.

**The evidence behind the program:**

The NDPP grew out of the landmark Diabetes Prevention Program (DPP) clinical trial, funded by the NIH and published in the New England Journal of Medicine in 2002. The trial enrolled over 3,000 adults with prediabetes and randomly assigned them to three groups: intensive lifestyle intervention, metformin (a diabetes medication), or placebo.

Results after an average of 2.8 years were striking:
- The lifestyle intervention group reduced their risk of developing type 2 diabetes by **58%** compared to placebo
- The metformin group reduced risk by **31%** compared to placebo
- In adults aged 60 and older, the lifestyle intervention reduced risk by **71%**

The lifestyle program was more effective than medication — and the benefits persisted in long-term follow-up studies. This is not a marginal improvement; it is a profound one.

**What the NDPP program actually involves:**

The CDC-recognized NDPP is a structured 12-month lifestyle change program delivered by trained lifestyle coaches. It consists of:
- **16 weekly core sessions** (approximately the first 6 months) focused on healthy eating, physical activity, stress management, and problem-solving
- **6 or more monthly follow-up sessions** (the second 6 months) to reinforce and maintain changes

The two primary goals of the program:
1. Achieve and maintain at least 5–7% body weight loss
2. Accumulate at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity physical activity

Programs are available in-person, online, and through a combination of both — making it accessible even for people with demanding schedules. Many employers and health insurers now cover CDC-recognized NDPP programs. Medicare covers the program for eligible beneficiaries (those with a positive prediabetes screening and a BMI of 25 or higher, or 23 or higher for Asian Americans).

**Who qualifies:**

You are eligible for the NDPP if you have:
- Prediabetes, defined as an HbA1c of 5.7–6.4%, a fasting plasma glucose of 100–125 mg/dL, or a 2-hour plasma glucose of 140–199 mg/dL on an oral glucose tolerance test
- A BMI of 25 or higher (23 or higher if you are of Asian descent)
- No previous diagnosis of type 2 diabetes
- Not been pregnant in the past year (gestational diabetes history actually qualifies you — it is a significant risk factor)

Even if you do not strictly meet the above criteria, you may benefit from a similar lifestyle intervention if you have multiple metabolic risk factors. Your physician can help assess your individual situation.

**Why the 5–7% weight loss goal matters:**

The NDPP's weight loss goal — modest by most standards — is specifically calibrated to reduce insulin resistance and improve beta cell function in the pancreas. You do not need to lose 50 pounds. A 150-pound person losing 8–10 pounds, combined with consistent physical activity, produces meaningful metabolic change. The program is not about aesthetics; it is about restoring insulin sensitivity.

**What happens if prediabetes is not addressed:**

Without intervention, approximately 15–30% of people with prediabetes will develop type 2 diabetes within 5 years. Type 2 diabetes substantially increases the risk of heart disease, stroke, kidney failure, nerve damage, and blindness — all largely preventable with early intervention. Prediabetes itself is associated with increased cardiovascular risk, independent of whether diabetes develops.

## Practical Tips

- Ask your physician to check your HbA1c or fasting glucose if you have not had one recently — prediabetes has no symptoms and is frequently undiagnosed
- If you qualify for NDPP, look for a CDC-recognized program through the CDC's online directory at prevent.diabetes.org
- The 150 minutes of weekly activity goal breaks down to about 30 minutes, 5 days per week — brisk walking counts
- Focusing on the first 5–7% of body weight loss has the biggest metabolic payoff; do not let perfect be the enemy of good
- Sleep deprivation and chronic stress both increase insulin resistance — the NDPP lifestyle sessions address these, not just diet and exercise
- If your employer offers wellness programs, ask whether they cover an NDPP-recognized program
- Gestational diabetes is a strong predictor of future type 2 diabetes — if you had it, prioritize screening and risk reduction even if your postpartum glucose was normal

## FAQ

**Q: I was told I have "borderline" diabetes. Is that the same as prediabetes?**
A: Yes — "borderline diabetes," "impaired fasting glucose," and "impaired glucose tolerance" are all terms that describe prediabetes. Prediabetes is defined by specific lab thresholds: HbA1c 5.7–6.4%, fasting glucose 100–125 mg/dL, or a 2-hour glucose of 140–199 mg/dL. If any of these apply to you, you likely qualify for the NDPP and would benefit significantly from lifestyle intervention.

**Q: Is the Diabetes Prevention Program the same as a diet program?**
A: It incorporates dietary guidance, but it is much more comprehensive than a diet program. The NDPP addresses physical activity, stress management, sleep, problem-solving, and behavior change strategies. The lifestyle coach model — trained, evidence-based, structured — is what distinguishes it from general wellness advice. The curriculum was developed through NIH-funded research and is consistently delivered by certified coaches.

**Q: My A1c is 5.6%. Should I be concerned?**
A: An HbA1c of 5.6% is at the upper edge of normal (prediabetes begins at 5.7%). While 5.6% is not technically prediabetes, it is worth monitoring closely — especially if you have other risk factors like obesity, family history of diabetes, or elevated triglycerides. Annual repeat testing and proactive lifestyle attention are appropriate. A conversation with your physician can put your specific result in context.

**Q: Can metformin replace the lifestyle program?**
A: Metformin is an option, but the DPP trial showed it was roughly half as effective as the lifestyle intervention (31% vs. 58% risk reduction). The lifestyle program also produces additional benefits — cardiovascular risk reduction, improved energy, better sleep — that medication does not replicate. Some physicians recommend metformin in addition to lifestyle intervention for higher-risk patients, but it is not a substitute for the program.
