## Quick Answer
Men die younger than women in the United States — and a large part of that gap is explained by preventable and detectable disease that goes unaddressed because men avoid primary care. The screenings and habits outlined here are the highest-value preventive investments for men at every age.

## Explanation

The statistics are difficult to ignore. Men are about 50% less likely than women to have a regular primary care physician. They are less likely to seek care when symptoms arise, less likely to follow through on recommended screenings, and more likely to arrive at a physician's office with a condition that has been silently progressing for years. The result is a meaningful and measurable gap in life expectancy: American men die, on average, about 5 years earlier than women.

Some of this disparity is biological. Much of it is behavioral and addressable.

**Blood pressure:**

Hypertension affects about 1 in 2 American adults, but men are more likely to develop it earlier — particularly before age 55. High blood pressure produces no symptoms and damages blood vessels, the heart, kidneys, and brain silently over years. The USPSTF recommends screening all adults aged 18 and older. If yours has not been checked in the past year, that is where to start.

**Cholesterol and lipid panel:**

The ACC/AHA recommend a baseline lipid panel at age 20, with repeat testing every 4–6 years in average-risk adults and more frequently if borderline results or risk factors are present. Men have a higher lifetime risk of cardiovascular disease and tend to develop it earlier than women. Knowing your LDL, HDL, triglycerides, and ideally ApoB gives you and your physician the data to make informed decisions about risk reduction.

**Diabetes screening:**

The USPSTF recommends screening for prediabetes and type 2 diabetes in overweight and obese adults aged 35–70. Men with a BMI of 25 or higher, particularly those who carry weight around the abdomen, are at elevated metabolic risk. Fasting glucose and HbA1c are both appropriate screening tests.

**Colorectal cancer:**

The ACS and USPSTF now recommend starting colorectal cancer screening at age 45 for average-risk adults — not 50, which was the older recommendation. Options include colonoscopy (every 10 years if normal), Cologuard stool DNA test, and annual FIT test. Men with a family history of colorectal cancer should start earlier.

**Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) ultrasound:**

This is a screening specific to men. The USPSTF recommends a one-time abdominal ultrasound screening for AAA in men aged 65–75 who have ever smoked (defined as at least 100 cigarettes in their lifetime). An abdominal aortic aneurysm — a dangerous bulging of the main abdominal artery — typically causes no symptoms before rupture, which is often fatal. A one-time ultrasound screen takes 15 minutes and can be lifesaving.

**Prostate cancer and PSA:**

PSA (prostate-specific antigen) testing remains one of the more nuanced conversations in men's preventive health. The USPSTF recommends a shared decision-making conversation about PSA screening in men aged 55–69, acknowledging both potential benefits (early detection) and harms (false positives, overdiagnosis, and treatment side effects). Men with a family history of prostate cancer, those of African ancestry, and those with BRCA2 mutations may benefit from earlier discussion — starting at age 40–45. This is not a test to order without a real conversation about what a positive result would mean for you.

**Testosterone: real concern vs. normal aging:**

Low testosterone (hypogonadism) is a real clinical diagnosis. Symptoms include persistent fatigue beyond what is explained by lifestyle, significantly reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, loss of muscle mass, decreased bone density, and depression. However, testosterone levels also decline naturally with age — roughly 1–2% per year after 30 — and many men with "low" testosterone on a lab test have no symptoms and do not benefit from treatment.

Testing should be driven by symptoms, not routine screening. If you have symptoms suggestive of hypogonadism, a morning total testosterone (when levels are highest) is the appropriate first test. Treatment decisions are based on both the number and the clinical picture, and should involve a real discussion about the risks and benefits of testosterone therapy.

**Depression screening:**

The USPSTF recommends screening all adults for depression. Men are significantly less likely than women to be diagnosed with or treated for depression, in part because they are less likely to report emotional symptoms and more likely to present with behavioral equivalents — increased irritability, anger, alcohol use, risk-taking. Depression in men is underdiagnosed and undertreated, and it is a major contributor to the high rates of suicide in men (which occur at nearly 4 times the rate of women in the US). A brief depression screen at your annual physical is not a sign of weakness; it is standard preventive medicine.

## Practical Tips

- Schedule an annual physical even when you feel well — many of the conditions that kill men prematurely have no early symptoms
- Know your four cardiovascular numbers: blood pressure, LDL cholesterol, fasting glucose, and BMI — these are the foundation of your heart disease risk
- If you are between 65 and 75 and have ever smoked, ask your physician about a one-time abdominal aortic aneurysm ultrasound
- Talk honestly about alcohol — men are significantly more likely to drink at hazardous levels, and alcohol interacts with virtually every other health condition
- Do not wait for symptoms to decide testosterone is low; get tested if you have symptoms, and have a real conversation about what the results mean
- The men most likely to benefit from preventive care are often the ones least likely to seek it — if you know someone who fits this description, this article is for them too
- Sleep matters for cardiovascular health, metabolic function, mental health, and testosterone levels — it is not optional

## FAQ

**Q: I'm 40 and feel fine. What labs should I actually be getting?**
A: At minimum: a lipid panel, fasting glucose or HbA1c, comprehensive metabolic panel (kidney and liver function), CBC, and a blood pressure check. If you smoke or have significant cardiovascular risk factors, additional tests may be appropriate. Depending on your symptoms and history, testosterone and thyroid function (TSH) may also be worth checking. Your physician can tailor this based on your specific history.

**Q: My doctor mentioned PSA screening. Should I do it?**
A: PSA testing is a shared decision, not a routine recommendation like a cholesterol check. The USPSTF recommends having a genuine discussion about the benefits and potential harms (false positives, biopsies, potential overtreatment of low-risk cancers) before deciding to test. Men aged 55–69 are the primary target group. If you have a family history of prostate cancer, are of African descent, or have BRCA mutations, the conversation may be appropriate earlier. Talk to your physician, not just about whether to test, but about what you would do with different results.

**Q: I'm tired all the time. Could it be low testosterone?**
A: Fatigue is one of the most common symptoms men report, and it has a very long list of causes — poor sleep, sleep apnea, depression, thyroid disease, anemia, diabetes, and yes, low testosterone among them. A workup for fatigue should be systematic, starting with the most common causes. If your sleep, mood, and metabolic health are evaluated and those causes have been addressed or excluded, then measuring testosterone is appropriate. A single low testosterone result also needs to be confirmed with a repeat morning draw before making treatment decisions.

**Q: How often should I actually see a physician if I'm healthy?**
A: For most healthy men under 50, a comprehensive annual physical is the foundation. Between physicals, you may go months without needing a visit — though the ability to message or call with questions is valuable. Over 50, or if you have any ongoing chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, elevated cholesterol), more frequent contact for monitoring and medication management is appropriate. The key is having an established relationship so that when you do need care, you are not starting from scratch.
