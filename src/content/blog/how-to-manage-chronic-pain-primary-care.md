## Quick Answer
Chronic pain — pain lasting more than three months — affects roughly 20 percent of American adults and is best managed through a multimodal approach that addresses the underlying drivers, not just the symptoms. Primary care, particularly in a direct primary care setting, is well-positioned to be the coordinating center of that approach.

## Explanation

Chronic pain is one of the most prevalent and most misunderstood conditions in medicine. It affects an estimated 50 million Americans, is the leading cause of disability, and is one of the primary reasons people seek medical care. Yet it remains among the most inadequately managed conditions in the healthcare system.

Part of the problem is a historical tendency to approach pain as a symptom to suppress rather than a condition to understand. Prescribe enough medication and the patient can function. But chronic pain is not simply acute pain that persists — it involves complex changes in the nervous system, inflammatory pathways, psychological states, and physical deconditioning that all interact with and amplify each other.

**Understanding chronic pain differently**

Chronic pain has three main biological drivers that effective treatment must address:

**Inflammation.** Systemic low-grade inflammation — driven by diet, poor sleep, excess body fat, stress, and sedentary behavior — is a significant contributor to many chronic pain conditions including fibromyalgia, back pain, and joint pain. Anti-inflammatory approaches to lifestyle are clinically meaningful, not just complementary.

**Central sensitization.** When pain persists for months, the nervous system can become dysregulated — a process called central sensitization. The pain alarm system starts firing more easily and more intensely, meaning the threshold for experiencing pain is lowered even when peripheral tissue damage is minimal. This is why chronic pain often responds poorly to purely structural interventions (surgery, injections) that address tissue damage without addressing the sensitized nervous system.

**Deconditioning.** Pain leads to avoidance of activity, which leads to muscle atrophy, reduced cardiovascular fitness, and weight gain — all of which increase pain. This cycle is one of the most important patterns to interrupt in chronic pain management.

**The multimodal approach**

Effective chronic pain management draws from multiple evidence-based modalities:

- **Physical therapy and movement.** Exercise is among the most effective interventions for most chronic pain conditions. Supervised physical therapy helps rebuild strength, improve mechanics, and break the deconditioning cycle. Gentle, consistent movement — not rest — is the principle.
- **Anti-inflammatory nutrition.** Mediterranean-pattern diets, which emphasize vegetables, legumes, fish, olive oil, and whole grains, have demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects. Reducing ultra-processed foods, refined sugars, and alcohol has meaningful effects on systemic inflammation.
- **Sleep optimization.** Pain disrupts sleep; poor sleep amplifies pain. Addressing sleep hygiene, sleep disorders, and the sleep-pain cycle is often one of the highest-yield interventions in chronic pain management.
- **Stress reduction and mental health.** Chronic stress perpetuates the inflammatory state and the central sensitization that drives chronic pain. Mind-body approaches — cognitive behavioral therapy for pain (CBT-P), mindfulness-based stress reduction, and psychological support — have strong evidence in chronic pain literature.
- **Appropriate medication.** Medications for chronic pain should complement, not replace, the above approaches. NSAIDs, duloxetine, gabapentinoids, and topical agents all have appropriate roles in specific conditions. The goal is the minimum effective medication burden alongside active lifestyle and behavioral intervention.

**The limitations of opioid-first approaches**

Opioids remain appropriate for specific acute pain situations and some end-of-life or cancer pain contexts. Their role in managing chronic non-cancer pain is limited and complex. Long-term opioid therapy does not consistently outperform other approaches for most chronic pain conditions, and carries significant risks of dependence, hyperalgesia (paradoxical increased pain sensitivity), hormonal disruption, and overdose. Guidelines across specialties now emphasize non-opioid, multimodal approaches as the foundation of chronic pain management.

This is not a dismissal of patients in pain who have used opioids or currently need them — it's a recognition that the conversation should center on function, quality of life, and addressing underlying drivers, with opioids as one tool among many, not the primary strategy.

Chronic pain management requires exactly what [direct primary care](/direct-primary-care) is designed to provide: time, continuity, and coordination. Managing a patient with chronic pain isn't a single appointment — it's an ongoing clinical relationship in which the physician tracks what's working, adjusts the approach when it isn't, coordinates physical therapy and specialist referrals, manages medications safely over time, and helps the patient stay engaged in their own care through the difficult periods.

## Practical Tips

- Movement is medicine for most chronic pain conditions. Walking, swimming, cycling, and low-impact strength training reduce pain over time — even when starting is difficult. Start small and build gradually.
- Keep a pain diary that includes sleep quality, stress levels, and food — not just pain scores. This often reveals patterns that inform treatment decisions.
- Request a referral to a physical therapist before requesting stronger medications. PT has strong evidence for back pain, osteoarthritis, and many other common pain conditions.
- Ask your doctor about the sleep-pain connection. Improving sleep quality often produces noticeable reductions in pain and fatigue.
- Reduce ultra-processed food and added sugar consumption. Even modest dietary improvements can reduce systemic inflammation over weeks to months.
- Psychological support for chronic pain is not a suggestion that your pain is "in your head." CBT for chronic pain and mindfulness-based approaches are evidence-based treatments that work through neurological mechanisms.
- Be honest with your physician about functional goals — not just pain levels. "I want to walk my dog without limping" is more useful clinical information than a number on a pain scale.

## FAQ

**Q: Is it safe to exercise with chronic pain?**
A: For most chronic pain conditions, yes — and it's often one of the best things you can do. The key is working with a physical therapist or knowledgeable physician to identify appropriate activities and intensity. The adage "no pain, no gain" does not apply to chronic pain management. Gentle, consistent, progressive movement is the goal.

**Q: My pain is real but my imaging shows nothing serious. What does that mean?**
A: Imaging findings correlate poorly with pain experience for many conditions, particularly low back pain. Studies consistently show that many people with significant structural findings on MRI have no pain, while many people in significant pain have minimal imaging findings. Pain is a complex neurological experience, not simply tissue damage. This doesn't make your pain any less real — it means the approach to treatment needs to go beyond what imaging shows.

**Q: How long does it take for lifestyle changes to reduce pain?**
A: This varies by condition and individual. Anti-inflammatory dietary changes may produce effects in four to eight weeks. Regular exercise effects on pain typically emerge within weeks of consistent activity. Sleep improvements can affect pain relatively quickly. The realistic timeframe for meaningful improvement through lifestyle approaches is typically weeks to a few months — not days.

**Q: When should chronic pain require specialist referral?**
A: Referral to a pain specialist, physiatrist, rheumatologist, or neurologist is appropriate when the diagnosis is uncertain, when primary care management has been optimized and pain remains uncontrolled, when interventional procedures may be appropriate (injections, nerve blocks), or when complex medication management is needed. Your primary care physician should remain involved as the coordinating physician throughout specialist involvement.
