## Quick Answer
DPC paired with a high-deductible health plan often costs significantly less than a traditional PPO when you account for premiums, copays, deductibles, urgent care visits, and lab costs. The savings are most pronounced for people who actually use primary care — families, people managing chronic conditions, and self-employed individuals paying full premiums.

## Explanation

The question "does direct primary care save money?" doesn't have a single answer — it depends on your specific situation, your insurance options, and how much you use healthcare. But for a large and growing number of patients, the math works out clearly in DPC's favor.

The key is looking at total annual healthcare spend, not just comparing monthly fees in isolation.

### The Real Cost of Traditional Primary Care

Most people underestimate what they actually spend on healthcare because the costs arrive in small pieces across the year. Let's build out a realistic picture for a common scenario: a self-employed individual or family on a traditional PPO.

**Scenario A: Individual on a Mid-Tier PPO**

| Expense | Annual Cost |
|---|---|
| Monthly premium ($450/month) | $5,400 |
| Primary care copays (4 visits × $35) | $140 |
| Urgent care visit (1 visit × $200 out of pocket) | $200 |
| Lab work (2 panels, standard rates) | $200–$400 |
| Specialist copay (1 visit × $60) | $60 |
| **Annual total** | **~$6,000–$6,200** |

This is before anything goes seriously wrong. And notice: the PPO premium is the dominant cost by far. Primary care copays are almost a rounding error compared to what you're paying in premiums.

**Scenario B: Individual with DPC + HDHP**

| Expense | Annual Cost |
|---|---|
| DPC membership: $200/month (Forward Family Medicine individual rate) — see [membership page](/membership) for family pricing | Variable |
| HDHP monthly premium (typically $200–$300 less/month than PPO) | $1,800–$2,400 less |
| Primary care visits | $0 (included in membership) |
| Urgent care visits (significantly reduced with DPC access) | $0–$100 |
| Lab work (wholesale DPC pricing — often 70–90% less) | $30–$100 |
| Specialist care (HDHP applies, same as PPO for major care) | Similar |
| **Annual total** | **Often $1,500–$3,000 less than Scenario A** |

The premium savings from switching to an HDHP are the biggest driver. When you add the elimination of copays, reduced urgent care dependence, and wholesale lab pricing, the gap widens further.

### Where the Savings Come From

Let's look at each category of savings individually.

**1. Insurance Premium Savings**

The single biggest opportunity. The monthly premium difference between a comparable PPO and HDHP often runs $150–$300 per month for an individual, more for a family. Over a year, that's $1,800–$3,600 in premium savings — often enough to cover your entire DPC membership cost and then some.

**2. Eliminated Primary Care Copays**

With DPC, you don't pay a copay for primary care visits. If you're someone who sees your primary care doctor four, six, or eight times per year — especially common for people managing chronic conditions or families with children — $30–$50 copays add up. At six visits per year, you're saving $180–$300 in copays alone.

**3. Reduced Urgent Care Dependence**

Studies show DPC members use urgent care 50–65% less frequently than traditionally insured patients. If you were previously making two or three urgent care visits per year at $150–$250 out of pocket (under an HDHP before meeting your deductible), eliminating those visits saves $300–$750 annually.

**4. Wholesale Lab Pricing**

This is the savings category that most surprises patients. Lab pricing in traditional healthcare is dramatically marked up. A basic metabolic panel that might cost $40–$80 at wholesale rates is billed at $300–$500 to your insurance (of which you may owe your full deductible amount). Many DPC practices offer labs at direct wholesale rates:

These are the actual prices members pay — not estimates. A standard metabolic workup that might cost $400–$800 through insurance billing costs under $20 at wholesale.

For someone requiring routine chronic condition monitoring (quarterly HbA1c checks, annual lipid panels, thyroid monitoring), the lab savings alone can represent $300–$800 per year.

**5. Medication Dispensing Savings**

Many DPC practices dispense common generic medications directly at cost. For patients on maintenance medications — metformin for diabetes, lisinopril for blood pressure, statins for cholesterol, levothyroxine for thyroid — the monthly savings can be substantial:

- Metformin 1000mg (monthly supply): Retail ~$30–$50 | DPC dispensed: often $5–$10
- Lisinopril 10mg (monthly supply): Retail ~$20–$40 | DPC dispensed: often $3–$7
- Atorvastatin 20mg (monthly supply): Retail ~$20–$40 | DPC dispensed: often $5–$12

A patient on two or three maintenance medications could save $50–$150 per month on medications alone — $600–$1,800 per year.

### A Complete Family Comparison

For a family of four currently on a family PPO plan:

**Traditional PPO Family:**
- Family premium: $18,000–$24,000/year (with employer contributing, family share often $6,000–$12,000)
- Primary care copays (8 visits/year × $35): $280
- Urgent care (3 visits/year × $200): $600
- Annual labs: $400
- **Out-of-pocket annual cost (family share): $7,000–$13,000+**

**DPC + Family HDHP:**
- Family DPC membership: see our [membership page](/membership)
- HDHP family premium: typically $3,000–$5,000 less/year than comparable PPO
- Primary care: $0 beyond membership
- Urgent care (significantly reduced): $0–$200
- Wholesale labs: $100–$200
- **Total annual savings versus PPO: often $2,000–$4,000+**

The family scenario shows the most dramatic savings because premium differences and eliminated copays compound across all family members.

### Who Saves the Most

DPC saves money most clearly for:

- **Self-employed individuals and families** paying full ACA market premiums
- **People managing chronic conditions** with frequent primary care visits and regular lab monitoring
- **Families with children** who use primary care frequently
- **Anyone on an HDHP** who was previously paying out of pocket for primary care visits
- **People currently relying on urgent care** due to poor primary care access

DPC offers the smallest financial advantage for someone with a heavily subsidized employer PPO, very low copays, and minimal healthcare utilization. But even in that case, the access and quality improvements may be worth the incremental cost difference.

## Practical Tips

- **Calculate your actual annual healthcare spend** before comparing — pull your insurance statements, track copays, add urgent care visits. The real number often surprises people.
- **Get HDHP premium quotes** before assuming DPC adds to your costs — the premium savings frequently more than cover the membership fee.
- **Ask about lab pricing** at any DPC practice you're considering — wholesale lab pricing is one of the clearest concrete savings and easy to quantify.
- **Ask about medication dispensing** — if you take maintenance medications, compare DPC dispensing prices to your current pharmacy cost.
- **Account for urgent care avoidance** — if you're currently making two or three urgent care visits per year, price those out and subtract them from your DPC cost comparison.
- **Open and fund an HSA** if you move to an HDHP — the triple tax benefit effectively reduces your real healthcare costs by your marginal tax rate. Note: Following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP — meaning your $200/month membership can be paid with pre-tax HSA dollars, adding meaningful tax savings on top of the other financial advantages.
- **Think multi-year** — the savings from DPC compound over time as your DPC physician prevents expensive complications, avoids unnecessary specialist referrals, and keeps you healthier through proactive care.

## FAQ

**Q: Is DPC always cheaper than traditional insurance?**
A: Not always, and it depends on your specific situation. DPC is most clearly cost-advantageous for self-employed individuals, families with children, people managing chronic conditions, and anyone on an HDHP who's been paying out of pocket for primary care. For someone with a heavily subsidized employer PPO and minimal healthcare use, the financial comparison is closer — though access and quality improvements may still make DPC worthwhile.

**Q: What if I join DPC and then have a major medical event that exceeds my HDHP deductible?**
A: Your HDHP covers you for major events. Yes, you'll need to meet your deductible, which is the tradeoff for lower premiums. This is why maintaining your HSA contributions matters — a well-funded HSA can cover your deductible without financial stress. The DPC + HDHP + HSA strategy is designed with this scenario in mind.

**Q: Are DPC lab savings real, or are they just different from insurance-negotiated rates?**
A: The savings are real and significant. DPC practices contract directly with labs at cash-pay wholesale rates that are dramatically lower than the rates charged to insurance (which are then passed on to patients via deductibles and cost-sharing). The markup in standard lab billing is extraordinary — often 10–30x the actual cost of running the test. DPC eliminates that markup for members.

**Q: How do I verify the cost savings before committing to DPC?**
A: Start by calculating your actual current annual spend: premiums (your share), copays, urgent care visits, lab costs (look at your EOBs from last year), and medication costs. Then get the DPC membership cost and an HDHP quote. Build the comparison side by side. A good DPC practice should be willing to help you think through this calculation — if they're not, that tells you something about their transparency.
