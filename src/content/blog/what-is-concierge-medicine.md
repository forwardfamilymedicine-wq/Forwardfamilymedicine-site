## Quick Answer
Concierge medicine is a model of primary care where patients pay a membership fee in exchange for enhanced access to their physician, longer appointments, and a more personal relationship than traditional insurance-based practices can offer.

## Explanation

If you've ever left a doctor's appointment feeling rushed, confused, or like you barely had time to explain what was wrong, you're not alone. The average primary care visit in a traditional insurance-based practice lasts just 15 to 18 minutes — and that includes time for documentation, prescription management, and anything else on the agenda. It's not that physicians don't care. It's that the system they work within makes genuine connection nearly impossible.

Concierge medicine is a direct response to that frustration.

**The core idea is simple:** patients pay a regular membership fee — monthly or annually — and in return, they get more. More time with their doctor. More access. More continuity. Instead of a physician managing 1,500 to 2,500 patients, a concierge or DPC doctor typically cares for a panel of 300 to 600 patients — compared to 1,500–2,500 in a conventional practice. At Forward Family Medicine, we cap at approximately 250 patients per physician, which is what makes genuine same-day access structurally reliable. That smaller panel is what makes everything else possible.

**What patients typically receive in a concierge practice:**

- Same-day or next-day appointments
- Extended visit times — at Forward Family Medicine, 1 to 2 hours
- Direct access to the physician by phone, text, or email
- After-hours availability for urgent questions
- Unhurried annual physicals and preventive care
- A doctor who actually knows your history, your family, your lifestyle

**There are two main models of concierge medicine**, and understanding the difference matters a great deal for your budget.

The first is *traditional concierge medicine*, which emerged in the late 1990s and gained attention as a luxury offering for wealthy patients. These practices typically charge a retainer fee of $2,000 to $5,000 per year — sometimes significantly more — and in many cases still accept insurance and bill for individual visits on top of the retainer. The retainer buys access and service level; insurance is still in the picture for the actual care.

The second model is *Direct Primary Care (DPC)*, which has grown rapidly over the past decade and is often described as "concierge medicine made affordable." In a DPC practice, patients pay a flat monthly membership fee — typically in the range of $75 to $200 per month for adults depending on the practice and what's included. At Forward Family Medicine, individual membership is $200/month and includes integrated board-eligible Lifestyle Medicine — a clinical service most DPC practices simply don't offer — and there is no insurance billing for primary care services. The fee covers most office visits, basic procedures, care coordination, and direct physician access. Because the practice doesn't spend resources on insurance billing and coding, the overhead drops dramatically, and that savings gets passed to patients.

**Common misconceptions about concierge medicine:**

One of the biggest myths is that concierge care is exclusively for wealthy patients. That may have been true in 1998, but the DPC model has fundamentally changed the landscape. A DPC membership often costs less per month than a single traditional copay-plus-office-visit charge, particularly for patients with high-deductible health plans.

Another misconception is that you don't need regular insurance if you have a concierge physician. Most concierge and DPC doctors strongly recommend that patients maintain some form of insurance — ideally a high-deductible or catastrophic plan — for hospitalizations, specialist care, imaging, surgery, and other services outside the scope of primary care.

Finally, some patients worry that concierge medicine means harder-to-reach specialists or a more isolated medical experience. In reality, good concierge and DPC physicians are often *better* at coordinating specialist care because they have time to advocate for their patients, write thoughtful referral letters, and follow up on results.

**Who is concierge medicine best for?**

Concierge and DPC practices are a particularly strong fit for patients who:

- Are frustrated by long waits and rushed appointments
- Have one or more chronic conditions requiring ongoing management
- Travel frequently and need telehealth access
- Want a physician who can be reached directly when something comes up
- Are self-employed or on high-deductible plans and want to reduce unnecessary specialist visits
- Simply value having a physician who knows them well

In the Philadelphia suburbs and along the Main Line, where schedules are demanding and access to good care matters, the concierge and DPC model has grown steadily as patients look for something better than the status quo.

## Practical Tips

- Ask any prospective concierge or DPC physician how many patients are currently on their panel — the smaller the panel, the more access you'll realistically receive.
- Understand what the membership fee does and does not include before signing up; most DPC practices are transparent about this upfront.
- If you're on a high-deductible health plan, run the numbers: a DPC membership plus an HDHP is often cheaper and more useful than a traditional PPO with high premiums.
- Don't assume that online reviews capture the full picture of a concierge practice — the best signal is often a brief meet-and-greet conversation with the physician.
- Ask specifically about after-hours access: what's the process, who responds, and how quickly?
- Even in a concierge or DPC model, you'll still want insurance for hospitalizations, emergency care, and specialists — plan accordingly.
- If you have a chronic condition, ask whether the practice has experience managing it and what their approach looks like.

## FAQ

**Q: Is concierge medicine the same as direct primary care?**
A: They share the same philosophy — smaller patient panels, better access, a direct physician relationship — but differ in structure. Traditional concierge practices often charge a higher retainer and may still bill insurance per visit. DPC practices charge a flat monthly fee and do not bill insurance for primary care, which typically results in lower costs for patients.

**Q: Do I still need health insurance if I join a concierge or DPC practice?**
A: Yes. Concierge and DPC practices cover primary care, but they don't replace insurance for hospitalizations, surgeries, specialist visits, emergency care, or imaging. Most patients pair a DPC membership with a high-deductible health plan or some form of catastrophic coverage.

**Q: How do I know if a concierge practice is legitimate and right for me?**
A: Look for board-certified physicians with clear, transparent pricing and a willingness to meet with you before you commit. Ask about panel size, appointment availability, how after-hours access works, and what services are included in the membership fee. A good practice will welcome these questions.

**Q: Can my employer's HSA or FSA be used to pay for concierge medicine?**
A: Following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying high-deductible health plan. Confirm current eligibility with your plan administrator.
