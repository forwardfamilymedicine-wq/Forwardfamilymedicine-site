## Quick Answer
A doctor meet-and-greet is a brief, no-obligation conversation with a physician before you become a patient. It's common in direct primary care practices, essentially unheard of in traditional insurance-based medicine, and one of the best ways to evaluate whether a practice is right for you.

## Explanation

Choosing a primary care physician is one of the most consequential healthcare decisions you'll make — and in the traditional system, you typically make it with almost no information. You search your insurance directory, check that the practice is accepting new patients, maybe read a few online reviews, and show up hoping for the best. The relationship forms (or doesn't) over repeated visits, and switching doctors is a friction-filled process most people avoid even when the fit isn't right.

Direct primary care practices often do something different: they offer a free meet-and-greet appointment before you enroll. This is a short (typically 20–30 minute) conversation with the physician where you can ask questions, describe your health situation, understand the practice model, and assess whether this is a person you could see managing your health over the long term. There's no clinical exam, no charges, and no obligation.

This is relatively rare in American medicine. Its existence in DPC practices reflects a philosophy that the patient-physician relationship is foundational — not a transaction — and that both parties benefit from choosing each other intentionally.

**What happens during a meet-and-greet**

The physician will typically introduce the practice model: how DPC works, what's included in membership, how to reach them, panel size, visit length, and how referrals and specialist coordination work. They'll ask about your health background, your current providers, and what you're looking for in a primary care physician.

You should feel genuinely invited to ask questions. This isn't a sales pitch — it's a mutual evaluation. The physician is assessing whether they can serve you well; you're assessing whether this is someone you trust with your health.

**Questions worth asking at a meet-and-greet**

Come prepared with questions that matter to you. Here are the ones that tend to reveal the most:

**About the practice model:**
- What is your current panel size? (Smaller panels mean more time per patient — a key DPC advantage.)
- What's typically included in the membership? What requires an additional cost?
- What is the average visit length for a routine appointment? For an acute sick visit?
- How do I reach you after hours? What's the typical response time?
- What happens if I need care when you're unavailable?

**About clinical approach:**
- What is your approach to preventive care and chronic disease management?
- Do you incorporate lifestyle medicine — nutrition, exercise, sleep — into your practice?
- How do you approach medication decisions? (This can reveal whether the physician is thoughtful about deprescribing and lifestyle approaches versus defaulting to medications.)
- How do you coordinate with specialists? What does that communication look like?

**About scope and limits:**
- What types of conditions or situations fall outside what you can manage?
- Do you do any procedures in the office? (Minor lacerations, skin biopsies, joint injections, etc.)
- How do you handle mental health concerns?

**About logistics:**
- How long have you been practicing?
- Are you the only physician, or is there a team?
- What happens to my care if you take a vacation or are unavailable for an extended period?

**How to evaluate the fit**

After the meeting, ask yourself: Did the physician listen? Were they thoughtful and direct? Did they explain things in plain language? Did you feel respected rather than processed? Did their philosophy of care align with yours?

The clinical credentials matter. The communication style matters just as much — maybe more. You're evaluating someone who you'll potentially share health decisions with for years. Trust your instincts about whether the conversation felt genuine.

**What a good answer looks like**

A DPC physician who knows their practice well should be able to answer these questions specifically, not vaguely. "We try to be available" is not the same as "Members can text me directly; I typically respond within a few hours during the day." Specificity reflects confidence and an honest understanding of what the practice actually delivers.

## Practical Tips

- Prepare three to five questions before your meet-and-greet that reflect your actual priorities — not a list of everything possible. Quality of engagement matters more than covering every topic.
- Be honest about your health history during the meet-and-greet. The physician needs to honestly assess whether they can serve your needs well.
- Ask specifically about any condition you're already managing. How they respond to your specific situation tells you more than general descriptions of their approach.
- Note how the physician handles uncertainty or questions they don't know the answer to. Intellectually honest physicians say "I don't know, but here's how I'd find out."
- Consider bringing a family member or partner if they'll also be patients at the practice — their impressions add useful perspective.
- Ask what the onboarding process looks like: what records you'll need to transfer, when your first formal visit would occur, and how continuity is established.
- Trust the conversation as data. If you feel rushed, dismissed, or that the physician is performing rather than engaging, that experience is probably representative.

## FAQ

**Q: Is the meet-and-greet really free and no-obligation?**
A: At Forward Family Medicine, yes — completely. There's no clinical examination, no billing, and no expectation that you'll enroll. It's a genuine opportunity to evaluate the practice before making a commitment.

**Q: What if I have a complex medical history? Is DPC right for me?**
A: Complex patients often benefit most from DPC's additional access and continuity. The meet-and-greet is precisely the right time to describe your health situation in detail and ask the physician directly whether your needs are within the practice's scope. A good DPC physician will give you an honest answer about what they can and can't manage effectively.

**Q: How long does a typical meet-and-greet last?**
A: At Forward Family Medicine, 20 minutes. This is enough time for meaningful conversation but not for a clinical evaluation. If you have extensive questions or a complex history, consider writing them down in advance to use the time efficiently.

**Q: What if I enroll and it's not the right fit?**
A: DPC practices typically operate on a month-to-month membership with no long-term contract. If the relationship isn't working for any reason, you're not locked in. That flexibility is intentional — it keeps the practice accountable to the quality of your experience.
