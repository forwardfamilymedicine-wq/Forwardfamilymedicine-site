## Quick Answer
The average traditional primary care physician manages 2,000 to 3,000 patients — requiring 20 to 30 visits per day to stay financially viable. DPC physicians cap their panels at roughly 300–600 patients — already a fraction of the traditional 2,000–3,000. At Forward Family Medicine, we cap at approximately 250 patients per physician — smaller than the DPC average — because we believe that number is what genuine availability and true continuity actually require. Panel size is the root cause of most primary care frustrations.

## Explanation

Have you ever wondered why it takes three weeks to get a routine appointment with your primary care doctor? Or why your visit felt like it lasted exactly seven minutes? Or why you left with your questions only half-answered?

The answer isn't that your doctor doesn't care. It's almost certainly that your doctor is managing far too many patients.

### The Math Behind Traditional Primary Care

Traditional primary care physicians are paid through insurance reimbursement — a set fee for each billing code associated with each visit. To cover their overhead (staff, rent, malpractice insurance, billing software, electronic health records) and earn a living, a traditional primary care practice needs to see a certain number of patients every single day.

The numbers work out roughly like this:

- A typical primary care practice has operating costs of $500,000–$700,000 per year
- Average reimbursement per primary care visit runs $100–$150 after insurance contracts and write-offs
- To break even and earn a reasonable salary, a physician needs to see approximately 20–30 patients every working day
- At that volume, with 48 working weeks per year, that means a panel of 2,000–3,000 active patients

This is not a conspiracy or negligence — it is arithmetic. The fee-for-service system forces primary care physicians into a volume trap. See more patients, generate more revenue. The only alternative is to run a financially unsustainable practice.

### What 2,500 Patients Means for You

When your doctor has 2,500 patients in their panel, here is your practical reality:

**Access:** If each of those patients needs to be seen just once per year, that's 2,500 visits to fit into roughly 240 working days. That's already more than 10 per day for routine annual visits alone — before anyone gets sick. Getting a same-day appointment when you genuinely need to be seen requires bumping someone else or squeezing you into a gap that may not exist.

**Visit length:** If your doctor is seeing 25 patients in an eight-hour day, the math allows roughly 19 minutes per patient — and that includes charting, reviewing labs, and handling in-basket messages between visits. By the time the physician actually walks in the room, you may have 10–12 minutes of actual face time. Studies consistently find the average primary care visit in the U.S. runs 7–10 minutes.

**Continuity:** When someone in a 2,500-patient panel calls with a question, the doctor may not remember their history off the top of their head. The staff handles a large portion of triage. Your call may get routed to a nurse who answers your question without the physician ever being involved.

**Responsiveness:** After-hours questions go to a nurse line or prompt a trip to urgent care, because it's simply not possible for one physician to be truly available to 2,500 people.

### The DPC Model: What 500 Patients Changes

DPC practices generally cap their panels at roughly 300–600 patients. At Forward Family Medicine, our cap is approximately 250 patients per physician — below the DPC average — which is what makes same-day access consistently reliable, not just theoretically possible. The funding model is memberships rather than per-visit billing, which means the physician's income is stable without seeing a high daily volume.

The numbers work out very differently:

- With 500 patients and a full schedule, a DPC physician typically sees 10–15 patients per day
- That allows 1-to-2-hour appointments — time to actually talk, examine, review history, and answer questions
- Because visit volume is lower, same-day and next-day scheduling is structurally possible, not the exception
- After-hours contact is manageable because the patient panel is small enough to be actually known by the physician
- The physician can proactively reach out to patients who need follow-up, because they know who those patients are

This is not a philosophical difference in care philosophy — it is a structural difference. The panel cap is what makes everything else possible.

### Why This Matters for Outcomes

Research consistently shows that continuity of care with a primary care physician is associated with lower rates of hospitalization, fewer emergency department visits, better management of chronic conditions, and higher patient satisfaction. Those outcomes require a physician who knows you, has time for you, and is available when you need them.

None of those things are scalable at 2,500 patients per physician. They are entirely achievable at 500.

The panel size difference is, in a very real sense, the entire argument for direct primary care.

## Practical Tips

- **Ask your current doctor their panel size** — most practices won't advertise this, but it's a legitimate question that tells you a lot about your likely experience.
- **Notice the access signals** — if it takes more than 2–3 business days to get a sick visit, that's a panel size problem, not a scheduling problem.
- **Value visit length** — a doctor who can spend 1 to 2 hours with you for a chronic disease review will provide genuinely better care than one with 10 minutes. That difference matters.
- **Understand that your doctor's frustration is real too** — physicians in high-volume practices often want to practice differently but are trapped by the economics of fee-for-service.
- **Think about the compound effect of continuity** — a physician who sees you consistently over years accumulates insight about your health that a new face every visit cannot replicate.
- **Don't assume DPC is only for the wealthy** — smaller panel + lower overhead + no billing staff often means DPC practices can operate at surprisingly accessible price points.
- **Ask about panel availability before enrolling** — good DPC practices close enrollment when they're full, because that's the whole point. Don't wait too long once you've decided.

## FAQ

**Q: Why don't traditional primary care practices just limit their panels?**
A: Because the fee-for-service payment model makes it financially impossible. If a traditional practice limited to 500 patients, they'd be seeing perhaps 8–12 patients per day at standard reimbursement rates — which wouldn't cover operating costs, let alone physician salary. The panel cap is only viable in DPC because membership fees replace per-visit billing and create a sustainable revenue model at lower volume.

**Q: Does a smaller panel mean a DPC practice earns less than a traditional practice?**
A: Not necessarily. DPC physicians trade high volume for predictable, stable membership revenue. Many DPC physicians report earning similar to or better than their fee-for-service peers — with less administrative burden, more satisfying work, and more control over their practice. The economic model is different, not inferior.

**Q: What happens when a DPC practice's panel fills up?**
A: Most DPC practices maintain a waitlist once they reach capacity. This is actually a feature, not a bug — it means the practice is committed to the panel limit rather than overriding it for revenue reasons. If you're interested in a DPC practice, it's worth joining a waitlist or enrolling before the panel closes.

**Q: Is a 400–600 patient panel actually enough to run a practice?**
A: Yes, when funded by membership fees. At $100–$200/month per adult member and 250–500 members, a DPC practice generates $300,000–$1.2M/year depending on panel size and membership rate. With leaner overhead — no billing staff, no insurance contracts — this supports a well-run practice delivering meaningfully better care than the fee-for-service model.
