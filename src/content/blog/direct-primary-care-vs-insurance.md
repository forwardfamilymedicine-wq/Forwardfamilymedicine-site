## Quick Answer
Direct primary care (DPC) is a membership model for unlimited primary care — it is not insurance and does not replace it. Traditional insurance covers hospitals, specialists, and emergencies; DPC covers your day-to-day relationship with your doctor. Used together, they can deliver better care at a lower total cost.

## Explanation

The confusion is understandable. Both involve paying a regular amount to access healthcare. But direct primary care and traditional health insurance are fundamentally different tools, and understanding the distinction can help you make much smarter healthcare decisions.

### What Traditional Insurance Actually Does

Health insurance is designed to protect you from catastrophic financial loss. When you need surgery, a hospital stay, cancer treatment, or emergency care, insurance steps in so a medical event doesn't bankrupt you. That's the core function it was built for.

The problem is that the insurance billing system has expanded to cover routine primary care — annual checkups, sick visits, medication management, chronic disease follow-ups. And that expansion has created an enormous amount of administrative work for everyone involved. Every visit requires coding, billing, authorizations, and claims processing. Doctors spend as much time on paperwork as they do on patients. The result: rushed appointments, high overhead, and a system where the physician-patient relationship is constantly mediated by a third party.

### What Direct Primary Care Does

DPC removes insurance from the primary care equation entirely. You pay your doctor a flat monthly fee — typically $75–$200 per month for adults depending on the practice and what's included. At Forward Family Medicine, individual membership is $200/month, which includes integrated board-eligible Lifestyle Medicine — something most DPC practices do not offer and that substantially changes the value equation for anyone managing a chronic condition. In exchange, you get:

- Unlimited primary care visits with no per-visit fees
- Same-day or next-day appointment availability
- Longer appointments (30–60 minutes vs. the national average of 7 minutes)
- Direct communication with your physician via text, phone, or video
- Wholesale-priced labs and medications in many practices

No claims. No prior authorizations. No explanation-of-benefits letters in the mail three months later.

### DPC Is NOT a Replacement for Insurance

This point cannot be overstated. If you join a DPC practice and drop your health insurance entirely, you are exposed. A broken leg, an appendectomy, a serious diagnosis — these can generate bills in the tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars. DPC does not cover any of that.

What DPC does do is dramatically reduce how often you need to use your insurance for primary care. When your DPC membership covers unlimited visits, same-day sick care, telehealth consultations, and medication management, your insurance becomes what it was originally designed to be: catastrophic coverage.

### The Smart Strategy: DPC + HDHP

For many patients — especially self-employed individuals, families, and anyone currently paying high premiums for a PPO — the financially savvy move is to combine a DPC membership with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP).

HDHPs carry lower monthly premiums than traditional PPOs. The tradeoff is a higher deductible if something major happens. But if your routine primary care needs are covered by DPC, you're far less likely to hit that deductible anyway. You get excellent primary care, solid catastrophic coverage, and the ability to contribute to a health savings account (HSA).

Compared to paying PPO premiums plus copays plus deductibles, the DPC + HDHP combination often comes out meaningfully ahead financially — while delivering significantly better care for routine needs.

### Who Benefits Most from This Combination

- **Self-employed and 1099 workers** who buy their own insurance
- **Small business owners** looking for a better healthcare strategy for themselves or their team
- **Families** whose children primarily use primary care services
- **People managing chronic conditions** who need frequent primary care contact
- **Anyone currently using urgent care regularly** because they can't get timely primary care appointments

## Practical Tips

- **Never drop health insurance entirely** — DPC covers primary care only; you need coverage for hospitals and specialists.
- **Compare your current annual healthcare spend** (premiums + copays + deductibles) to DPC membership + HDHP premiums before assuming traditional insurance is cheaper.
- **Ask your DPC practice about lab and medication pricing** — the savings alone can offset a meaningful portion of your monthly membership fee.
- **Use DPC to stay ahead of chronic conditions** — more frequent check-ins mean fewer expensive specialist referrals and hospitalizations down the line.
- **If you have an HDHP, open an HSA** — following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP — confirm with your plan administrator.
- **Think of insurance as catastrophic protection** — if you shift your mindset away from using insurance for every visit, HDHPs become far more attractive financially.
- **Ask your employer about DPC benefits** — some employers offer DPC as a supplemental benefit alongside traditional insurance coverage.

## FAQ

**Q: Is direct primary care considered insurance?**
A: No. The IRS, state insurance regulators, and the ACA all classify DPC as a membership arrangement, not insurance. DPC membership fees are not insurance premiums, and DPC does not provide coverage for hospitals, specialists, emergencies, or anything outside primary care.

**Q: Can I use my health insurance at a DPC practice?**
A: Most DPC practices do not bill insurance for office visits — that's by design. However, if your DPC practice orders outside labs, imaging, or specialist referrals, your insurance would apply to those services. Some DPC practices will submit claims for procedures or services they provide if requested, but the membership model is built around not needing to.

**Q: What happens if I need a specialist while enrolled in DPC?**
A: Specialist visits are handled through your health insurance, just as they would be in any other primary care arrangement. A good DPC physician will help coordinate that referral, communicate with the specialist on your behalf, and follow up on the results — which is actually better care coordination than most traditionally insured patients receive.

**Q: Is DPC + HDHP legal under the ACA?**
A: Yes. Having a DPC membership alongside an ACA-compliant high-deductible health plan is perfectly legal. In fact, several states have passed legislation explicitly clarifying that DPC memberships do not disqualify a patient from contributing to an HSA, though federal law on the HSA question is still evolving. Consult a tax advisor for current guidance specific to your situation.
