## Quick Answer
A thorough annual physical is a comprehensive head-to-toe review of your health — including your history, vital signs, physical exam, age-appropriate screenings, lab work, and a conversation about your lifestyle and goals. It goes well beyond what most insurance-defined "wellness visits" actually cover.

## Explanation

There is a meaningful difference between what insurance calls an "Annual Wellness Visit" (AWV) and what a truly comprehensive annual physical looks like. The insurance-defined AWV is largely a paperwork exercise — a structured checklist of questions used to update your health record and generate quality metrics. It is not the same as a hands-on, doctor-led physical exam. In a direct primary care practice, the annual physical is what it was always supposed to be: unrushed, thorough, and genuinely useful.

**What a comprehensive annual physical actually includes:**

**Medical history review.** Your physician reviews your past medical diagnoses, surgeries, hospitalizations, and family history. For established patients, this means revisiting what has changed since your last visit. For new patients, it is a complete intake. This context shapes every other decision made during the visit.

**Medication reconciliation.** Every prescription, over-the-counter medication, supplement, and herbal product gets reviewed. Interactions, appropriate dosing, continued necessity, and any side effects you may have attributed to something else — all of it gets examined.

**Vital signs.** Blood pressure (in both arms if there is any concern), heart rate, respiratory rate, weight, BMI, and often oxygen saturation. Your physician is looking for trends over time, not just whether today's numbers fall inside a range.

**Physical examination by system.** A skilled physician examines your skin, lymph nodes, thyroid, heart, lungs, abdomen, and musculoskeletal system. Depending on your age and history, this may include a neurological screen, a breast exam, a testicular exam, or a prostate assessment. This is where "putting hands on the patient" catches things that no questionnaire will find.

**Age-appropriate screening discussion.** Based on your age, sex, family history, and risk profile, your physician reviews which screenings are due or overdue — colorectal cancer, cervical cancer, breast cancer, lung cancer (if you qualify), osteoporosis, abdominal aortic aneurysm, STIs, and others. The USPSTF (U.S. Preventive Services Task Force) guidelines provide the evidence base for most of these recommendations, and your physician can explain the reasoning behind each one in plain language.

**Lab orders.** A baseline or updated panel — commonly a complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel, lipid panel, HbA1c or fasting glucose, and thyroid function — helps establish your metabolic picture and detect early abnormalities. Which labs are ordered depends on your individual history, not a one-size-fits-all protocol.

**Lifestyle and mental health assessment.** Diet, exercise, alcohol use, tobacco, sleep quality, and stress are not afterthoughts — they are often the most powerful levers for your long-term health. A good annual physical includes a real conversation about these, not just a checkbox.

**Immunization review.** Are your vaccines current? This includes flu, Tdap, COVID-19, shingles (Shingrix starting at 50), pneumococcal vaccines (starting at 65 or earlier with risk factors), and others based on your history.

**What to bring to your annual physical:**
- A list of all current medications and doses (or bring the bottles)
- Records from any specialists you have seen in the past year
- Any outside lab results or imaging reports
- A written list of your questions — you are far more likely to remember them this way
- Your insurance card if applicable, though in a DPC practice this matters less

**Questions worth preparing:**
- Is there anything in my family history I should be screened for that we have not addressed?
- Are all my current medications still the right choice?
- Based on my numbers, what is my actual cardiovascular risk?
- What is the one lifestyle change that would make the most difference for me right now?

## Practical Tips

- Schedule your annual physical when you are not sick — the visit is specifically designed for prevention, not acute illness management
- Fast for 8–12 hours before the appointment if your physician has ordered a fasting lipid panel or glucose
- Write down your questions in advance; people forget 50–80% of what they planned to ask once they are in the exam room
- Bring a complete medication list, including supplements and vitamins
- Be honest about alcohol use, sleep, and stress — your physician is not there to judge, and accurate information leads to better care
- If you have had bloodwork done elsewhere recently, bring those results so they are not repeated unnecessarily
- Follow up on any referrals, specialist recommendations, or screening orders within 30 days — the annual physical only works if you complete the follow-through

## FAQ

**Q: Is an annual physical the same as an "annual wellness visit" through my insurance?**
A: No. The insurance-defined Annual Wellness Visit is a structured questionnaire and health risk assessment — it is primarily an administrative tool. It does not include a hands-on physical exam and is focused on data collection for quality reporting. A comprehensive annual physical, as practiced in direct primary care, is a full clinical examination and personalized prevention review.

**Q: How long should an annual physical take?**
A: At Forward Family Medicine, annual physicals typically run 1 to 2 hours for established patients — and longer for new patients with significant medical history. This is by design. A physical that runs 15–20 minutes is not delivering a comprehensive evaluation; a physician managing a panel of 250 patients has the time to do this properly.

**Q: Do I need to get blood work done before the appointment or after?**
A: Many physicians prefer to have labs drawn before the appointment so results can be reviewed during the visit. At Forward Family Medicine, we typically order labs in advance and review the results with you in real time during your physical. We will let you know what to fast for and when to get labs drawn.

**Q: What if I feel fine — do I still need an annual physical?**
A: Yes, and this is precisely the point. Many significant conditions — hypertension, prediabetes, elevated cholesterol, early-stage cancers — produce no symptoms in their early stages. The annual physical is designed to find things before they cause problems, when intervention is most effective. Feeling fine is a starting point, not a reason to skip it.
