## Quick Answer
Employers are pairing Direct Primary Care memberships with high-deductible health plans to reduce overall benefits costs while giving employees faster, better primary care — a model that works for small businesses and self-insured companies alike.

## Explanation

The math of employer-sponsored health insurance in the United States has become increasingly difficult to justify. Average annual premiums for employer-sponsored family coverage now exceed $25,000, with employers shouldering the majority of that cost. Year-over-year increases have consistently outpaced inflation for more than a decade. For small businesses in particular, offering competitive health benefits without going bankrupt in the process has become a genuine strategic challenge.

Direct Primary Care (DPC) has emerged as a meaningful part of the answer for a growing number of employers. It doesn't replace health insurance entirely — that would leave employees without coverage for hospitalizations, surgeries, specialist care, and catastrophic events. But it changes the primary care layer of the benefits stack in a way that reduces cost and meaningfully improves the employee experience.

**The Basic Model**

An employer covers a DPC membership for each employee — or offers it as an elective benefit — and pairs it with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) rather than a rich PPO or low-deductible plan. The logic:

- The DPC membership covers unlimited primary care visits, same-day or next-day access, direct messaging with the physician, and basic in-office procedures
- Because employees can access primary care immediately and at no incremental cost, they don't need to pay into a low-deductible plan to afford frequent doctor visits
- The HDHP handles true insurance events: hospitalizations, surgeries, ER visits, specialist care
- The HDHP premium is substantially lower than a rich PPO, which partially or fully offsets the DPC membership cost

For many employers, this structure reduces per-member-per-month (PMPM) total healthcare spend. For employees, it eliminates the frustration of rationing primary care visits due to copay concerns — a surprisingly significant driver of delayed care and downstream costs.

**Why This Reduces Overall Costs**

Traditional insurance-based primary care is plagued by inefficiency. Billing overhead consumes 30–40% of every healthcare dollar spent in the administrative layer. When primary care runs through insurance, physicians see more patients in less time to cover overhead, quality suffers, preventive care is deprioritized, and employees end up in urgent care or ERs for problems a primary care physician could have addressed — at a fraction of the cost.

DPC removes the insurance billing layer from primary care entirely. The physician's overhead drops dramatically. That savings flows through to lower membership fees and more time per patient. Employees with a DPC physician are more likely to seek care early, manage chronic conditions proactively, and avoid expensive downstream events.

For self-insured employers — who bear the direct risk of employee health claims — reducing high-cost events through better primary care access is directly visible on the balance sheet. Several self-insured employer case studies have shown 20–40% reductions in total healthcare spend over 2–3 years with DPC integration.

**Who Is Already Doing This**

Self-insured employers, particularly in the 50–500 employee range, have been early adopters. Small businesses that previously couldn't afford meaningful health benefits have used DPC as a way to offer *something* valuable without the full cost of group coverage. Increasingly, larger employers are piloting DPC alongside traditional benefits as a voluntary option for employees who want better primary care access.

Professional employer organizations (PEOs) and benefits brokers have begun actively recommending DPC as a component of benefits strategy, rather than treating it as a fringe option.

**What Employees Actually Experience**

From the employee perspective, the DPC membership feels dramatically different from traditional insurance. Same-day or next-day appointments. Direct access to the physician by phone or message. Visits that aren't rushed. A physician who knows them over time. No surprise bills for primary care. For many employees, particularly those managing a chronic condition or those with families, this is a meaningfully better experience than what they had before.

## Practical Tips

- If you're an employer, ask your benefits broker to model a DPC + HDHP option against your current plan — the PMPM comparison is often eye-opening
- Pair the DPC membership with an HSA to help employees cover the HDHP deductible for non-primary-care expenses
- Consider offering DPC as an elective benefit initially — employees who self-select are more likely to engage and demonstrate the model's value
- Look for DPC practices that offer employer agreements with clear service scope and pricing
- Ask prospective DPC physicians what their patient panel size is — a DPC physician with 600 patients provides very different access than one with 1,200
- For self-insured employers, request claims data from your TPA to establish a baseline before implementing DPC — you'll want to measure the impact
- Communicate the DPC benefit clearly to employees: many won't understand it's different from a standard copay-based plan

## FAQ

**Q: Does DPC replace health insurance for my employees?**
A: No. DPC covers primary care only. Employees still need health insurance for hospitalizations, specialist care, surgeries, ER visits, imaging, and other non-primary-care services. DPC is typically paired with a high-deductible health plan to keep total benefits costs manageable.

**Q: Is DPC an eligible expense under an HSA?**
A: Following the Primary Care Enhancement Act (effective January 1, 2026), DPC membership fees are now HSA-eligible when paired with a qualifying HDHP. Confirm with your plan administrator. The HDHP paired with DPC can also feed an HSA that employees use for deductible expenses. Tax situations vary — encourage employees to consult a tax advisor.

The full analysis requires comparing this cost against the premium reduction from moving to an HDHP and modeling expected claims patterns.

**Q: What happens if an employee needs a specialist?**
A: Specialist care, labs, imaging, and hospitalizations all still go through the employee's health insurance (the HDHP). The DPC physician coordinates referrals, communicates with specialists, and helps the employee navigate that process — but the specialist's care itself is billed to insurance, not included in the DPC membership.
