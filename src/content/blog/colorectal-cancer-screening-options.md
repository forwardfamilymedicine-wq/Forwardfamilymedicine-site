## Quick Answer
The American Cancer Society and USPSTF both recommend starting colorectal cancer screening at age 45 for average-risk adults. You have real choices: colonoscopy, Cologuard, annual FIT test, and CT colonography all have different tradeoffs, and the best option is the one you will actually complete.

## Explanation

Colorectal cancer is the second leading cause of cancer death in the United States — and it is one of the most preventable. Unlike many cancers, colorectal cancer typically grows slowly from precancerous polyps over a period of 10 to 15 years, giving us a real window to intervene before cancer develops. Screening is not just about detecting cancer early; it is often about removing the precursors before cancer ever forms.

**The updated starting age:**

In 2018, the American Cancer Society revised its recommendation, lowering the starting age for average-risk adults from 50 to 45. The USPSTF followed with a similar recommendation in 2021. This change was driven by rising rates of colorectal cancer in younger adults — a trend that began in the 1990s and has accelerated since. If you turned 45 recently and have not started screening, it is time.

**Screening options: a practical comparison:**

**Colonoscopy — the gold standard**
Colonoscopy allows a physician to directly visualize the entire colon and rectum, and to remove any polyps found during the same procedure. If the exam is normal, it typically does not need to be repeated for 10 years. The tradeoff is the preparation (a bowel cleanse the day before, which most people find to be the hardest part), sedation (you need a driver), and time off from work. Serious complications are rare but possible, including bowel perforation and bleeding — estimated at roughly 1 per 1,000 procedures.

**Cologuard (stool DNA test)**
Cologuard is a noninvasive at-home test that detects blood and abnormal DNA shed by polyps and cancers into the stool. It requires no bowel prep, no sedation, and no time off work. A negative result is reassuring for 1–3 years. The key tradeoffs: sensitivity is lower than colonoscopy for detecting certain precancerous polyps, the false positive rate is higher than FIT (about 13%), and a positive result requires follow-up colonoscopy. It is a reasonable option for patients who would otherwise decline colonoscopy entirely.

**FIT test (fecal immunochemical test)**
The FIT test detects hidden blood in the stool using an antibody-based method. It is inexpensive, requires no bowel prep, and can be done at home. The significant requirement: it must be done annually, every year. Studies show that annual FIT testing has comparable long-term cancer mortality reduction to colonoscopy — but only if it is done consistently. A positive FIT result always requires follow-up colonoscopy.

**CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy)**
CT colonography uses computed tomography imaging to create a three-dimensional view of the colon. It requires the same bowel prep as colonoscopy but no sedation. If polyps are found, a follow-up colonoscopy for removal is still needed. It is repeated every 5 years if normal. Insurance coverage varies, and it exposes patients to a small amount of radiation.

**Which option is right for you?**

There is no universally superior option. The USPSTF and major guidelines acknowledge that adherence matters more than theoretical test performance. The colonoscopy you had three years ago beats the Cologuard you ordered but never completed. A productive conversation with your physician should account for your preferences, your risk factors, and practical logistics.

**When to start earlier or screen more frequently:**

Average-risk guidelines (starting at 45) do not apply to everyone. Start at 40, or 10 years before the youngest affected relative's diagnosis, if:
- A first-degree relative (parent, sibling, child) had colorectal cancer or adenomatous polyps before age 60
- You have two or more first-degree relatives with colorectal cancer at any age

Screen more frequently or start much earlier if you have:
- Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis)
- A personal history of colorectal cancer or advanced polyps
- Lynch syndrome or familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)
- A history of abdominal/pelvic radiation therapy

For patients with Lynch syndrome, colonoscopy every 1–2 years starting at 20–25 is typically recommended.

## Practical Tips

- If you turned 45 recently and have not started screening, schedule a conversation with your physician to choose the right option
- Be honest about your preferences — if you are not willing to do a colonoscopy, a FIT test done annually is far better than nothing
- A positive FIT or Cologuard result always requires follow-up colonoscopy; do not ignore it or delay
- If you have a first-degree relative who had colon cancer, discuss your personal screening timeline — the average-risk guidelines may not apply to you
- The bowel prep for colonoscopy has improved significantly; newer split-dose prep options are much more tolerable than older protocols
- Clear your schedule for the day of and day after colonoscopy — the sedation takes time to clear
- Keep a record of your screening results and the year they were done; this is critical information for future care

## FAQ

**Q: I just turned 45 and feel completely healthy. Do I really need a colonoscopy?**
A: The recommendation applies to all average-risk adults at 45, regardless of symptoms. Colorectal cancer and precancerous polyps typically cause no symptoms until they are advanced. Screening is specifically designed to find things before symptoms develop — that is when intervention is most effective and least invasive.

**Q: What is the difference between Cologuard and a FIT test?**
A: Both are stool-based, noninvasive tests done at home. The FIT test detects blood in the stool using an antibody and must be repeated annually. Cologuard detects both blood and abnormal DNA shed by polyps and cancers, and is repeated every 1–3 years. Cologuard detects a broader range of precancerous lesions but has a higher false positive rate than FIT. Both require a colonoscopy for follow-up if results are positive.

**Q: My colonoscopy was normal at 50. Can I wait until I'm 60 for the next one?**
A: If your colonoscopy was completely normal (no polyps found), a 10-year interval is standard for average-risk patients. So yes, if your screening colonoscopy at 50 was normal, your next one would typically be at 60. However, if polyps were found and removed, the follow-up interval is typically shorter — 3 years for high-risk polyps, 5–10 years for low-risk ones — based on the number, size, and type of polyps.

**Q: Does colorectal cancer screening hurt?**
A: Colonoscopy itself is performed under sedation, so most patients experience no discomfort during the procedure. The preparation (bowel cleanse the day before) is the part that most patients find unpleasant — though newer split-dose prep options have improved significantly. Stool-based tests (FIT, Cologuard) are entirely noninvasive and painless.
