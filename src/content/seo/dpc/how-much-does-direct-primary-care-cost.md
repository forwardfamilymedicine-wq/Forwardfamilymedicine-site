# How Much Does Direct Primary Care Cost?

## Quick Answer

**Direct Primary Care memberships typically cost $70–$150 per month for adults**, depending on the practice, your location, and your age. Children's add-on rates run $30–$60/month. Family plans range from $150–$300/month for two adults and children. There is no per-visit fee — the monthly membership covers unlimited primary care. Labs and medications are billed separately at wholesale/near-cost pricing.

---

## Why This Matters

One of the most common questions people have about DPC is whether it actually saves money. The answer requires looking at total cost — not just comparing the membership fee to a single insurance copay, but accounting for the full picture of what you spend on primary care each year.

Understanding DPC pricing also helps you make smarter decisions about what kind of health insurance to pair with it.

---

## What Affects DPC Pricing

### Practice Location
DPC fees vary by region. Urban and suburban practices in higher cost-of-living areas (like the Philadelphia Main Line) typically charge slightly more than rural practices. Most practices in the Northeast range from $85–$140/month for individual adults.

### Patient Age
Most DPC practices use age-banded pricing:
- **Children (0–17):** $25–$60/month (as add-on to family plan)
- **Young adults (18–30):** $70–$100/month
- **Adults (30–64):** $85–$150/month
- **Seniors (65+):** $100–$175/month

Older patients have higher average care utilization, which is reflected in pricing — though the unlimited visit model means it never costs more to see your doctor.

### Panel Inclusion
Some practices include annual labs in the membership fee. Others (including most DPC practices) charge labs separately at wholesale pricing. The latter is often better for patients because you only pay for labs you actually need.

### Enrollment Fees
Most practices charge a one-time enrollment fee ($50–$150 per individual, $100–$250 per family) to cover onboarding and record transfer costs.

## What's Included in the Membership Fee

At a DPC practice like Forward Family Medicine, one flat monthly fee covers:

- Unlimited primary care visits — in-person, phone, and video
- Same-day or next-day appointment availability
- Direct physician access (phone, text, secure message)
- Annual physical exams and preventive screenings
- Chronic disease management (diabetes, hypertension, thyroid, etc.)
- Lifestyle medicine counseling
- Minor in-office procedures
- Prescription refills and medication management
- Care coordination and specialist referrals

**Not included in membership:**
- Laboratory work (charged at cost)
- Prescription medications (dispensed at cost)
- Specialist visits, imaging, hospital care

## How DPC Costs Compare to Traditional Insurance

The real comparison isn't DPC vs. $0 — it's DPC + a lean insurance plan vs. a traditional PPO.

### Scenario: Adult, age 40, one chronic condition

**Traditional PPO (employer plan, moderate tier):**
| Cost Item | Annual Estimate |
|---|---|
| Employee premium contribution | $2,400–$4,800/year |
| Primary care copays (4–6 visits) | $120–$360/year |
| Specialist copays (2 visits) | $100–$200/year |
| Lab cost after insurance | $100–$400/year |
| Prescriptions | $200–$600/year |
| **Estimated total** | **$2,920–$6,360/year** |

**DPC Membership + High-Deductible Health Plan:**
| Cost Item | Annual Estimate |
|---|---|
| DPC membership ($110/month) | $1,320/year |
| HDHP premium (lower than PPO) | $1,200–$2,400/year |
| Labs at cost | $40–$120/year |
| Prescriptions at cost | $60–$200/year |
| HDHP deductible (if used for hospital/specialist) | $0–$1,500 (unlikely for primary care) |
| **Estimated total** | **$2,620–$4,540/year** |

*Estimates are illustrative. Individual results vary widely by employer, plan, location, and health utilization.*

The combination of DPC + HDHP often costs the same or less as a PPO — while delivering *far* better primary care access.

## Practical Tips for Calculating Your DPC Cost

- **Compare apples to apples.** Add up your last 12 months of copays, lab bills, prescription costs, and urgent care visits. That's your actual current primary care spend.
- **Get an HDHP quote.** Ask your benefits coordinator or use a marketplace plan calculator to compare HDHP premium costs vs. your current plan.
- **Estimate lab savings.** Ask any DPC practice for their lab pricing sheet. The difference vs. standard billed rates is often dramatic.
- **Factor in time.** If you spend 2–3 hours getting a single appointment (wait on hold, drive, wait in waiting room, short visit), multiply that by your hourly rate. Access has real economic value.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is DPC membership tax-deductible?**
DPC membership fees may qualify as a deductible medical expense if you itemize. They may also be reimbursable through an HRA or eligible for HSA use, depending on IRS guidance and your specific plan. Consult a tax advisor — the rules have evolved and vary by situation. [More on HSA and DPC →](/membership/hsa-dpc-membership)

**Are there any hidden fees?**
Reputable DPC practices have no hidden fees. You'll know your monthly rate, enrollment fee, and approximate lab costs before you sign anything. Ask us for a cost breakdown at your Meet & Greet.

**What if I have a month where I don't use the practice at all?**
The membership is like any subscription — it covers availability, not just usage. The value is having your doctor there when you need them. Most members find they use it more than they expected once access is this easy.

**Is the pricing lower than what I'd pay without insurance?**
Almost certainly. Uninsured patients in traditional practices pay full rack rates for everything. DPC membership + wholesale lab pricing is specifically designed to be affordable for uninsured patients. [More on primary care without insurance →](/membership/primary-care-without-insurance)
