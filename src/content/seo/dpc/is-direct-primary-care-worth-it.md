# Is Direct Primary Care Worth It?

## Quick Answer

**Yes — for most patients, Direct Primary Care is worth it.** DPC delivers same-day access, direct physician contact, longer visits, and wholesale lab pricing for a flat monthly fee that typically costs less than what most people pay in copays and deductibles each year. The real question is whether it's worth it *for you specifically* — and that depends on how you currently use healthcare.

---

## Why This Matters

Healthcare in the United States is expensive, fragmented, and frustrating. The average primary care visit lasts 15 minutes. Getting an appointment takes days or weeks. Your doctor may see 2,000+ patients and barely have time to review your chart before you walk in.

Direct Primary Care offers a structural alternative. By replacing insurance billing with a flat monthly membership, DPC practices remove the per-visit incentive that drives overloaded schedules — and replace it with something patients actually want: a physician with time for you.

The question of whether it's "worth it" comes down to three factors: **cost, access, and health outcomes.**

---

## The Cost Case for DPC

The most common objection is paying a monthly fee "on top of" health insurance. But that framing misses the math.

**What traditional primary care actually costs most people:**
- Average primary care copay: $25–$60 per visit
- Average specialist copay: $50–$100 per visit
- Deductible (before insurance pays anything): $1,000–$3,000+/year
- Annual out-of-pocket for primary care costs, labs, and prescriptions: often $600–$1,500+/year
- Hidden cost: time lost to hold music, call centers, and 2-week waits for appointments

**What DPC typically costs:**
- Monthly membership: $70–$150/month for adults ($840–$1,800/year)
- Labs: at wholesale/near-cost (often 80–90% less than standard billed rates)
- Prescription medications: at near-cost through direct partnerships

When you pair DPC with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) instead of a premium PPO, the combination often costs the same or less than traditional insurance alone — while delivering dramatically better primary care access.

**The transparency dividend:** In DPC, you know what your primary care costs. No EOBs. No surprise bills. No wondering if that five-minute call will generate a $150 charge. Predictability has real value.

## The Access Case for DPC

Beyond cost, consider the value of access that most people don't have:

- **Same-day or next-day appointments.** Not "the next available is in three weeks." Today or tomorrow.
- **Direct contact.** You call or text your actual physician — not a nurse line that takes a message.
- **After-hours availability.** Real urgent concerns get answered by your own doctor, not a generic on-call service.
- **Telehealth included.** Many concerns are resolved by phone or message without a visit at all.

If you've ever delayed care because getting an appointment felt like more trouble than the problem was worth — that's a real cost too, just one that doesn't show up on a bill.

## Practical Tips for Evaluating DPC for Yourself

- **Add up last year's out-of-pocket primary care costs.** Copays + labs + prescriptions + urgent care. Compare to a DPC membership fee.
- **Count how many times you needed same-day care.** Did you get it? If not, what did you do instead (urgent care, ER, wait and hope)?
- **Think about your chronic conditions.** If you have a condition requiring regular management, the unlimited visit model has compounding value.
- **Consider your current relationship with your doctor.** Do they know your history? Could you reach them if something came up?
- **Request a Meet & Greet.** The best way to know if DPC is worth it is to see a DPC practice firsthand.

## DPC Is Most Worth It For:

- People with one or more chronic conditions requiring ongoing management
- Families with children (one physician who knows every member)
- Self-employed individuals paying out-of-pocket for healthcare
- Anyone currently on a high-deductible plan who rarely meets their deductible
- Patients who value prevention and want proactive, not reactive, care
- People who have ever felt rushed, dismissed, or hard to reach at their current practice

## DPC Is Less Ideal For:

- People whose employers fully cover PPO premiums with $0 copays (rare, but they exist)
- Those who primarily need specialist or hospital-based care (DPC doesn't change that)
- Anyone moving frequently who can't build a long-term relationship with one practice
