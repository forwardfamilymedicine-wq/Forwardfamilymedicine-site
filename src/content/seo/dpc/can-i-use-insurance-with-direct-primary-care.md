# Can I Use Insurance with Direct Primary Care?

## Quick Answer

**Yes — and most DPC members do.** Direct Primary Care membership covers primary care without insurance billing, but you should still maintain health insurance for hospital stays, emergency care, specialist visits, and surgery. The most common and cost-effective combination is a DPC membership paired with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) — providing comprehensive primary care at the DPC level plus catastrophic coverage through the insurance plan.

---

## Why This Matters

One of the most common misconceptions about DPC is that it replaces health insurance. It does not — and no reputable DPC practice would suggest it does. The two serve different purposes and work best together, not instead of each other.

Understanding how DPC and insurance fit together helps you build the most cost-effective healthcare arrangement for your situation.

---

## What DPC Covers vs. What Insurance Covers

| Healthcare Need | DPC Membership Covers? | Insurance Covers? |
|---|---|---|
| Primary care visits (unlimited) | ✅ Yes | ✅ Typically yes (copays apply) |
| Same-day acute care | ✅ Yes | ✅ With copay |
| Annual physical | ✅ Yes | ✅ Usually preventive benefit |
| Chronic disease management | ✅ Yes | ✅ With copays |
| Labs (at wholesale pricing) | Separate charge (near-cost) | ✅ After deductible |
| Medications (at wholesale) | Separate charge (near-cost) | ✅ After deductible/formulary |
| Specialist visits | ❌ No (referral coordination only) | ✅ After copay/deductible |
| Emergency room visit | ❌ No | ✅ After copay/deductible |
| Hospital stay / surgery | ❌ No | ✅ After deductible |
| Imaging (MRI, X-ray, CT) | ❌ No (ordering + advocacy) | ✅ After deductible |

## The Recommended Combination: DPC + HDHP

**High-deductible health plans (HDHPs)** have lower monthly premiums than traditional PPO or HMO plans. The trade-off is a higher deductible before coverage kicks in for non-preventive services.

In a traditional insurance-only setup, this is a problem: you pay a high deductible before anything is covered, which discourages people from seeking primary care.

In a DPC + HDHP setup, the dynamic changes:
- Your primary care is fully covered by your DPC membership — you never pay a copay or hit a deductible for primary care
- Your HDHP deductible only applies to specialist care, imaging, hospital, and emergency services
- Because DPC covers your routine needs, you *actually* hit your HDHP deductible less often
- You can open an HSA (only available with an HDHP) to build tax-advantaged savings for future healthcare costs

**The result:** lower insurance premiums + no primary care copays + HSA savings = lower total healthcare cost in most years.

## Can I Use My Current Insurance and Add DPC?

Yes. If you have existing employer-sponsored insurance and want to add DPC membership, you can do both. Your DPC practice simply won't bill your insurance for primary care services — you pay the monthly membership fee.

You may choose to:
- Keep your current employer plan as-is and add DPC for better primary care access
- Switch to a less expensive HDHP at open enrollment to offset DPC membership cost
- Use your existing insurance for specialist and hospital needs while using DPC for all primary care

## Can DPC Work with These Insurance Types?

### Employer PPO or HMO
Yes. You can join a DPC practice regardless of your insurance plan. The DPC practice won't bill your insurance — your membership fee covers primary care. Your insurance remains available for specialists, labs (if you prefer), and hospital care.

### High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
The ideal pairing for DPC. Lower premiums + DPC coverage for primary care = strong cost combination. HDHP also enables HSA contributions.

### Medicare
DPC membership fees are not covered by Medicare. However, Medicare beneficiaries can join a DPC practice as self-pay members and use Medicare for specialist, hospital, and emergency coverage. This is legal and increasingly common.

### Medicaid
Similar to Medicare — Medicaid does not cover DPC fees. Members on Medicaid pay membership fees directly and use Medicaid for non-primary care services. Some patients on Medicaid find DPC accessible because the membership fee is transparent and fixed.

### Health-Sharing Ministries
Health-sharing ministries (not insurance, but cost-sharing organizations) pair well with DPC. The ministry covers large medical events; DPC covers primary care. This is the lowest-cost combination for many self-employed and high-health-risk individuals.

### No Insurance
DPC provides comprehensive primary care without insurance. For patients without health insurance, DPC membership is one of the most cost-effective ways to maintain access to quality primary care. We strongly recommend adding at least a catastrophic coverage plan.

## What Happens to Labs — Do I Use My Insurance or Pay DPC Rates?

You have a choice. When your DPC physician orders labs:
- **DPC wholesale pricing:** You pay directly at cost (often 80–90% below standard billed rates)
- **Through your insurance:** Your insurance processes the claim; you may pay less if you've met your deductible, or more if you haven't

For many patients, especially those on an HDHP who haven't met their deductible, the DPC wholesale rate is dramatically cheaper. We'll always give you the option to compare.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can my insurance bill be submitted to my DPC practice?**
No — it works the other way. Your DPC practice doesn't bill insurance. However, some patients with out-of-network benefits have submitted DPC receipts for partial reimbursement from their insurer. Check with your insurance plan.

**If I join DPC, can I still use my insurance for labs?**
Yes. You can choose to use your insurance for labs if you prefer. Your DPC physician will work with whatever lab arrangement you choose.

**Does DPC work with an HRA or Health Reimbursement Arrangement?**
Some employer HRAs can reimburse DPC membership fees. This depends on how the HRA is structured. Ask your HR department.

**What's the best insurance to pair with DPC?**
For most individuals not receiving employer-subsidized insurance: an HDHP (individual or family) paired with an HSA. For employer plan holders: ask your benefits coordinator whether switching to your employer's HDHP option makes financial sense.

You absolutely can use insurance with Direct Primary Care — and you should. DPC handles primary care comprehensively; insurance handles the rest. The most financially effective combination for most patients is DPC + HDHP + HSA, which typically costs less than a traditional PPO while delivering better primary care.
