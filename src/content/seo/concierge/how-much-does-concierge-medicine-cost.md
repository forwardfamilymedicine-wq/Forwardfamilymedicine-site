# How Much Does Concierge Medicine Cost?

## Quick Answer

**Traditional concierge medicine retainers typically cost $1,500–$6,000+ per year ($125–$500/month).** Direct Primary Care practices, which deliver the same concierge-style experience (same-day access, direct physician contact, longer visits), typically charge $70–$150/month — making them the more accessible alternative for most families. Both models may also have enrollment fees and per-service charges for labs, imaging, and medications outside the membership.

---

## Why This Matters

"Concierge medicine" has become a broad term that covers a range of practices and price points. If you've heard the term and wondered whether it's within reach, the answer is: it depends entirely on which model you're looking at. Understanding the pricing landscape helps you find genuine value — not just a premium-sounding name.

---

## The Two Main Pricing Models

### Traditional Concierge Medicine (High Retainer)

The original concierge model — pioneered in the late 1990s — charges patients an annual retainer fee in exchange for VIP-level access. In many practices, this is *in addition to* regular insurance billing for visits.

**Typical pricing:**
- Annual retainer: $1,500–$6,000+ per person
- Insurance billing: Yes — for office visits, labs, and procedures
- Who it targets: High-income individuals and executives seeking premium access

At this price point, concierge medicine panels can be very small (50–200 patients), and access is exceptionally personalized. But the cost puts it out of reach for most families — and many patients pay both the retainer *and* their insurance copays for visits.

### Direct Primary Care (Concierge-Style at Lower Cost)

Direct Primary Care emerged partly as a more accessible alternative to traditional concierge medicine. DPC practices offer the same core benefits — small panels, direct access, longer visits — but use a flat monthly membership and do not bill insurance for primary care.

**Typical pricing:**
- Monthly membership: $70–$150/month for adults
- Insurance billing: No — for primary care services
- Who it targets: A broad range of patients seeking better primary care

DPC is not technically "concierge medicine" in the traditional sense, but it delivers an equivalent (or better) experience for most primary care needs at a fraction of the cost.

## What's Included — and What Costs Extra

**Typically included in membership (both concierge and DPC):**
- Unlimited primary care visits (in-person, phone, video)
- Direct physician access
- Annual physical exams
- Chronic disease management
- Preventive care and screenings
- Prescription management

**Typically charged separately:**
- Laboratory work (at cost or billed to insurance)
- Prescription medications
- Specialist referrals (billed through insurance)
- Imaging
- Vaccinations (at cost or billed to insurance)
- Hospital care

The key difference: DPC practices price labs and medications at wholesale rates — a significant cost advantage compared to insurance-billed rates at traditional or high-end concierge practices.

## Is Concierge Medicine Worth the Cost?

Whether the cost is worth it depends on what you're comparing it to:

**Compared to a traditional PPO plan alone:**
DPC-style concierge pricing + HDHP often totals less than a traditional PPO while delivering dramatically better primary care access.

**Compared to high-end traditional concierge:**
DPC delivers comparable access and relationship quality at 70–90% less cost. The ultra-high-end retainer model offers amenities (house calls, 24-hour phone lines, airport-level concierge) that most patients don't need and wouldn't use regularly.

**Compared to nothing (uninsured or underinsured):**
DPC membership makes high-quality primary care affordable for people without employer-sponsored insurance — a group for whom concierge medicine was historically completely out of reach.

## Practical Tips for Evaluating Concierge Medicine Costs

- **Ask for a full cost breakdown.** Request the membership fee, enrollment fee, and estimated lab costs before committing.
- **Ask whether the practice bills insurance.** If yes, your total cost includes both the membership and your copays/deductibles. If DPC model (no billing), your membership is your cost.
- **Compare to your current annual spend.** Add up your actual out-of-pocket primary care costs — copays, labs, urgent care, prescriptions. Many patients find they're already spending near DPC membership levels.
- **Evaluate the panel size.** The value driver in any concierge model is the physician's time. A concierge practice with 600+ patients is delivering less than one with 300 — regardless of the marketing language.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does health insurance cover concierge medicine fees?**
Insurance does not cover DPC or concierge membership fees. Some high-end concierge practices still bill insurance for the actual visit services (on top of the retainer). DPC practices do not bill insurance at all for covered services.

**Can I use my HSA for concierge medicine fees?**
HSA eligibility for DPC/concierge membership fees is a nuanced IRS question. Some circumstances allow it; others don't. Consult a tax advisor and see our [HSA and DPC guide →](/membership/hsa-dpc-membership).

**Is the DPC model as good as traditional concierge medicine?**
For most primary care needs, yes — and often better. DPC practices typically have the same or smaller panel sizes, the same direct access model, and often more robust lifestyle medicine and chronic disease management. The ultra-premium traditional concierge model adds amenities (house calls, certain cosmetic services) that go beyond primary care scope.
