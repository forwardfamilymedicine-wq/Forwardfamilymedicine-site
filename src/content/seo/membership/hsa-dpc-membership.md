# Can I Use My HSA for a DPC Membership?

## Quick Answer

**The IRS currently does not recognize DPC membership fees as HSA-qualified expenses under a strict reading of the rules — because DPC memberships are structured as service contracts, not insurance or traditional medical expenses.** However, many qualified medical expenses *within* a DPC practice (labs, medications, procedures billed separately) are HSA-eligible. Some proposed legislation aims to clarify this. Consult a tax advisor for guidance specific to your situation, as rules may change and individual circumstances vary.

---

## Why This Matters

Health Savings Accounts are one of the most tax-advantaged tools available for healthcare costs — contributions are pre-tax, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified expenses are tax-free. For patients pairing DPC with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), understanding what can and can't be paid from an HSA is important for maximizing value.

---

## The Current IRS Position

Under IRS rules, HSA funds can be used for "qualified medical expenses" as defined in IRS Publication 502. These include doctor visits, labs, prescriptions, and many other medical costs.

The complication with DPC membership fees is this: the IRS has historically treated *prepaid medical service contracts* (like gym memberships, some health club fees, and fixed-fee primary care arrangements) as non-qualified because they're structured as access fees rather than payment for specific medical services.

**The practical implication:**
- DPC *membership fees* — likely not HSA-eligible under current rules
- Medical services received through a DPC membership that are billed separately (labs, medications, procedures) — generally HSA-eligible
- Over-the-counter medications, dental, vision costs — separate HSA categories

---

## The Legislative Picture

The AAFP, DPC Alliance, and various patient advocacy groups have pushed for legislation explicitly classifying DPC membership fees as HSA-qualified expenses. The Primary Care Enhancement Act and similar proposals have been introduced in Congress.

As of early 2026, no law has definitively resolved this at the federal level, though some states have taken positions. The landscape is evolving — which is another reason to consult a tax professional who follows healthcare tax law.

## What You *Can* Use Your HSA For at a DPC Practice

Even if the membership fee isn't HSA-eligible, a significant portion of DPC-related costs typically are:

| Expense | HSA-Eligible? |
|---|---|
| DPC monthly membership fee | Generally no (check with advisor) |
| Lab work ordered by DPC physician | Yes |
| Prescription medications | Yes |
| In-office procedures (cortisone injection, suturing) | Yes |
| Immunizations | Yes |
| Medical equipment (blood pressure cuff, glucometer) | Yes |
| Imaging (X-ray, MRI — if ordered) | Yes |
| Specialist visits | Yes |

If your DPC practice charges separately for labs, medications, and procedures (as most do), those charges are HSA-eligible and can be paid from your account.

## The DPC + HDHP + HSA Combination

Many DPC members use this three-part strategy:

1. **DPC membership** — covers all primary care (paid from checking account or credit card)
2. **High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)** — covers hospital, ER, specialist, and catastrophic costs (lower premiums than PPO)
3. **HSA contributions** — built up to cover HDHP deductible costs and eligible medical expenses (labs, meds, imaging when needed)

The combination often costs less per year than a traditional PPO while building HSA savings that roll over year-to-year. The HSA can also be invested for long-term tax-free healthcare savings.

## Practical Tips

- **Keep itemized receipts from your DPC practice** for any separately billed services — these are your HSA documentation
- **Track your HDHP deductible** — if you hit it for specialist or hospital care, all remaining qualified expenses that year are covered by your plan at higher rates
- **Max out your HSA contributions** if you're on an HDHP — unused funds roll over, grow tax-free, and can be used in retirement for any expense tax-free (after age 65)
- **Ask your benefits administrator** about your specific HSA plan's rules — some have additional restrictions or allowances beyond IRS minimums
- **Monitor legislative changes** — HSA eligibility for DPC fees is an active area of policy discussion

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use an FSA (Flexible Spending Account) for DPC membership fees?**
FSAs have similar rules to HSAs regarding qualified medical expenses. The same uncertainty about DPC membership fees applies. Separately billed services (labs, prescriptions) are generally FSA-eligible.

**Does it matter if my DPC practice provides a receipt that itemizes medical services?**
Potentially. If your DPC practice can document that the membership fee directly corresponds to specific medical services rather than access/availability, that may affect the analysis. This is worth discussing with a tax advisor.

**What if my employer's HRA (Health Reimbursement Account) can cover DPC fees?**
HRAs are employer-defined, giving more flexibility than individual HSAs. Some employer HRAs explicitly allow DPC membership fees. Check with your HR department.

**Is this likely to change?**
Possibly. There is bipartisan legislative support for clarifying HSA eligibility for DPC. Monitor developments through the DPC Alliance or consult your tax advisor annually.
