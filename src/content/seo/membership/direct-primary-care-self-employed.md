# Direct Primary Care for the Self-Employed

## Quick Answer

**Direct Primary Care is one of the best healthcare solutions for self-employed individuals.** A flat monthly membership fee ($70–$150/month) covers all primary care with no copays, no insurance billing, and no HR department required. Paired with a high-deductible health plan (HDHP) and an HSA, DPC typically costs far less than a standalone individual PPO — while delivering dramatically better primary care access.

---

## Why This Matters

Self-employed individuals face a healthcare dilemma that W-2 employees rarely encounter: you bear the full cost of health coverage, you make all the decisions alone, and you don't have an employer's group plan to buffer costs. For freelancers, consultants, solo business owners, and independent contractors, healthcare can be the single largest variable expense in a working year.

DPC changes the primary care portion of that equation entirely.

---

## The Self-Employed Healthcare Problem

Most self-employed individuals have three options:
1. **Individual ACA marketplace plan** — often expensive ($400–$800+/month for a solo adult), with high deductibles and copays
2. **COBRA from a previous employer** — temporary, expensive, and not a long-term solution
3. **Health-sharing ministries** — lower cost, but not insurance and with significant coverage limitations

All three leave primary care either expensive (high copays, deductibles) or uncertain (health-sharing).

DPC solves the primary care layer cleanly and affordably — and can be layered onto any of the above options.

---

## How DPC Works for Self-Employed Patients

**You pay a flat monthly membership fee** — no insurance involved for primary care. In return, you receive:

- Unlimited primary care visits (in-person, phone, video)
- Direct physician access by phone, text, or message
- Same-day or next-day appointments
- Annual physicals and preventive care
- Chronic disease management
- Prescription management
- Labs at wholesale pricing
- Lifestyle medicine counseling

**You keep a lean insurance plan** for hospital, ER, and specialist coverage. The right pairing is typically a high-deductible health plan (HDHP), which has significantly lower premiums than a full PPO.

**You contribute to an HSA** (available only with an HDHP) to build tax-advantaged savings for deductible and eligible medical costs.

## The Cost Comparison for a Self-Employed Adult

**Option A: Traditional individual PPO**
- Monthly premium: $450–$700/month
- Deductible: $1,500–$3,000
- Copay: $35–$60 per visit
- Annual primary care costs (est.): $600–$1,500
- **Total annual estimate: $6,000–$10,000+**

**Option B: DPC + HDHP + HSA**
- DPC membership: $100–$130/month ($1,200–$1,560/year)
- HDHP premium (individual): $200–$400/month ($2,400–$4,800/year)
- Labs at wholesale (full year): $60–$150/year
- HSA contribution (reduces taxable income): up to $4,300/year (2025 limit)
- **Total annual estimate: $3,700–$6,500** (before HSA tax savings)

*Estimates are illustrative. Individual results vary based on age, health, location, and plan selection.*

## Why Self-Employed Patients Especially Benefit

### Predictable Costs
Cash flow matters when you're self-employed. DPC membership is a fixed monthly cost — no surprises, no deductibles to meet before primary care is affordable. You can budget for healthcare the way you budget for software subscriptions.

### No HR Required
There's no open enrollment period, no plan comparison spreadsheet, no HR department to call. You enroll in a DPC practice and you're done with primary care administration.

### You Use It More — Because It's Easy
Self-employed individuals often defer routine care because scheduling around a busy client workload is difficult. DPC same-day access and direct physician contact make routine care compatible with an irregular schedule. A quick call or message handles many concerns without blocking a morning.

### The Relationship Matters More Without a Backup
When you're employed, your employer's HR system is a backup resource for navigating healthcare. When you're self-employed, you're on your own. Having a physician you can call directly — who knows your history and can advocate for you — is more valuable without that institutional backup.

### Tax Strategy Alignment
Self-employed individuals can deduct health insurance premiums on Schedule C. When you pair a deductible HDHP with DPC, you maximize the premium deduction while minimizing premium cost. Your DPC membership may be deductible as a medical expense if you itemize (consult a tax advisor). [More on HSA and DPC →](/faq)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I deduct DPC membership fees as a business expense?**
This is a nuanced tax question. If you're self-employed and pay for your own health insurance (and it's not reimbursed by an employer), some healthcare costs may be deductible. DPC membership fees occupy a grey area — consult a CPA or tax advisor familiar with Schedule C healthcare deductions.

**What if I have a very healthy year and barely use the practice?**
The membership covers *availability* — the fact that your physician is there when you need them, not just when you've used them enough. Most self-employed members find they use the practice more than expected once the access barrier is removed.

**Can my spouse and children be added to a self-employed DPC membership?**
Yes — family plans are available. This is often a major cost advantage: covering a family under a DPC membership typically costs less than a family COBRA plan or ACA family premium.
