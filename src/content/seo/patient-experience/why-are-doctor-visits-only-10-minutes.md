# Why Are Doctor Visits Only 10 Minutes?

## Quick Answer

**Doctor visits are so short because traditional medical practices are structured around insurance billing — and insurance billing rewards volume over time.** A typical primary care physician must see 20–30 patients per day to cover overhead costs (staff, rent, malpractice, billing software), which compresses each appointment to 7–12 minutes. This isn't a failure of individual physicians — it's the predictable output of a payment model that makes time a liability.

---

## Why This Matters

Most patients have experienced a visit that felt too short. You arrived with three concerns, addressed one, and left with two unresolved questions. Or you waited 25 minutes to be seen and then felt rushed out in 12. Or your doctor was looking at a computer screen while you described a symptom.

These experiences are not accidental. They're the result of a system with misaligned financial incentives — and understanding why visit times are so short is the first step to knowing what to look for instead.

---

## The Economics of Short Visits

Here's how the math works in a traditional primary care practice:

**A typical independent primary care practice has significant overhead:**
- Office rent and utilities
- Front desk and medical assistant staff
- Billing department staff (often 2–3 people just to handle insurance claims)
- Electronic health record (EHR) software
- Malpractice insurance
- Lab equipment, supplies

Total overhead for a small primary care practice often runs $400,000–$600,000 per year. The physician must generate enough revenue to cover all of this *before* taking home a salary.

**Insurance reimbursement is low — and based on volume:**
Medicare and most insurance plans pay primary care physicians $50–$100 per visit for standard office appointments. A "complex" visit pays more, but most visits are coded at lower levels.

At $75 average reimbursement per visit, a physician needs to see **5,000–7,000 patients per year** just to cover overhead. That's 20–28 patients every working day, 50 weeks a year. Simple arithmetic makes 10-minute visits the only viable option — not a preference, but a structural requirement.

**Documentation makes it worse:**
Most physicians spend an average of 2 hours per day on documentation after seeing patients. That's time the patient never sees but the doctor never gets back. A doctor who sees 25 patients and documents for 2 hours has an 11-hour workday — before returning phone calls.

---

## What This Means for Patients

Short visits create real clinical consequences:

- **Incomplete care.** Preventive discussions, lifestyle counseling, and complex history-taking get cut short or skipped entirely.
- **Reactive, not proactive.** When time is scarce, practices default to treating today's chief complaint rather than looking ahead.
- **Fragmentation.** Chronic conditions that need ongoing management get abbreviated attention every quarter, leading to inconsistent care.
- **Patient disengagement.** When patients feel rushed, they stop bringing up concerns — and problems go unaddressed until they become urgent.

A 2021 study in *Annals of Family Medicine* found that if primary care physicians did everything recommended by clinical guidelines for their average patient panel, they would need 21.7 hours per day — for primary care alone. Something always gets cut. That something is usually *you.*

## Practical Tips for Patients Stuck in Short-Visit Medicine

If DPC isn't available to you yet, here are ways to maximize short appointments:

- **Prioritize before you arrive.** Decide your top 1–2 concerns and lead with them. Don't save the most important thing for last.
- **Write it down.** Bring a list. Doctors respond well to organized patients.
- **Schedule for length.** Many practices offer 30- or 45-minute "comprehensive" appointments — ask specifically. You may pay more, but you'll get more.
- **Request a dedicated preventive visit.** A separate annual physical is specifically designed to cover the ground a sick visit can't.
- **Follow up by message.** Many practices now have patient portals. Non-urgent follow-up questions don't need an appointment.

But if you're consistently leaving appointments with unresolved concerns or feeling like a number — that's not a patient problem. It's a practice model problem. And there are alternatives.
