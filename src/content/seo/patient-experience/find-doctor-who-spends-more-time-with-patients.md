# How to Find a Doctor Who Spends More Time With You

## Quick Answer

**To find a doctor who spends more time with patients, look for Direct Primary Care (DPC) or concierge medicine practices.** These models intentionally limit panel sizes to 400–600 patients — compared to 2,000+ in traditional practices — which is the structural reason some doctors have time and others don't. It's not personality; it's panel size.

---

## Why This Matters

Most patients know the feeling: you waited three weeks for a 15-minute appointment, left with two unanswered questions, and aren't sure you'd get a callback if you called tomorrow. You didn't choose the wrong doctor — you're in the wrong model.

Finding a physician who genuinely has time for you requires understanding why most don't — and knowing what to look for in a practice that runs differently.

---

## Why Most Doctors Don't Have More Time

Traditional primary care practices depend on insurance reimbursement, which is volume-based. At $70–$90 per visit from a typical insurer, a physician needs to see 20–30 patients per day to keep the practice financially viable. That leaves 10–15 minutes per patient on average — and often less, once documentation time is subtracted.

This is a system problem, not a doctor quality problem. Most primary care physicians would prefer to spend more time with patients. The structure of insurance billing makes it financially impossible in a traditional practice.

**The solution is a different practice structure** — one where the revenue model doesn't punish physicians for spending 45 minutes with a complex patient.

---

## What to Look For: Practice Models That Prioritize Time

### Direct Primary Care (DPC)
DPC practices charge a flat monthly membership fee and do not bill insurance for primary care visits. Because revenue is predictable and per-visit billing is eliminated, the physician can see fewer patients per day — typically 8–12, compared to 20–30.

**Signs a practice is truly DPC:**
- Published monthly membership fee (no insurance billing for primary care)
- Clear statement about patient panel size (under 600–700 is meaningful)
- Same-day or next-day scheduling described as standard
- Direct physician contact (not a call center or nurse-only portal)

### Concierge Medicine
Concierge practices charge a membership or retainer fee — sometimes in addition to insurance — for enhanced access and smaller panels. Quality varies widely by practice. Look for:
- Transparent retainer fee
- Panel size under 600
- A physician (not a mid-level provider) as your primary contact

### What to Avoid
- "Concierge" in the name doesn't always mean DPC or small panel. Ask directly: "How many patients are in your panel?"
- Large health system primary care practices are typically volume-based regardless of the physician's intentions.
- "Premium" practice marketing language with no concrete panel size information.

## Questions to Ask When Evaluating a Practice

Before switching to any new practice, ask these directly:

1. **"How many patients are in your panel?"** — Under 600 is strong; 1,000+ defeats the purpose
2. **"What's your average appointment wait time?"** — Same-day or next-day should be standard, not a boast
3. **"Can I contact the physician directly between visits?"** — Phone, text, or message — not just a portal that routes to a nurse
4. **"How long are your standard appointments?"** — 30 minutes minimum for established patients; 45–60 for complex concerns
5. **"Do you bill insurance for visits?"** — DPC practices do not; this determines the revenue model and thus the pace

## Practical Tips for Finding More Personalized Care

- **Search "direct primary care [your city]"** — The DPC Frontier and AAFP both maintain searchable directories of DPC practices
- **Look for small, independent practices** — Solo or small-group practices have more flexibility than large health systems
- **Read reviews carefully** — Patient reviews that mention wait time, visit length, and physician availability reveal the practice culture
- **Schedule a Meet & Greet before committing** — Most DPC and concierge practices offer a free consultation. Use it to ask the questions above in person
- **Trust your first visit** — A physician who reviews your history before you walk in, doesn't rush, and follows up proactively is running a different kind of practice
